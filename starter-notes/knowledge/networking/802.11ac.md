# 802.11ac

**802.11ac** (also branded as **Wi-Fi 5**) is a wireless networking standard ratified by the IEEE in 2013 that operates exclusively on the **5 GHz frequency band**, delivering significantly higher throughput than its predecessors through technologies like **Multi-User MIMO (MU-MIMO)** and **256-QAM modulation**. It builds directly upon [[802.11n]] and introduced **beamforming**, wider channel widths (up to 160 MHz), and theoretical speeds exceeding 6.9 Gbps in Wave 2 implementations. From a security perspective, understanding 802.11ac is essential because it expanded the attack surface of enterprise wireless networks while also enabling more sophisticated [[WPA2]] and [[WPA3]] deployments.

---

## Overview

802.11ac emerged from a clear need: the explosion of mobile devices, HD video streaming, and cloud-dependent enterprise workflows was saturating 2.4 GHz networks running 802.11n. By confining itself exclusively to the 5 GHz band, 802.11ac avoided the notoriously congested 2.4 GHz spectrum shared with Bluetooth, microwaves, baby monitors, and legacy Wi-Fi devices. The 5 GHz band offered more non-overlapping channels and far less interference, though at the cost of reduced range compared to 2.4 GHz (higher frequencies attenuate faster through walls and obstacles).

The standard was released in two major waves. **Wave 1** (2013) supported single-user MIMO (SU-MIMO) with up to three spatial streams, 80 MHz channel widths, and 256-QAM — yielding per-stream rates of 433 Mbps and theoretical maximums around 1.3 Gbps for three-stream devices. **Wave 2** (2016) introduced **MU-MIMO**, allowing an access point to simultaneously transmit to up to four clients rather than serving them sequentially. Wave 2 also added 160 MHz channel support and up to eight spatial streams, pushing theoretical aggregate throughput to 6.93 Gbps. In practice, real-world throughput is typically 30–60% of theoretical maximums due to protocol overhead, retransmissions, interference, and client capability limitations.

From an enterprise and homelab standpoint, 802.11ac dominated corporate deployments from roughly 2015 through 2020, when [[802.11ax]] (Wi-Fi 6) began displacing it in new installations. Vendors like Cisco (Aironet/Catalyst series), Aruba, Ubiquiti (UniFi AC series), and Netgear (Nighthawk) released extensive 802.11ac product lines. Security practitioners frequently encounter 802.11ac hardware in live environments and penetration testing engagements, making it a standard that remains highly relevant regardless of newer generations now entering the market.

The security model of 802.11ac itself is identical to 802.11n in the sense that the standard defines PHY and MAC layer behavior but defers to WPA2 (802.11i) and WPA3 for authentication and encryption. This means all the vulnerabilities of [[WPA2-Personal]] (PMKID attacks, 4-way handshake capture) and [[WPA2-Enterprise]] (RADIUS misconfiguration, rogue AP attacks) apply equally to 802.11ac networks. The higher throughput of 802.11ac actually increases the severity of certain attacks — for instance, a rogue AP operating at 802.11ac speeds is far more convincing and useful to an attacker than a slower legacy impersonation.

## How It Works

### Physical Layer (PHY) Fundamentals

802.11ac operates on OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing), the same modulation scheme used by 802.11a/n, but with significant enhancements. The 5 GHz band spans roughly 5.150–5.850 GHz (varies by regulatory domain), divided into 20 MHz sub-channels that can be bonded into 40, 80, or 160 MHz wide channels.

**Channel widths and their implications:**

| Channel Width | Available Non-Overlapping Channels (US) | Typical Throughput (1 stream) |
|---|---|---|
| 20 MHz | ~25 | ~87.6 Mbps |
| 40 MHz | ~12 | ~200 Mbps |
| 80 MHz | ~6 | ~433 Mbps |
| 160 MHz | ~2 | ~867 Mbps |

Wider channels deliver higher throughput but reduce the number of non-overlapping channels available, creating contention in dense deployments — a critical consideration for security-aware network architects.

### MIMO and Spatial Streams

802.11ac uses **MIMO (Multiple Input, Multiple Output)** with up to 8 spatial streams. Each spatial stream is an independent data path between antenna arrays. The notation **NxM:SS** describes antenna configuration — for example, `4x4:3` means 4 transmit antennas, 4 receive antennas, 3 spatial streams.

**MU-MIMO (Multi-User MIMO)**, introduced in Wave 2, allows an AP to transmit to multiple clients simultaneously on different spatial streams. An 8x8:8 AP could theoretically serve four 2-stream clients at once. MU-MIMO is downlink-only in 802.11ac (both uplink and downlink MU-MIMO arrived with Wi-Fi 6).

### Beamforming

802.11ac standardized **explicit beamforming** (also called Transmit Beamforming, TxBF). The transmitter sends a sounding frame; the receiver measures channel conditions and sends back channel state information (CSI). The AP uses this CSI to shape the RF beam toward the target client, improving signal quality and reducing interference to neighboring devices.

```
AP                          Client
 |--NDP Announcement (NDPA)-->|
 |--Null Data Packet (NDP)--->|
 |<--Compressed Beamforming---|
 |   Report (CBR)             |
 |--Beamformed Data---------->|
```

### 802.11ac Frame Exchange (Association Process)

The association process itself is unchanged from previous 802.11 generations:

1. **Probe Request/Response** — Client discovers APs broadcasting compatible SSIDs
2. **Authentication** — Open System authentication (actual security is in 4-way handshake)
3. **Association Request/Response** — Client negotiates capabilities (HT/VHT IEs)
4. **4-Way Handshake** — WPA2/WPA3 key exchange (EAPOL frames)
5. **Group Key Handshake** — Broadcast/multicast key distribution

**VHT (Very High Throughput) Information Element** is the 802.11ac-specific capability advertisement seen in beacons:

```
VHT Capabilities IE (Tag 191):
  Max MPDU Length: 11454 bytes
  Supported Channel Width: 80 MHz / 160 MHz
  RX LDPC: Yes
  Short GI 80 MHz: Yes
  Short GI 160/80+80 MHz: Yes
  TX STBC: Yes
  RX STBC: 1 stream
  MU Beamformer Capable: Yes
```

### Modulation and Coding Schemes (MCS)

802.11ac uses MCS indices 0–9 (versus 0–7 in 802.11n), adding support for **256-QAM** (MCS 8 and 9):

| MCS Index | Modulation | Code Rate | 1-Stream 80 MHz Throughput |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | BPSK | 1/2 | 29.3 Mbps |
| 4 | 16-QAM | 3/4 | 175.5 Mbps |
| 7 | 64-QAM | 5/6 | 292.5 Mbps |
| 8 | 256-QAM | 3/4 | 351 Mbps |
| 9 | 256-QAM | 5/6 | 390 Mbps |

256-QAM encodes 8 bits per symbol versus 64-QAM's 6 bits, but requires a higher SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio) — approximately 30+ dB — to decode reliably.

### Checking 802.11ac Capabilities on Linux

```bash
# Check interface wireless capabilities
iw list | grep -A 20 "VHT Capabilities"

# Show current connection details including frequency and bitrate
iw dev wlan0 link

# Scan for 802.11ac APs (look for VHT in output)
sudo iw dev wlan0 scan | grep -E "SSID|freq|VHT"

# Show supported channels on 5 GHz band
iw phy phy0 channels | grep -A 3 "5[0-9][0-9][0-9]"
```

## Key Concepts

- **VHT (Very High Throughput):** The PHY/MAC amendment designation for 802.11ac features; VHT Information Elements in beacon frames indicate 802.11ac capability. Contrast with HT (High Throughput) for 802.11n.

- **MU-MIMO (Multi-User MIMO):** A Wave 2 feature enabling an AP to transmit to multiple clients simultaneously via separate spatial streams, dramatically improving efficiency in dense environments. In 802.11ac, MU-MIMO is downlink-only (AP → clients).

- **Channel Bonding:** The aggregation of adjacent 20 MHz sub-channels into wider channels (40/80/160 MHz) to increase data rates. Wider channels are more susceptible to interference and reduce the number of deployable APs in enterprise environments.

- **256-QAM:** A dense modulation scheme encoding 8 bits per RF symbol (256 constellation points), requiring high SNR conditions. Represents a 33% data rate improvement over 64-QAM but degrades rapidly with distance or interference.

- **Beamforming (Explicit TxBF):** A standardized mechanism in 802.11ac where the AP steers transmit energy directionally toward specific clients based on channel sounding feedback, improving per-client SNR without increasing transmit power.

- **Short Guard Interval (SGI):** Reduces the guard interval between OFDM symbols from 800 ns to 400 ns, increasing throughput by approximately 11%, but only effective in low-multipath environments. Configurable and impacts both performance and interference resilience.

- **A-MPDU (Aggregated MAC Protocol Data Unit):** Frame aggregation mechanism that bundles multiple frames into a single transmission, dramatically reducing per-frame overhead. 802.11ac supports A-MPDUs up to 1,048,575 bytes (vs 65,535 bytes in 802.11n).

- **STBC (Space-Time Block Coding):** A diversity technique that transmits copies of a data stream across multiple antennas using space-time codes, improving reliability in poor channel conditions without requiring CSI feedback from the receiver.

## Security Implications

### Inherited WPA2 Vulnerabilities

Because 802.11ac uses [[WPA2]] (and optionally [[WPA3]]) for security, all WPA2 attack classes apply:

- **PMKID Attack (2018):** Discovered by Jens Steube (Hashcat author), this attack extracts the PMKID from a single EAPOL frame sent by the AP during association — no need to capture the 4-way handshake. The PMKID is derived from: `PMKID = HMAC-SHA1-128(PMK, "PMK Name" || AP_MAC || Client_MAC)`. The high throughput of 802.11ac means attackers can quickly reconnect to capture PMKIDs.

- **4-Way Handshake Capture:** Using tools like `hcxdumptool` and `aircrack-ng`, attackers capture the WPA2 handshake and perform offline dictionary/brute-force attacks. 802.11ac does not change this fundamental vulnerability.

- **KRACK (Key Reinstallation Attack, CVE-2017-13077 through CVE-2017-13088):** Affects the 4-way handshake and group key handshake in WPA2. By replaying handshake messages, an attacker forces nonce reuse, enabling decryption of unicast traffic and potentially injection. All 802.11ac devices running WPA2 were vulnerable unless patched.

### Rogue Access Points and Evil Twin Attacks

802.11ac's higher capability makes rogue APs more dangerous. Tools like **hostapd-wpe** (WPA Enterprise evil twin) and **Airbase-ng** can create 802.11ac-capable rogue APs. A rogue AP impersonating a corporate SSID at 802.11ac speeds is indistinguishable to clients.

```
Attack Chain (Evil Twin):
1. Scan target network → identify SSID, BSSID, channel
2. Deauth target clients (802.11 deauth flood, unprotected in WPA2)
3. Spin up evil twin AP on same SSID/channel
4. Capture WPA2 handshake or harvest EAP credentials
```

**802.11w (Management Frame Protection)** mitigates deauth flood attacks but is not mandated until WPA3.

### Denial-of-Service via Channel Saturation

The 80/160 MHz channels used by 802.11ac, while delivering high throughput, also create a larger spectrum footprint that can be disrupted. An attacker with an 802.11ac-capable radio can flood a wide channel, impacting all clients on that frequency.

### Captive Portal Bypass

Many 802.11ac guest networks rely on [[captive portals]] for access control. These are trivially bypassed by MAC address cloning from an authenticated device — 802.11ac introduces no mechanism to prevent this.

### WPA3-SAE Transition Mode Downgrade

When an AP broadcasts both WPA2-PSK and WPA3-SAE (transition mode), clients can be forced into WPA2 by an adversary — a downgrade attack that exposes the network to classic WPA2 attacks. This is relevant to all modern 802.11ac APs offering WPA3 support.

## Defensive Measures

### Firmware and Patch Management

Ensure all 802.11ac APs are running current firmware. KRACK and related vulnerabilities were patched in 2017–2018 by most major vendors. Cisco, Aruba, and Ubiquiti all released patches; verify patch status:

```bash
# Ubiquiti UniFi: check firmware version via SSH
mca-ctrl -t dump-cfg | grep version

# Check against vendor security advisories
# Cisco: https://tools.cisco.com/security/center/
# Aruba: https://www.arubanetworks.com/support-services/security-bulletins/
```

### Enable 802.11w (Management Frame Protection)

Management Frame Protection prevents deauthentication flood attacks. Configure as **Required** (not Optional) for all corporate SSIDs:

```
# Cisco WLC (legacy CLI)
config wlan security wpa akm pmf required <wlan-id>

# Hostapd configuration
ieee80211w=2          # 2 = Required, 1 = Optional
```

### Wireless Intrusion Prevention System (WIPS)

Deploy a [[WIPS]] to detect:
- Rogue APs (BSSIDs not in authorized list)
- Deauth floods (sudden increase in deauth frames)
- Ad-hoc networks
- Evil twin SSIDs (duplicate SSIDs with different BSSIDs)

Tools: **Cisco DNA Center WIPS**, **Aruba RFProtect**, **Kismet** (open source), **Zeek** with wireless plugins.
