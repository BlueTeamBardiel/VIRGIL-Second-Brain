# 802.11 Wireless

**IEEE 802.11** is a family of [[Wireless Networking]] standards that define how devices communicate over **radio frequency (RF)** channels in local area networks, commonly known as **Wi-Fi**. The standard is maintained by the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) and operates primarily in the **2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz** frequency bands. Understanding 802.11 is foundational to both [[Network Security]] and the [[Security+ SY0-701]] exam, as wireless networks represent one of the most commonly exploited attack surfaces in modern environments.

---

## Overview

The 802.11 standard was first published in 1997, providing 1–2 Mbps wireless connectivity, but it has evolved through numerous amendments that dramatically increased speed, range, and security. Each amendment is denoted by a letter suffix (e.g., 802.11a, 802.11b, 802.11g, 802.11n, 802.11ac, 802.11ax) with each generation improving modulation schemes, antenna technology, and channel utilization. The current consumer-facing generation is **Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax)**, with **Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be)** emerging in enterprise environments.

Wireless communication using 802.11 occurs between two primary types of devices: **stations (STAs)**, which are client devices such as laptops and phones, and **access points (APs)**, which bridge wireless clients to a wired infrastructure. A group of devices sharing an AP forms a **Basic Service Set (BSS)**, identified by a **BSSID** (the AP's MAC address). Multiple APs sharing the same **SSID** (network name) form an **Extended Service Set (ESS)**, enabling roaming across a campus or building.

The physical layer (PHY) of 802.11 uses **spread-spectrum** or **OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing)** modulation depending on the amendment. Newer standards (802.11ax and beyond) use **OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access)**, which allows an AP to communicate with multiple clients simultaneously on the same channel by dividing it into smaller sub-carriers called **Resource Units (RUs)**. This dramatically improves efficiency in dense environments such as offices and stadiums.

The medium access method used by 802.11 is **CSMA/CA (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance)**, rather than the CSMA/CD used in wired Ethernet. Because wireless devices cannot detect collisions while transmitting, they use a "listen before transmitting" approach combined with random backoff timers and optional **RTS/CTS (Request to Send / Clear to Send)** handshakes to reduce the chance of simultaneous transmissions. This is a fundamental architectural distinction from wired networking.

Security has been one of the most historically troubled areas of 802.11. The original security mechanism, **WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy)**, was cryptographically broken by the early 2000s. Its replacement, **WPA (Wi-Fi Protected Access)**, and then **WPA2**, became the dominant security frameworks. **WPA3**, introduced in 2018, addresses many WPA2 shortcomings using **Simultaneous Authentication of Equals (SAE)** and forward secrecy. The evolution of 802.11 security is a core topic on the [[Security+ SY0-701]] exam.

## How It Works

### Frequency Bands and Channels

802.11 operates across several frequency bands, each with specific channel layouts:

| Band | Channels (US) | Non-Overlapping | Common Standards |
|------|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 2.4 GHz | 1–11 | 3 (1, 6, 11) | 802.11b/g/n |
| 5 GHz | 36–165 (select) | ~24 | 802.11a/n/ac/ax |
| 6 GHz | 1–233 | Many | 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6E) |

The **2.4 GHz** band offers better range and wall penetration but is heavily congested and only has 3 non-overlapping channels, making it prone to interference from microwaves, Bluetooth, and neighboring networks. The **5 GHz** band offers higher throughput and far more non-overlapping channels but shorter range. The **6 GHz** band (Wi-Fi 6E) is available only to newer devices and provides a clean, uncongested spectrum.

### The Association Process

When a client connects to a Wi-Fi network, it undergoes the following sequence:

1. **Scanning** — The client either listens passively for **beacon frames** broadcast by APs (every 100ms by default) or sends **probe request** frames actively to discover networks.
2. **Authentication** — In open networks, the client sends an **Authentication Request** and receives an **Authentication Response** (this is now largely a formality; actual authentication happens in later stages under WPA2/WPA3).
3. **Association** — The client sends an **Association Request** containing its capabilities (supported rates, HT/VHT/HE capabilities). The AP responds with an **Association Response** assigning an **Association ID (AID)**.
4. **4-Way Handshake (WPA2/WPA3)** — The client and AP derive a **Pairwise Transient Key (PTK)** from the **Pairwise Master Key (PMK)**, which is derived from the passphrase (PSK) or from 802.1X authentication. This handshake occurs in 4 EAPOL frames:
   - AP → Client: ANonce (AP's random nonce)
   - Client → AP: SNonce + MIC (client's nonce + message integrity code)
   - AP → Client: GTK (Group Temporal Key) + MIC
   - Client → AP: ACK

```
# Capture 4-way handshake with aircrack-ng suite
sudo airmon-ng start wlan0          # Enable monitor mode
sudo airodump-ng wlan0mon           # Discover networks
sudo airodump-ng -c 6 --bssid AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF -w capture wlan0mon  # Target channel 6
sudo aireplay-ng --deauth 5 -a AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF wlan0mon   # Force re-auth
sudo aircrack-ng -w /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt capture*.cap     # Crack PMK
```

### Frame Types

802.11 defines three categories of frames:

- **Management Frames** — Handle connection setup/teardown (beacons, probe requests/responses, authentication, association, deauthentication, disassociation). These are **not encrypted** in WPA2 (protected in WPA3 via Management Frame Protection).
- **Control Frames** — Assist data delivery (RTS, CTS, ACK, Block ACK).
- **Data Frames** — Carry the actual payload, encrypted under WPA2/WPA3.

### Modulation and Data Rates

| Standard | Max Throughput | Modulation | Frequency |
|----------|---------------|------------|-----------|
| 802.11b | 11 Mbps | DSSS | 2.4 GHz |
| 802.11g | 54 Mbps | OFDM | 2.4 GHz |
| 802.11n (Wi-Fi 4) | 600 Mbps | MIMO-OFDM | 2.4/5 GHz |
| 802.11ac (Wi-Fi 5) | 3.5 Gbps | MU-MIMO, 256-QAM | 5 GHz |
| 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6/6E) | 9.6 Gbps | OFDMA, 1024-QAM | 2.4/5/6 GHz |

Higher-order **QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation)** packs more bits per symbol — 256-QAM transmits 8 bits per symbol vs. 64-QAM's 6 — but requires a cleaner signal (higher SNR).

### Checking Wireless Interfaces in Linux

```bash
# List wireless interfaces
iw dev

# Show network scan results
sudo iw dev wlan0 scan | grep -E 'SSID|signal|freq'

# Check interface capabilities
iw phy phy0 info | grep -A5 'Supported interface modes'

# Enable monitor mode manually
sudo ip link set wlan0 down
sudo iw wlan0 set monitor control
sudo ip link set wlan0 up

# Check current association
iw dev wlan0 link
```

## Key Concepts

- **SSID (Service Set Identifier)** — The human-readable name of a wireless network (up to 32 bytes). SSIDs can be hidden (not broadcast in beacon frames), though this provides minimal security as SSIDs are still visible in probe request/response frames captured in monitor mode.

- **BSSID (Basic Service Set Identifier)** — The MAC address of the access point radio, used to uniquely identify a BSS. When a client connects to an AP, it associates with a specific BSSID, which is important when distinguishing between APs in an ESS.

- **WPA2-Personal vs. WPA2-Enterprise** — **WPA2-Personal (PSK)** uses a pre-shared key where everyone on the network uses the same passphrase. **WPA2-Enterprise** integrates with [[RADIUS]] and [[802.1X]] to authenticate each user individually with credentials or certificates, providing per-user keys and centralized access control. Enterprise mode is strongly preferred in corporate environments.

- **PMK, PTK, GTK** — The **Pairwise Master Key (PMK)** is derived from the passphrase and SSID in PSK mode, or from the RADIUS session in Enterprise mode. The **Pairwise Transient Key (PTK)** is derived per-session from the PMK and used for unicast frame encryption. The **Group Temporal Key (GTK)** encrypts broadcast and multicast frames shared across all clients.

- **RSSI (Received Signal Strength Indicator)** — A measure of wireless signal power at the receiver, typically expressed in dBm (negative values; closer to 0 is stronger). Useful for diagnosing coverage issues and in wireless survey tools. Values above -70 dBm are generally considered usable; below -80 dBm indicates poor connectivity.

- **Channel Bonding** — A technique where two adjacent channels are combined to double effective bandwidth. 802.11n introduced 40 MHz channels (2×20 MHz), 802.11ac supports up to 160 MHz channels, and 802.11ax similarly supports wide channels. Wider channels increase throughput but at the cost of increased interference potential.

- **MFP/PMF (Management Frame Protection)** — **802.11w** introduced the ability to cryptographically protect management frames, preventing spoofed deauthentication and disassociation attacks. WPA3 mandates PMF; WPA2 implementations should enable it as a mandatory option.

## Security Implications

### WEP — Completely Broken

**WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy)** was the original 802.11 security mechanism, using RC4 with a 40- or 104-bit static key and a 24-bit Initialization Vector (IV). Because IVs are reused (only 16 million possibilities) and RC4 was poorly applied, researchers demonstrated in 2001 (Fluhrer, Mantin, and Shamir attack) that WEP could be cracked with as few as **40,000 captured packets**. Tools like `aircrack-ng` can crack WEP keys in minutes. WEP must never be used.

### WPA/TKIP — Deprecated

**WPA with TKIP (Temporal Key Integrity Protocol)** was a stopgap replacement for WEP. While more robust, TKIP contains several vulnerabilities including the **TKIP-Michael attack**, and was formally deprecated by the IEEE in 2012. It must not be used in modern deployments.

### WPA2 — Offline Dictionary Attacks

The **4-way handshake** in WPA2-PSK transmits enough information for an attacker to mount an offline dictionary attack. By capturing the handshake (or using the **PMKID attack** discovered in 2018 which eliminates the need for client capture), an attacker can test millions of passphrases offline:

```bash
# PMKID attack using hcxdumptool + hashcat (no client deauth required)
sudo hcxdumptool -i wlan0mon --enable_status=1 -o capture.pcapng
hcxpcapngtool -o hash.hc22000 capture.pcapng
hashcat -m 22000 hash.hc22000 /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt
```

The **KRACK (Key Reinstallation Attack)** vulnerability (CVE-2017-13077 through CVE-2017-13088) exploited the 4-way handshake to force nonce reuse, potentially allowing decryption of encrypted traffic. Most major platforms were patched by late 2017.

### WPA3 Dragonblood

**Dragonblood** (CVE-2019-9494, CVE-2019-9496) was a series of vulnerabilities in WPA3's SAE handshake revealed in 2019 by Vanhoef and Ronen. The attacks allowed side-channel timing attacks to recover the password offline. These were addressed in the **WPA3-R2** (revision 2) specification and via firmware updates.

### Evil Twin / Rogue AP Attacks

An attacker creates an AP with the same SSID and stronger signal than the legitimate AP, causing clients to associate with the attacker's AP. Combined with a [[Captive Portal]] or [[MITM]] setup, this can harvest credentials or inject malicious content. Tools like **hostapd-wpe** automate this for WPA2-Enterprise credential harvesting (capturing NetNTLM hashes from Windows clients using EAP-PEAP without certificate validation).

### Deauthentication Attacks

Because **management frames in WPA2 are unauthenticated**, an attacker can send spoofed deauthentication frames to disconnect clients from their AP, forcing reconnection (and potentially capturing the 4-way handshake). This is trivially executed with `aireplay-ng --deauth`. WPA3 and 802.11w (PMF) prevent this by authenticating management frames.

### War Driving and Warshipping

**War driving** involves physically moving through an area while scanning for open or weakly secured Wi-Fi networks. **Warshipping** is an evolution where attackers mail devices with hidden Wi-Fi radios to a target's physical address. Both techniques are used in reconnaissance phases.

## Defensive Measures

### Use WPA3 or WPA2 with Strong Configuration

```
# Hostapd configuration for WPA2 with PMF (management frame protection)
interface=wlan0
ssid=CorporateNetwork
hw_mode=g
channel=6
wpa=2
wpa_key_mgmt=WPA-PSK
rsn_pairwise=CCMP
wpa_passphrase=Use-A-Passphrase-Over-20-Characters
ieee80211w=2          # 2 = Required (MFP mandatory)
```

For WPA3-SAE:
```
wpa=2
wpa_key_mgmt=SAE
rsn_pairwise=CCMP
sae_password=YourStrongPassphrase
ieee80211w=2
```

### Wireless Security Checklist

1. **Use WPA3-SAE** for new deployments; WPA2-AES (CCMP) minimum for legacy devices.
2. **Enable Management Frame Protection (802.11w/PMF)** — mandatory in WPA3, configurable in WPA2.
3. **Disable WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup)** — the WPS PIN attack (Reaver tool) can brute-force the WPS PIN in hours due to a design flaw in the protocol.
