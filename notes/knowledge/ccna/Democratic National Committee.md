# Democratic National Committee

## What it is
Think of it as a corporate headquarters for a major political party — full of strategic documents, donor databases, and internal communications that opponents would pay dearly to read. The Democratic National Committee (DNC) is the governing body of the U.S. Democratic Party, and in 2016 it became one of the most publicly documented victims of a state-sponsored cyber intrusion in political history.

## Why it matters
In 2016, Russian intelligence units APT28 (Fancy Bear) and APT29 (Cozy Bear) breached DNC networks simultaneously, remaining undetected for months while exfiltrating emails and opposition research. The stolen data was released via WikiLeaks and DCLeaks, demonstrating the full kill chain from initial access through exfiltration to weaponized influence operations — a textbook example of why dwell time detection matters.

## Key facts
- **APT28 and APT29** operated independently inside the DNC network, unaware of each other — illustrating that multiple threat actors can cohabit a compromised environment
- **Spearphishing** was the initial access vector; a fake Google password-reset email tricked campaign chairman John Podesta into surrendering credentials
- **CrowdStrike** was the incident response firm that attributed the breach and coined the "Fancy Bear" / "Cozy Bear" designations used in threat intelligence
- **Dwell time** exceeded several months before detection, highlighting the danger of slow lateral movement and living-off-the-land techniques
- The breach accelerated U.S. government focus on **election security** and led to CISA's expanded role protecting critical political infrastructure
