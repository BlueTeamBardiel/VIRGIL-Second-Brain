# impersonation

## What it is
Like an actor wearing a perfect costume and rehearsing a script to convince a bouncer they're someone else — impersonation is the act of pretending to be a trusted entity (a person, system, or organization) to manipulate a target into revealing information or granting access. It exploits the human tendency to trust recognized identities rather than verify them.

## Why it matters
In a classic vishing (voice phishing) attack, an adversary calls an IT help desk impersonating a senior executive, claiming to be locked out before an urgent board meeting. Under social pressure, the technician resets the account credentials and reads them aloud — bypassing every technical control in place because the attack targeted human trust, not software.

## Key facts
- Impersonation is a core technique within **social engineering** and appears explicitly in the CompTIA Security+ exam domain on threats, attacks, and social engineering.
- **Pretexting** is the fabricated backstory used to make impersonation credible — the two concepts work together but are distinct.
- Impersonation can be digital (spoofed email sender addresses, cloned websites) or physical (tailgating with a fake badge).
- **Caller ID spoofing** is a common technical enabler of voice-based impersonation, making the attacker's number appear legitimate.
- Defenses include **callback verification procedures**, strict identity verification policies for help desks, and multi-factor authentication that doesn't rely solely on knowledge factors an impersonator could obtain.
