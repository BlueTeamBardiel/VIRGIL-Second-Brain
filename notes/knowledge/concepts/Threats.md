# Threats

## What it is
Like a storm system that *could* destroy your house — it doesn't matter if your roof is weak or strong, the storm is a threat regardless. A **threat** is any potential event, actor, or circumstance that could exploit a vulnerability to cause harm to an asset. Threats exist independently of whether a vulnerability is present; they represent *intent or potential*, not guaranteed outcomes.

## Why it matters
In 2021, the Kaseya VSA supply chain attack demonstrated a sophisticated **threat actor** (REvil ransomware group) leveraging zero-day vulnerabilities to compromise over 1,500 businesses downstream from a single managed service provider. Understanding the threat — a financially motivated, technically advanced criminal group — allowed defenders to prioritize patching and network segmentation as countermeasures before attribution was even complete.

## Key facts
- Threats are categorized by **source**: adversarial (hackers, insiders, nation-states), accidental (misconfigured systems), structural (hardware failure), and environmental (floods, power loss)
- A **threat actor** has three key attributes evaluated in risk analysis: **capability**, **intent**, and **opportunity** — all three must align for a threat to materialize into an attack
- The **threat landscape** is dynamic — new threats emerge from geopolitical events, technology shifts (e.g., AI-assisted phishing), and newly discovered vulnerability classes
- Security+ distinguishes **threat vectors** (the pathway used — email, USB, web) from the threat itself (the actor or event)
- Nation-state threats are classified as **Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)** because they combine high capability, sustained intent, and patient, multi-stage attack campaigns
