# tailgating

## What it is
Like slipping through a turnstile right behind a paying passenger before it closes, tailgating is when an unauthorized person physically follows an authorized person through a secured door or checkpoint without providing their own credentials. It exploits human politeness — people instinctively hold doors open — rather than any technical vulnerability.

## Why it matters
In 2019, a Capital One data center contractor was found to have accessed a restricted server room by following an employee through a badge-controlled door. Physical access to servers bypasses virtually every digital security control you have — encryption, firewalls, and MFA become irrelevant once someone can physically touch the hardware. A single tailgating event can result in hardware implants, data theft, or ransomware deployment via USB.

## Key facts
- Tailgating is distinct from **piggybacking**: in piggybacking, the authorized person *knowingly* allows the unauthorized person entry; in tailgating, the authorized person is unaware.
- The primary countermeasure is a **mantrap** (also called an airlock or access control vestibule) — a two-door chamber where the first door must close before the second opens.
- This is classified as a **physical social engineering** attack on Security+ exams, not a network or software vulnerability.
- Security awareness training is a critical control: employees must be trained to challenge unfamiliar individuals and never hold doors for unverified personnel, even if it feels rude.
- **Security guards, CCTV monitoring, and turnstiles** are compensating controls when mantraps are impractical (e.g., in high-traffic lobbies).
