# Tabletop Exercise

## What it is
Like a military war game played on a map instead of a battlefield, a tabletop exercise is a discussion-based simulation where key personnel talk through their responses to a hypothetical incident scenario without actually executing any technical actions. It tests the *plan*, not the infrastructure — validating whether people know their roles, responsibilities, and decision trees before a real crisis hits.

## Why it matters
After the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack, many organizations realized their incident response plans existed on paper but had never been verbally walked through by executives, legal counsel, and IT together. A tabletop exercise would have surfaced gaps like "who authorizes paying the ransom?" and "does legal need to be on the call before we notify regulators?" — decisions that cost critical hours when made under pressure for the first time.

## Key facts
- Tabletop exercises are **discussion-based**, not operational — no systems are touched, no failovers triggered; contrast with **functional** or **full-scale** drills that involve actual execution
- They typically follow an **injects** model: a facilitator introduces scenario updates ("now the attacker has pivoted to your backup systems") to stress-test decision-making
- Governed by frameworks like **NIST SP 800-84** (Testing, Training, and Exercise Programs) and **FEMA's Homeland Security Exercise and Evaluation Program (HSEEP)**
- A key deliverable is the **After-Action Report (AAR)**, documenting gaps, findings, and corrective action items
- Common participants include IR team, legal, communications/PR, C-suite, and third-party vendors — not just technical staff
- For **Security+ and CySA+**, tabletop exercises fall under Business Continuity Planning (BCP) and Incident Response (IR) domains
