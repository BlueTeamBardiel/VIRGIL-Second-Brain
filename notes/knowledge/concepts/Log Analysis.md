# Log Analysis

## What it is
Like a ship's black box after a crash, logs are the timestamped record of everything a system did — and analyzing them means reconstructing *exactly* what happened and when. Precisely defined, log analysis is the systematic examination of event records generated by operating systems, applications, and network devices to detect anomalies, investigate incidents, and establish forensic timelines.

## Why it matters
During the 2020 SolarWinds supply chain attack, attackers operated undetected for months — but their lateral movement *was* recorded in Windows Event Logs and network flow data. Organizations with centralized SIEM systems that actively analyzed those logs could detect suspicious authentication patterns (e.g., service accounts logging in at 3 AM from unusual hosts) and trigger alerts before exfiltration completed.

## Key facts
- **Windows Event IDs to memorize**: 4624 (successful logon), 4625 (failed logon), 4648 (explicit credential use), 4688 (process creation), 4720 (user account created)
- **Syslog severity levels** run 0 (Emergency) through 7 (Debug) — Security+ expects you to know that severity 2 = Critical, not to be confused with "high"
- **Log retention** matters legally: PCI-DSS requires 12 months of log retention with 3 months immediately available
- **SIEM correlation rules** reduce noise by grouping related events — five failed logins followed by a success = one correlated brute-force alert, not six separate events
- **Timestamps must be normalized** to UTC in multi-source environments; timezone mismatches are a common source of failed forensic timelines
