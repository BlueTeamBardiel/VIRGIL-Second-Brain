# PHI

## What it is
Like a medical chart locked in a hospital records room — your name, diagnosis, and insurance number all bundled together — PHI (Protected Health Information) is any individually identifiable health information created, received, or transmitted by a covered entity. Under HIPAA, PHI includes 18 specific identifiers (names, dates, geographic data, etc.) linked to a person's past, present, or future health condition, treatment, or payment.

## Why it matters
In 2015, Anthem Health suffered a breach exposing nearly 78.8 million records containing names, SSNs, birth dates, and medical IDs — all classified as PHI. Attackers used spear-phishing to steal credentials and exfiltrated the data without encrypting a single file, exploiting Anthem's failure to encrypt data at rest. The resulting HHS settlement reached $16 million, demonstrating that PHI breaches carry uniquely severe regulatory consequences beyond typical data breach costs.

## Key facts
- HIPAA defines **18 PHI identifiers** — including names, phone numbers, IP addresses, device serial numbers, and full-face photographs
- PHI is governed by **HIPAA's Privacy Rule** (restricts use/disclosure) and **Security Rule** (administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for ePHI)
- **ePHI** (electronic PHI) specifically triggers the Security Rule requirements; breaches must be reported to HHS within **60 days** of discovery
- Organizations handling PHI are either **Covered Entities** (hospitals, insurers, providers) or **Business Associates** (third-party vendors) — both share liability
- The **HIPAA Breach Notification Rule** requires notifying affected individuals, HHS, and sometimes media if >500 residents of a state are impacted
- PHI has **high black market value** (~$250–$1,000 per record) compared to credit card data (~$5), making healthcare a prime ransomware target
