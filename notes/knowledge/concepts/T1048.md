# T1048

## What it is
Like a spy smuggling classified documents out of an embassy hidden inside a diplomatic pouch that guards wave through unchecked, T1048 (Exfiltration Over Alternative Protocol) is the technique of stealing data by routing it through protocols not normally monitored for outbound threats — such as DNS, ICMP, FTP, or SMTP — instead of standard HTTPS channels. Attackers deliberately choose these "trusted" channels because security teams tend to focus egress filtering on web traffic while leaving other protocols wide open.

## Why it matters
In the 2020 SolarWinds compromise, attackers used DNS-based exfiltration to quietly siphon reconnaissance data out of victim networks — DNS queries containing encoded payload fragments flew past perimeter defenses because DNS traffic is almost universally permitted outbound. Defenders who had robust HTTP inspection but no DNS traffic analysis remained completely blind to the outbound data flow.

## Key facts
- **DNS tunneling** is the most common sub-technique (T1048.003), encoding stolen data in subdomain labels of DNS queries sent to attacker-controlled resolvers
- **ICMP exfiltration** hides data in ping packet payloads — tools like `icmpsh` and `ptunnel` automate this
- **FTP/SFTP** (T1048.002) is used when attackers have established credentials; often blends with legitimate sysadmin behavior
- Detection relies on **behavioral anomalies**: unusually high DNS query volume, abnormally large DNS responses, or ICMP packets with non-standard payload sizes
- Mitigation includes **egress filtering**, DNS RPZ (Response Policy Zones), and monitoring with tools like Zeek/Bro that inspect protocol content, not just headers
