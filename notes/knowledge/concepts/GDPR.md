# GDPR

## What it is
Think of GDPR as a strict European landlord who owns the building where data lives: tenants (companies) must follow exact rules about how they store, use, and dispose of their residents' belongings — or face massive fines. Formally, the General Data Protection Regulation is an EU law (effective May 2018) that governs how organizations collect, process, and protect the personal data of EU citizens, regardless of where the organization itself is located.

## Why it matters
In 2019, British Airways suffered a data breach exposing ~500,000 customers' personal and payment data — the UK's ICO initially fined them £183 million under GDPR for failing to implement adequate security controls. This demonstrates that GDPR isn't just a privacy framework; it creates direct financial liability for organizations with poor security posture, forcing companies to treat data protection as a business risk, not just a compliance checkbox.

## Key facts
- **72-hour breach notification rule**: Organizations must report personal data breaches to the relevant supervisory authority within 72 hours of discovery
- **Maximum fines**: Up to €20 million OR 4% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher) for the most serious violations
- **Data Subject Rights**: Includes Right to Access, Right to Erasure ("Right to be Forgotten"), Right to Data Portability, and Right to Restrict Processing
- **Data Protection Officer (DPO)**: Required for organizations that process data at large scale or handle sensitive categories of data (health, biometrics, criminal records)
- **Lawful basis for processing**: Organizations must establish one of six legal bases before processing personal data (consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests)
