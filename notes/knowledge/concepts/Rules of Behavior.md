# Rules of Behavior

## What it is
Like the laminated safety card in an airplane seat pocket — nobody reads it until something goes wrong, but it legally defines what passengers are expected to do. Rules of Behavior (RoB) are formally documented policies that specify acceptable and unacceptable actions for users who access an information system, establishing accountability before access is granted.

## Why it matters
In 2014, a VA hospital employee copied unencrypted veteran records to a personal USB drive "to work from home," claiming no one told him it was prohibited. Had signed Rules of Behavior been in place and enforced, the organization would have had clear legal standing for disciplinary action and demonstrated due diligence to regulators — potentially avoiding millions in fines and breach notification costs.

## Key facts
- Rules of Behavior are required by **NIST SP 800-53 (PL-4)** and are mandatory for federal systems under FISMA compliance
- Users must **read, sign, and acknowledge** RoB documents before being granted system access — the signature creates legal accountability
- RoB typically address: personal use policies, password handling, data classification obligations, social media restrictions, and consequence statements for violations
- Unlike an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP), RoB are specifically scoped to **a particular system or set of systems**, not the organization broadly
- RoB must be **reviewed and re-signed** when systems change significantly or on a defined periodic cycle (commonly annual)
