# cyber operations

## What it is
Like a military campaign where ground troops, air support, and intelligence units coordinate toward a strategic objective, cyber operations are structured, mission-driven activities that combine offensive, defensive, and intelligence capabilities to achieve specific effects in or through cyberspace. Precisely defined, cyber operations are organized actions taken by nation-states, military units, or advanced threat actors to manipulate, deny, disrupt, degrade, or destroy information systems for strategic, political, or military gain.

## Why it matters
During the 2015–2016 Ukraine power grid attacks, Russia's Sandworm team executed a full-spectrum cyber operation: they used spearphishing to gain initial access, deployed BlackEnergy malware for persistence, conducted reconnaissance over months, then coordinated simultaneous SCADA disruptions that cut power to 230,000 civilians. This illustrates how cyber operations unfold across months with deliberate phases rather than as isolated intrusion events.

## Key facts
- Cyber operations are categorized by DoD into three types: **Offensive Cyber Operations (OCO)**, **Defensive Cyber Operations (DCO)**, and **DODIN Operations** (Department of Defense Information Network)
- The **Cyber Kill Chain** (Lockheed Martin) maps the typical phases of a cyber operation: Reconnaissance → Weaponization → Delivery → Exploitation → Installation → C2 → Actions on Objectives
- Nation-state cyber operations often operate under **OPSEC constraints** lasting months or years before executing their final payload
- **Attribution** in cyber operations is intentionally difficult; adversaries use false flags, third-party infrastructure, and shared toolsets to complicate forensic analysis
- The **Tallinn Manual** provides the leading legal framework analyzing when cyber operations constitute acts of war under international law
