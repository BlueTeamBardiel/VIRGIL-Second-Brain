# NIST SP 800-53

## What it is
Think of it as the master recipe book for federal kitchen safety — every dish (system) must follow specific preparation standards (controls) to avoid poisoning the diners (data breaches). More precisely, NIST SP 800-53 is a publication from the National Institute of Standards and Technology that provides a comprehensive catalog of security and privacy controls for federal information systems and organizations. It forms the technical backbone of the Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program (FedRAMP) and the Risk Management Framework (RMF).

## Why it matters
When the Office of Personnel Management suffered its catastrophic 2015 breach — exposing 21.5 million security clearance records — investigators found that multi-factor authentication controls (Control IA-2 in SP 800-53) had not been implemented on critical systems. Had the control baseline been properly enforced, lateral movement and credential theft would have been significantly harder. This breach directly accelerated mandatory SP 800-53 compliance audits across federal agencies.

## Key facts
- Organized into **20 control families** (e.g., AC = Access Control, IR = Incident Response, SI = System and Information Integrity), each with individual controls and enhancements
- Defines **three impact baselines** — Low, Moderate, and High — derived from FIPS 199 categorization; each baseline specifies which controls are required
- **Revision 5 (2020)** was a landmark update: it made controls technology-neutral and added a dedicated **Privacy control family (PT)**
- SP 800-53 pairs with **SP 800-53A** (assessment procedures) and **SP 800-53B** (control baselines) — they are separate but interdependent documents
- Mandatory for federal agencies under **FISMA**; widely adopted by private sector organizations as a gold-standard security framework
