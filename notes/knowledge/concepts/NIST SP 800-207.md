# NIST SP 800-207

## What it is
Like a nightclub that checks your ID at every door inside the building — not just at the entrance — Zero Trust Architecture assumes no user or device is automatically trusted, even after initial authentication. NIST SP 800-207 is the federal publication that formally defines Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA), establishing its core tenets, logical components, and deployment models for government and enterprise networks.

## Why it matters
In the 2020 SolarWinds attack, adversaries moved laterally across networks for months *after* gaining initial access — precisely because perimeter-based trust allowed internal traffic to flow freely. A ZTA implementation following SP 800-207 would require continuous verification at each resource request, dramatically limiting that lateral movement by enforcing least-privilege access decisions through a Policy Decision Point (PDP) before every transaction.

## Key facts
- Defines **seven core tenets** of Zero Trust, including "all communication is secured regardless of network location" and "access to resources is determined dynamically"
- Introduces the **Policy Decision Point (PDP)** and **Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)** as the two critical logical components — the PDP evaluates access requests; the PEP grants or blocks them
- Describes **three ZTA deployment approaches**: identity-governed, micro-segmentation, and software-defined perimeter (network-based)
- Explicitly states that the **network location is no longer sufficient** for trust — replaces implicit trust with continuous, risk-based evaluation
- Published in **August 2020** by NIST; applies directly to federal agency modernization under Executive Order 14028 on Cybersecurity
