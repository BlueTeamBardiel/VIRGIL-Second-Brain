# Dark Web Monitoring

## What it is
Think of it like hiring a private investigator to haunt underground flea markets where stolen goods are sold — except the goods are your employees' credentials, your company's source code, or your customers' credit card numbers. Dark web monitoring is the continuous process of scanning hidden services (Tor-based markets, paste sites, private forums) for an organization's sensitive data to detect breaches and credential exposure before attackers can weaponize them.

## Why it matters
In 2021, a ransomware group compromised a healthcare network and posted 50GB of patient records on a dark web forum as a negotiation tactic. Organizations running dark web monitoring services detected the leak within hours, allowing the legal and incident response teams to activate before journalists or patients discovered it independently — compressing the breach response window from weeks to days.

## Key facts
- Dark web monitoring is a **detective control**, not a preventive one — it tells you a breach *already happened*, not that it's happening now
- Commonly monitored data types: corporate email credentials, PII, financial card dumps, API keys, and proprietary source code
- Tools like **Recorded Future**, **SpyCloud**, and **Have I Been Pwned** aggregate breach data from dark web sources; HIBP alone indexes billions of compromised credentials
- A credential found on the dark web is an automatic trigger for **mandatory password reset** and investigation under most IR playbooks
- Dark web monitoring feeds directly into **threat intelligence programs** and can satisfy compliance requirements under frameworks like NIST CSF and certain cyber insurance policies
