# CWE-613

## What it is
Like a library book that never gets recalled no matter how overdue it becomes, an authentication token that never expires keeps granting access indefinitely. CWE-613 is Insufficient Session Expiration — a weakness where an application fails to properly invalidate session tokens or credentials after a reasonable timeout period or after logout.

## Why it matters
In 2012, attackers exploiting stolen session cookies from LinkedIn's breach continued accessing accounts long after the incident because session tokens were never invalidated server-side. A properly implemented session expiration policy would have rendered those stolen tokens useless within minutes or hours, dramatically limiting the blast radius of credential theft.

## Key facts
- **Logout ≠ Expiration**: Many vulnerable apps only delete the client-side cookie on logout but keep the server-side session alive — stolen tokens still work.
- **OWASP Top 10 mapping**: CWE-613 contributes to A07:2021 (Identification and Authentication Failures), a frequent exam topic.
- **Two expiration types matter**: *Absolute timeout* (session dies after X hours regardless of activity) and *idle timeout* (session dies after X minutes of inactivity) — both should be implemented.
- **Recommended timeframes**: NIST SP 800-63B recommends reauthentication after 30 minutes of inactivity for sensitive applications; 12 hours absolute maximum.
- **JWT-specific danger**: Stateless JWT tokens with distant or missing `exp` claims are a classic CWE-613 manifestation — the server cannot revoke them without maintaining a deny-list.
