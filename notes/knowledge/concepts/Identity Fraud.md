# Identity Fraud

## What it is
Like a forger who steals a master painter's signature and slaps it on counterfeit canvases to sell as originals, identity fraud occurs when an attacker uses stolen personal information to impersonate a victim for financial or criminal gain. Unlike identity theft (the act of stealing the data), identity fraud is the *use* of that stolen data — opening credit accounts, filing false tax returns, or accessing medical benefits under someone else's name.

## Why it matters
In 2021, attackers used stolen SSNs and personal data harvested from the Equifax breach to file fraudulent unemployment claims, costing states over $400 billion in pandemic relief funds. Defenders counter this through multi-factor identity verification, knowledge-based authentication challenges, and real-time credit freeze alerts — layered controls designed to break the attacker's ability to impersonate at the application layer.

## Key facts
- **Identity fraud ≠ identity theft**: theft is the data acquisition; fraud is the criminal exploitation of it — Security+ distinguishes these terms
- Synthetic identity fraud combines real and fabricated data (e.g., a valid SSN with a fake name), making it harder to detect than pure impersonation
- The FTC's IdentityTheft.gov is the official U.S. reporting mechanism — relevant for compliance and incident response scenarios
- Medical identity fraud is particularly dangerous because it corrupts health records, potentially causing life-threatening treatment errors
- Credit freezes (Section 301 of the Economic Growth Act) are the strongest consumer-level control — they prevent new account fraud even if credentials are compromised
