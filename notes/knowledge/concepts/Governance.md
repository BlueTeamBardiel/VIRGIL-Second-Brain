# Governance

## What it is
Think of governance like the rulebook and referee system for a professional sports league — it doesn't play the game, but it defines what's allowed, who enforces it, and what happens when someone cheats. In cybersecurity, governance is the framework of policies, roles, accountability structures, and decision-making processes that direct how an organization manages and controls its security program. It answers "who decides what?" rather than "how do we technically do it?"

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax suffered a breach exposing 147 million records — not because the patch didn't exist (it did), but because no governance structure clearly assigned ownership for patching the vulnerable Apache Struts component. A functioning governance model would have defined accountability chains, required audit trails on critical systems, and enforced patch SLAs — making the breach far less likely regardless of technical controls.

## Key facts
- Governance frameworks include **NIST CSF**, **ISO/IEC 27001**, and **COBIT** — each providing structure for aligning security with business objectives
- **Separation of duties** is a core governance control: no single person should both approve and execute a high-risk action
- Governance sits above risk management and compliance — it *drives* both rather than responding to them
- **Board-level accountability** is a governance requirement under regulations like SOX and GDPR Article 5; security is not just an IT issue
- The three governance documents hierarchy: **Policies** (what must be done) → **Standards** (specific requirements) → **Procedures** (how to do it)
