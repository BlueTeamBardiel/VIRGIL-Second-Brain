# MOU

## What it is
Like two neighboring fire departments agreeing to share equipment during emergencies before signing any formal contracts, a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) is a non-binding written agreement that documents mutual intent to cooperate between two organizations. It establishes shared expectations and responsibilities without creating legal obligations, serving as a precursor to or substitute for a formal contract.

## Why it matters
When a hospital and a cloud vendor begin sharing patient data for a joint research initiative, an MOU defines data handling expectations, access boundaries, and incident notification procedures before legal contracts are finalized. This gap period is dangerous — if a breach occurs and no MOU exists, responsibility for notification and remediation becomes legally ambiguous, potentially delaying response and increasing regulatory exposure under HIPAA.

## Key facts
- MOUs are **non-legally binding** but carry significant reputational and operational weight — violating one damages trust and can trigger formal legal consequences in related agreements
- In government and military contexts, MOUs are used extensively to define **information sharing arrangements** between agencies (e.g., DHS and FBI sharing threat intelligence)
- MOUs should document: **scope of cooperation, data handling expectations, points of contact, duration, and termination conditions**
- Distinguished from an **SLA** (Service Level Agreement), which is legally binding and metrics-driven, and from an **ISA** (Interconnection Security Agreement), which governs technical connections between networks
- For Security+ and CySA+: MOUs appear in the **third-party risk and vendor management** domain — they represent the lowest formality tier in the agreement hierarchy: MOU → SLA → Contract
