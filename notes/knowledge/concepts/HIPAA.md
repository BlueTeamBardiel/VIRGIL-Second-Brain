# HIPAA

## What it is
Think of HIPAA as a federal bodyguard assigned specifically to medical records — it doesn't protect all your data, just the stuff written on your chart. The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (1996) is a U.S. federal law mandating how covered entities (hospitals, insurers, healthcare providers) and their business associates must protect Protected Health Information (PHI). It establishes both privacy rights and specific technical/administrative security requirements.

## Why it matters
In 2015, Anthem Health suffered a breach exposing 78.8 million patient records through a spear-phishing attack that compromised an administrator's credentials — resulting in a $115 million settlement. HIPAA's Security Rule would require Anthem to have implemented access controls, audit logging, and workforce security training, all of which were found deficient. This case illustrates that HIPAA compliance is both a legal obligation and a minimum security baseline, not a ceiling.

## Key facts
- **Three Rules**: Privacy Rule (patient rights over PHI), Security Rule (technical/administrative/physical safeguards for ePHI), Breach Notification Rule (notify affected individuals within 60 days of discovery)
- **Covered Entities vs. Business Associates**: Both are liable; a cloud provider storing ePHI is a Business Associate and requires a signed BAA (Business Associate Agreement)
- **ePHI**: Electronic PHI must be protected with access controls, encryption in transit and at rest, audit controls, and integrity mechanisms
- **Breach threshold**: Exposure of unsecured PHI affecting 500+ individuals requires notification to HHS *and* media in the affected state
- **Penalties**: Tiered from $100–$50,000 per violation (up to $1.9M/year per category) depending on culpability — willful neglect carries the highest fines
