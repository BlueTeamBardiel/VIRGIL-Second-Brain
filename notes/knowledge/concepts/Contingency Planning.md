# Contingency Planning

## What it is
Like a submarine crew drilling for flooding scenarios before they ever leave port, contingency planning is the proactive process of preparing responses to adverse events before they occur. It is the organizational discipline of identifying potential disruptions and documenting the procedures, resources, and personnel needed to maintain or restore operations. It encompasses Business Continuity Plans (BCP), Disaster Recovery Plans (DRP), and Incident Response Plans (IRP) under one strategic umbrella.

## Why it matters
In 2021, a ransomware attack on Colonial Pipeline halted 45% of the U.S. East Coast's fuel supply for nearly six days — largely because operational recovery procedures were inadequate for a cyber-triggered shutdown. Organizations with mature contingency plans define their Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPO) in advance, enabling faster, decisive action rather than improvised chaos under pressure.

## Key facts
- **BCP** focuses on keeping *business functions* alive during a disruption; **DRP** focuses specifically on restoring *IT systems and data* after a disaster — they are complementary, not interchangeable
- **RTO** = maximum acceptable downtime before business impact becomes critical; **RPO** = maximum acceptable data loss measured in time
- NIST SP 800-34 is the authoritative guide for IT Contingency Planning in federal environments
- Contingency plans must be **tested regularly** — tabletop exercises, parallel testing, and full-interruption tests are all valid methods (full-interruption is highest risk/highest fidelity)
- Plans must include **alternate processing sites**: hot sites (fully operational, highest cost), warm sites (partially equipped), and cold sites (space only, lowest cost)
