# Right-to-Audit Clause

## What it is
Think of it like a landlord insisting on the right to inspect an apartment before renewing a lease — you're not taking the tenant's word that everything is fine. A right-to-audit clause is a contractual provision that grants an organization the legal authority to examine a vendor's or third-party's security controls, processes, and compliance posture at any time or on a scheduled basis. It ensures that trust in the supply chain is verified, not assumed.

## Why it matters
In 2013, Target's catastrophic breach originated through a compromised HVAC vendor — a third party with network access but minimal oversight. A right-to-audit clause with that vendor could have mandated periodic security reviews, potentially revealing the weak credentials that attackers exploited. It transforms vendor security from a handshake agreement into an enforceable, inspectable obligation.

## Key facts
- Right-to-audit clauses appear in vendor contracts, SLAs, and third-party agreements and are a key component of **supply chain risk management**
- They can require vendors to produce **SOC 2 Type II reports**, penetration test results, or allow direct on-site inspections
- Absence of this clause means an organization has **no legal standing** to demand security evidence from a non-compliant vendor
- Relevant to **NIST SP 800-161** (supply chain risk management) and frameworks like ISO 27001 Annex A control A.15 (supplier relationships)
- On Security+/CySA+ exams, right-to-audit appears under **third-party risk**, **vendor management**, and **data governance** domains
