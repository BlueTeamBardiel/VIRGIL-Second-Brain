# Threat Actor

## What it is
Like a chess opponent whose skill level and motivation determines how you prepare your defense, a threat actor is any individual or group that poses a risk to an organization's systems or data. Precisely defined: a threat actor is an entity responsible for a cybersecurity incident or attack, characterized by their intent, capability, and resources.

## Why it matters
In 2020, the SolarWinds supply chain attack was attributed to APT29 (Cozy Bear), a nation-state threat actor backed by Russian intelligence. Identifying the actor type immediately told defenders to expect extremely sophisticated, long-dwell-time intrusions — not opportunistic ransomware — which shaped the entire incident response and remediation strategy.

## Key facts
- **Nation-state actors** have the highest capability and resources; motivated by espionage, sabotage, or geopolitical goals (e.g., Lazarus Group/North Korea)
- **Hacktivists** are motivated by ideology, not profit; they typically use DDoS and defacement (e.g., Anonymous)
- **Insider threats** are uniquely dangerous because they already have authorized access — malicious insiders cause the highest average financial damage per incident
- **Script kiddies** have low sophistication, relying on pre-built tools; still dangerous because they generate high volume noise
- **Attributes used to classify threat actors**: intent (why), sophistication (how skilled), resources (how funded), and attack surface targeted — all four appear on Security+/CySA+ exams
