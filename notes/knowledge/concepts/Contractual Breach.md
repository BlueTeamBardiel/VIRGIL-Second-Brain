# Contractual Breach

## What it is
Like a locksmith who copies your house key for a third party despite signing an NDA, a contractual breach occurs when one party fails to fulfill legally binding obligations outlined in a security agreement. In cybersecurity, this typically means a vendor, employee, or partner violates terms defined in contracts such as NDAs, SLAs, or MSAs — creating legal liability and potential security exposure simultaneously.

## Why it matters
In 2020, a major cloud provider's SLA breach — failing to meet guaranteed uptime thresholds — exposed a healthcare client to HIPAA liability because critical patient systems went offline beyond contractually permitted windows. The client had outsourced security responsibilities but retained legal accountability, illustrating why contractual language around shared responsibility must be precise and enforceable.

## Key facts
- **SLA (Service Level Agreement)** breaches are the most common contractual breach type in cloud security — failing uptime, RTO, or RPO guarantees creates measurable damages.
- **NDAs** (Non-Disclosure Agreements) are legally binding; a contractor leaking proprietary vulnerability data constitutes both a contractual breach AND potentially a criminal offense under the CFAA.
- Breach of contract is a **civil matter**, not criminal — remedies include damages, injunctions, or contract termination, not imprisonment.
- **Third-party risk management** programs exist specifically to monitor vendor compliance and detect contractual breaches before they cause security incidents.
- Under **GDPR and HIPAA**, a vendor's contractual breach of a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) or BAA can trigger regulatory fines against *both* parties, not just the breaching vendor.
