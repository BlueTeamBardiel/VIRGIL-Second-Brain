# NIST CSF

## What it is
Think of it as a building's fire safety blueprint — it doesn't mandate specific sprinkler brands, but it tells you exactly what categories of protection you need and how to measure readiness. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) is a voluntary risk management framework published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology that organizes security activities into a common language for identifying, protecting against, detecting, responding to, and recovering from cyber threats.

## Why it matters
After the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack, investigators found the company lacked mature detection and response capabilities — gaps that CSF's "Detect" and "Respond" functions would have directly addressed. Organizations using CSF could benchmark their current posture against target states on a four-tier maturity scale, prioritizing exactly the network segmentation and incident response planning that was missing.

## Key facts
- **Five core functions (now six in CSF 2.0):** Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover — with **Govern** added in the 2024 v2.0 revision to emphasize leadership accountability
- **Not a compliance mandate** — it's voluntary for most sectors, though critical infrastructure operators are strongly encouraged to adopt it; FISMA compliance uses the related NIST SP 800-53
- **Tiers 1–4** describe implementation maturity: Partial → Risk Informed → Repeatable → Adaptive; higher tiers mean more proactive, integrated risk management
- **Profiles** allow organizations to map *current state* vs. *target state* to create a prioritized roadmap — this gap analysis is a core exam concept
- CSF is **framework-agnostic** and designed to complement ISO 27001, COBIT, and CIS Controls rather than replace them
