# Emergency Contacts

## What it is
Like the laminated card on an airplane seatback telling you exactly who to call when smoke fills the cabin, emergency contacts in cybersecurity are pre-designated individuals and organizations to notify during an active incident. They are a formalized list of internal responders, external vendors, law enforcement agencies, and regulatory bodies that must be reached according to a defined escalation path during a security event.

## Why it matters
During the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack, delayed notification of CISA and FBI allowed attackers more time to operate and complicated the federal response. Organizations with pre-established emergency contact lists — including vendor hotline numbers, legal counsel, and sector-specific ISACs — can compress response time from hours to minutes by eliminating the frantic "who do we call?" phase during a crisis.

## Key facts
- Emergency contacts must be documented **out-of-band** (printed hard copies, offline storage) because a network compromise may make digital contact lists inaccessible
- CISA (1-888-282-0870) and the FBI Cyber Division are standard federal contacts U.S. organizations should pre-register with before an incident occurs
- The **IR plan** must specify contacts by *role*, not just name, to survive personnel turnover (e.g., "CISO on-call" rather than "Jane Smith")
- Regulatory frameworks like HIPAA (72-hour breach notification) and GDPR (72-hour DPA notification) make external contacts legally mandatory, not optional
- Tabletop exercises should **test** emergency contact lists — wrong numbers or outdated emails are common findings that only surface during drills
