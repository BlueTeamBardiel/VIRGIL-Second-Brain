# Memorandum of Understanding

## What it is
Think of it like two neighbors agreeing to share a driveway — no lawyers, no deed transfer, just a handshake documented on paper. An MOU is a non-binding formal agreement between two or more organizations that outlines mutual expectations, roles, and intentions for cooperation, typically before a legally enforceable contract is in place. It establishes *what* each party agrees to do without creating legal liability for non-performance.

## Why it matters
When two companies plan to share threat intelligence feeds or interconnect their networks, they often sign an MOU before the formal Interconnection Security Agreement (ISA) is finalized. This matters defensively because a poorly scoped MOU can leave ambiguity about who is responsible for incident response when a shared system is breached — attackers exploit organizational confusion during the critical first hours of an incident.

## Key facts
- An MOU is **not legally binding** — it expresses intent, not obligation; contrast this with a contract or SLA which are enforceable
- MOUs are commonly used between **government agencies or between public and private sector partners** to formalize collaboration without procurement delays
- On Security+/CySA+ exams, MOU appears alongside **ISA, SLA, BPA (Business Partnership Agreement), and NDA** as agreement types you must distinguish
- MOUs typically define **scope, purpose, duration, and points of contact** — but not detailed technical controls (that belongs in the ISA)
- When third-party risk is assessed, an MOU alone is considered **insufficient** for high-sensitivity data sharing; a full contract with compliance clauses is required
