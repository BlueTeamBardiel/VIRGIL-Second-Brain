# Procurement

## What it is
Like a restaurant that must vet every supplier before putting ingredients on the plate, procurement is the formal process an organization uses to evaluate, acquire, and onboard hardware, software, and services. It is the security-gated pipeline through which third-party components enter an organization's environment, encompassing vendor assessment, contract negotiation, and supply chain risk management.

## Why it matters
In 2020, SolarWinds' Orion software was trojanized before delivery — organizations that skipped rigorous procurement controls installed the backdoored update without question, giving attackers access to thousands of networks including U.S. federal agencies. A mature procurement process requiring cryptographic build verification and vendor security attestations would have raised early flags.

## Key facts
- **Vendor Due Diligence**: Procurement should include review of vendor SOC 2 reports, penetration test results, and financial stability to assess third-party risk before contract signing.
- **Right-to-Audit Clauses**: Contracts should include provisions allowing the purchasing organization to audit the vendor's security controls — this is a Security+ testable contract term.
- **End-of-Life (EOL) Awareness**: Procurement must document product support timelines; acquiring hardware or software near EOL introduces unpatched vulnerability risk from day one.
- **Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM)**: NIST SP 800-161 provides guidance specifically for managing cybersecurity risks across the supply chain during procurement decisions.
- **Hardware Integrity**: Procurement of hardware should include tamper-evident packaging verification and, for sensitive environments, known-good hash validation of firmware — counterfeit Cisco routers have been documented as real attack vectors.
