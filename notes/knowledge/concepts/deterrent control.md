# deterrent control

## What it is
Like a "Beware of Dog" sign that may or may not be backed by an actual dog, a deterrent control discourages attackers from attempting an action by increasing their perceived risk or effort. It doesn't technically prevent an attack — it psychologically discourages one before it starts.

## Why it matters
A company posts visible security camera signage and warning banners on login portals stating "All activity is monitored and logged." A would-be insider threat or opportunistic attacker, seeing these warnings, may abandon their attempt entirely — even if the monitoring infrastructure behind the sign is minimal. The deterrent worked without a single firewall rule firing.

## Key facts
- Deterrent controls are **administrative or physical** in nature and operate on *perception* rather than technical enforcement — examples include warning banners, security guards, visible cameras, and legal notices.
- On the Security+ exam, deterrent is classified as a **control type** (alongside preventive, detective, corrective, compensating) — not a control category (which is administrative, technical, physical).
- A **login warning banner** ("unauthorized access is prohibited and monitored") is the canonical technical-context example of a deterrent control.
- Deterrent controls can **fail if the attacker calls the bluff** — a fake camera dome deters only until someone notices the lack of wiring.
- They are often **layered with other controls**: a deterrent slows attackers down long enough for detective and preventive controls to engage.
