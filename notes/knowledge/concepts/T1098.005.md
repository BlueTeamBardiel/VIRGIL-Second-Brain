# T1098.005

## What it is
Like a thief who doesn't pick the lock but instead gets a copy of the master key made at the hardware store, this technique involves adding adversary-controlled credentials to legitimate cloud accounts rather than stealing existing ones. T1098.005 (Account Manipulation: Device Registration) occurs when an attacker registers a new device or credential — such as an MFA device or Entra ID (Azure AD) joined device — to a compromised account, enabling persistent access that survives password resets.

## Why it matters
In the 2022 Lapsus$ attacks, threat actors registered attacker-controlled MFA devices to compromised Microsoft and Okta accounts, locking out legitimate users and maintaining access even after incident responders changed passwords. Defenders must monitor for unexpected device registrations in Azure AD audit logs, particularly registrations occurring outside business hours or from unfamiliar IP geolocations.

## Key facts
- Attackers target cloud identity platforms like Microsoft Entra ID, Okta, and AWS IAM to register rogue devices or authenticator apps
- Persistence survives password resets because the registered device/MFA factor is a separate credential object
- Commonly follows initial access via phishing or credential stuffing — registration is the *persistence* phase, not the entry
- Detection signal: `Add registered owner to device` or `Register device` events in Entra ID audit logs from anomalous accounts
- Conditional Access Policies requiring compliant/managed devices can block attacker-registered rogue devices from authenticating
