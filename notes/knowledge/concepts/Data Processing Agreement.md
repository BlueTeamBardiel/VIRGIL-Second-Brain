# Data Processing Agreement

## What it is
Like a detailed catering contract that specifies exactly what the chef can cook, who touches the food, and what happens to leftovers — a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) is a legally binding contract between a data controller (the organization that owns the data) and a data processor (a third party that handles it on their behalf). It defines the scope, purpose, security obligations, and liability for processing personal data, and is mandated under GDPR Article 28.

## Why it matters
In 2023, a healthcare provider shared patient records with a cloud analytics vendor without a DPA in place. When the vendor suffered a breach and sold anonymized datasets to advertisers, the healthcare provider was held jointly liable under GDPR — fined €1.2 million despite never mishandling the data themselves. A proper DPA would have contractually bound the vendor to specific retention limits, breach notification windows (72 hours under GDPR), and prohibited onward data transfers.

## Key facts
- A DPA must specify the **subject matter, duration, nature, and purpose** of processing, plus the type of personal data and categories of data subjects
- Under GDPR, using a processor **without** a DPA makes the controller fully liable for the processor's violations
- DPAs must require processors to implement **Article 32 technical and organizational measures** (encryption, pseudonymization, access controls)
- Sub-processors (vendors hired by your vendor) must be covered by the **same contractual obligations** — the controller must approve them
- DPAs are distinct from Privacy Policies (public-facing) and NDAs (confidentiality only) — they address **operational security requirements**
