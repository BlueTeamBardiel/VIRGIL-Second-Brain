# CWE-255

## What it is
Like a hotel that hands out master keys without logging who receives them or requiring a signature, this weakness occurs when software mismanages credentials — storing, transmitting, or handling passwords, keys, or tokens insecurely. CWE-255 is a category-level weakness covering improper credential management across the entire lifecycle, from creation and storage to transmission and revocation.

## Why it matters
In the 2012 LinkedIn breach, over 6.5 million SHA-1 unsalted password hashes were dumped publicly and cracked within hours — a textbook CWE-255 failure where credentials were stored without adequate protection. Had LinkedIn used bcrypt or Argon2 with unique salts, the attacker's rainbow table attacks would have been computationally infeasible, buying time and protecting users.

## Key facts
- CWE-255 is a **category**, not a specific weakness — it parents more precise CWEs like CWE-256 (plaintext password storage), CWE-257 (recoverable password format), and CWE-522 (insufficiently protected credentials)
- Common manifestations: hardcoded credentials in source code (CWE-798), credentials in log files, or passwords sent over unencrypted channels
- NIST SP 800-63B provides the authoritative guidance on credential management — memorize that it prohibits SMS OTP as a *sole* authenticator for high-assurance systems
- Secure password storage requires adaptive hashing algorithms (bcrypt, scrypt, Argon2) — **not** MD5 or SHA-1, which are fast and attacker-friendly
- Default credentials left unchanged (e.g., admin/admin on routers) fall under this category and appear frequently on Security+ exam scenarios
