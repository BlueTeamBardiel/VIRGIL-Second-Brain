# Cybersquatting

## What it is
Imagine a squatter who moves into a prime piece of real estate the moment the original owner's lease expires, then demands a ransom to leave. Cybersquatting is exactly that — registering a domain name that is identical or confusingly similar to a legitimate brand, trademark, or person's name with the intent to profit from the goodwill of that identity. It is formalized as a violation under the **Anti-Cybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (ACPA)** of 1999 in the United States.

## Why it matters
A threat actor registers **paypa1.com** (using the numeral "1" instead of "l") before a phishing campaign launches, redirecting victims who mistype the URL to a credential-harvesting page. This variant — called **typosquatting** — was used in real attacks against PayPal, capturing thousands of login credentials before the domain was seized. Defenders monitor for lookalike domain registrations using tools like **dnstwist** to catch these registrations early.

## Key facts
- Cybersquatting violates the **ACPA (15 U.S.C. § 1125(d))**, which allows trademark holders to sue for statutory damages of $1,000–$100,000 per domain.
- **Typosquatting** exploits common misspellings; **combosquatting** appends words like "-login" or "-secure" to legitimate brand names.
- The **UDRP (Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy)** run by ICANN provides a faster, cheaper alternative to litigation for reclaiming domains.
- Cybersquatting is commonly paired with **phishing**, **malware distribution**, or **ad revenue farming** on parked pages.
- Defenders use **brand monitoring services** and **proactive defensive domain registration** (buying common misspellings themselves) as countermeasures.
