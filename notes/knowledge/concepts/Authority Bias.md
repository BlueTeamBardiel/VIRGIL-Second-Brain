# Authority Bias

## What it is
Like a soldier who keeps fighting a decades-long war because no commanding officer ever officially told him it ended, we follow authority figures even when the orders make no rational sense. Authority bias is the cognitive tendency to attribute greater accuracy, credibility, and compliance-worthiness to the commands or messages of perceived authority figures — regardless of whether that authority is legitimate.

## Why it matters
In a Business Email Compromise (BEC) attack, an attacker spoofs the CEO's email address and instructs the CFO to wire $2.3 million to a "vendor" immediately and confidentially. The CFO complies without verification precisely because authority bias suppresses their skepticism — questioning the boss feels socially costly, so the wire transfer happens before anyone thinks to pick up the phone.

## Key facts
- Authority bias is a core mechanism exploited in **pretexting** and **impersonation attacks**, where attackers pose as executives, IT helpdesk staff, or government officials
- The **FBI IC3 reports BEC losses exceeding $2.9 billion annually**, making authority-exploiting attacks among the costliest social engineering vectors
- **Vishing (voice phishing)** attacks frequently use caller ID spoofing to impersonate IT support, leveraging authority bias to extract credentials in real time
- Defense: organizations use **call-back verification procedures** and **dual-authorization controls** for financial transactions to interrupt authority-driven compliance
- Security awareness training that specifically **simulates CEO fraud emails** measurably reduces susceptibility, because naming the bias makes it visible to employees
