# threat actors

## What it is
Like casting villains in a heist film — each has different skills, motives, and resources — threat actors are the specific individuals or groups who carry out attacks against systems. Precisely defined: a threat actor is any entity that intentionally causes or enables harm to an organization's information systems, distinguished by motivation, capability, and sophistication.

## Why it matters
In 2020, the SolarWinds supply chain attack was attributed to APT29 (Cozy Bear), a nation-state threat actor linked to Russian intelligence. Understanding the actor type immediately informed defenders: this wasn't opportunistic ransomware but a patient, well-funded espionage operation — requiring fundamentally different incident response priorities, such as assuming long-term persistence and exfiltration over immediate system disruption.

## Key facts
- **Nation-state actors** have the highest capability and funding; goals are typically espionage, sabotage, or geopolitical advantage — not financial gain
- **Hacktivists** (e.g., Anonymous) are ideologically motivated; attacks tend toward defacement and DDoS rather than data theft
- **Organized crime** groups are profit-driven; most ransomware and credential-theft campaigns originate here
- **Insider threats** are subdivided into *malicious* (intentional harm) and *unintentional* (negligence) — both appear on Security+/CySA+ exams
- **Script kiddies** have low sophistication but high volume; they exploit existing tools and represent a significant portion of opportunistic attacks on unpatched systems
- Threat actors are characterized using **attributes**: internal vs. external, level of sophistication, resources/funding, and intent — all testable categories on CompTIA exams
