# Service Level Agreement

## What it is
Think of an SLA like a restaurant's reservation guarantee — if your table isn't ready within 15 minutes, you get a free appetizer. A Service Level Agreement (SLA) is a formal contract between a service provider and a customer that defines measurable performance standards — uptime percentages, response times, and remediation commitments — along with penalties for failing to meet them. It transforms vague promises into enforceable, quantified obligations.

## Why it matters
During a ransomware incident, an organization's cloud provider may be obligated by SLA to restore services within four hours — failure to meet this triggers financial penalties and may void the contract. Security teams use SLAs to pressure vendors into maintaining patch cycles and breach notification windows, ensuring that third-party weaknesses don't become your problem without accountability. A poorly written SLA with no security clauses left Target exposed when its HVAC vendor had network access with zero defined security requirements.

## Key facts
- **Uptime SLAs** are typically expressed as "nines" — 99.9% uptime allows ~8.7 hours of downtime per year; 99.999% allows ~5 minutes
- SLAs should specify **Mean Time to Repair (MTTR)** and **Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF)** as measurable security metrics
- **Security-specific SLA clauses** must define breach notification timelines (e.g., "notify within 72 hours"), aligning with GDPR and other regulatory requirements
- SLAs are a critical component of **vendor risk management** and third-party due diligence assessments on Security+ and CySA+
- When SLA terms aren't met, the remedy is typically a **Service Level Credit** — not necessarily full contract termination
