# Risk Reporting

## What it is
Like a ship captain logging weather conditions, ice sightings, and near-misses into a voyage journal so the whole fleet can make smarter decisions, risk reporting is the structured process of communicating identified risks, their likelihood, impact, and status to decision-makers and stakeholders. It transforms raw risk assessment data into actionable intelligence for leadership, auditors, and governance bodies.

## Why it matters
During the 2017 Equifax breach, risk reports had flagged the unpatched Apache Struts vulnerability weeks before exploitation — but those reports failed to reach the right people with urgency. Effective risk reporting ensures critical findings escalate to executives who can authorize emergency patching budgets, preventing the gap between "we knew" and "we acted."

## Key facts
- Risk reports must convey **four core elements**: risk description, likelihood rating, potential impact (financial/operational), and current mitigation status
- **Heat maps** (risk matrices) are the standard visual tool for reporting — plotting likelihood vs. impact in a color-coded grid for executive audiences
- Risk reporting feeds directly into the **risk register**, a living document tracking all identified risks and their treatment decisions over time
- Reports should be **audience-tiered**: technical details for IT/security teams, business impact summaries for executives, compliance evidence for auditors
- CySA+ and Security+ expect you to know that risk reporting supports **governance frameworks** like NIST RMF, ISO 27001, and SOC 2, where documented risk communication is a formal control requirement
- **Residual risk** (what remains after controls are applied) must be explicitly reported so leadership can make informed risk acceptance decisions
