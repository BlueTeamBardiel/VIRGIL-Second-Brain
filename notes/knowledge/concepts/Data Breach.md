# Data Breach

## What it is
Like a thief drilling through a bank vault wall and photographing every account ledger without taking a single bill — the damage is in the exposure, not the removal. A data breach is the unauthorized access, acquisition, or disclosure of sensitive, protected, or confidential information to an untrusted environment. The breach may involve exfiltration, but simply viewing or exposing data without authorization qualifies.

## Why it matters
In 2013, Target suffered a breach when attackers compromised an HVAC vendor's credentials, pivoted through the network, and exfiltrated 40 million credit card records via malware installed on POS terminals. This illustrates how third-party access and insufficient network segmentation transform a minor vendor relationship into a catastrophic data exposure event.

## Key facts
- **Notification requirements**: Under HIPAA, covered entities must notify affected individuals within 60 days of discovering a breach; PCI DSS requires immediate reporting to card brands and acquirers.
- **Three breach categories**: Confidentiality breach (unauthorized disclosure), Integrity breach (unauthorized modification), Availability breach (unauthorized destruction or loss).
- **Cost benchmark**: IBM's 2023 Cost of a Data Breach Report pegs the average breach at $4.45 million — a critical figure for risk quantification on exams.
- **Indicators of compromise (IOCs)**: Unusual outbound traffic, unexpected privileged account activity, and large data transfers at odd hours are primary detection signals for active breaches.
- **Breach vs. incident**: All breaches are security incidents, but not all incidents are breaches — a distinction Security+ explicitly tests. A breach requires confirmed data exposure.
