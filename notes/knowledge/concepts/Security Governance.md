# Security Governance

## What it is
Think of security governance like a city's building code: it doesn't swing the hammer, but it defines *what gets built, why, and who's accountable when a wall collapses*. Security governance is the framework of policies, roles, and oversight mechanisms that direct how an organization manages cybersecurity risk — ensuring security decisions align with business objectives and legal obligations.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax suffered a breach exposing 147 million records partly because no single executive owned the responsibility for patching known vulnerabilities — a governance failure, not just a technical one. Strong governance would have established clear accountability chains, mandatory patch timelines, and board-level reporting so the Apache Struts vulnerability couldn't linger unpatched for months.

## Key facts
- **Three lines of defense model**: operational controls (1st), risk/compliance oversight (2nd), independent audit (3rd) — a common governance framework tested on Security+
- **CISO vs. Board responsibility**: governance separates *strategic* security direction (board/executives) from *operational* security execution (CISO/security teams)
- **Key governance documents**: policies (what must be done), standards (specific requirements), procedures (how to do it), guidelines (recommendations) — know this hierarchy
- **NIST CSF and ISO 27001** are the two dominant governance frameworks; NIST CSF uses Identify-Protect-Detect-Respond-Recover; ISO 27001 is certification-based
- **Due care vs. due diligence**: due diligence = researching risks before acting; due care = taking reasonable steps to protect assets — both are legal/governance concepts appearing on exams
