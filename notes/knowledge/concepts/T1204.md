# T1204

## What it is
Like a con artist who tricks a bank teller into opening the vault door themselves, User Execution is when an attacker convinces a human victim to run malicious code on their behalf. Rather than exploiting a technical vulnerability, the attacker weaponizes human behavior — a click, a double-click, or a macro enable — to initiate execution. MITRE ATT&CK T1204 covers this technique under two sub-techniques: T1204.001 (Malicious Link) and T1204.002 (Malicious File).

## Why it matters
The 2017 NotPetya outbreak leveraged malicious Office documents distributed via phishing emails; users who enabled macros triggered a payload that spread laterally and wiped entire corporate networks. Defenders countering T1204 focus on disabling Office macros by default, applying Protected View policies, and running endpoint detection that flags unusual child processes spawned by Office applications or browsers.

## Key facts
- **T1204.001** involves tricking users into clicking a URL that downloads or directly executes malware (e.g., drive-by download link in a phishing email)
- **T1204.002** involves tricking users into opening and executing a malicious file — most commonly Office documents with embedded macros, LNK files, or disguised executables
- This technique requires **no vulnerability** in the OS or application — human action is the exploit
- A common defense is **Attack Surface Reduction (ASR) rules** in Microsoft Defender, specifically rules that block Office child process creation
- User Execution is frequently the **Initial Access delivery mechanism** for ransomware and RAT campaigns, making it one of the highest-frequency techniques seen in real-world incidents
