# ISO

## What it is
Think of ISO like the international building codes that ensure a skyscraper in Tokyo uses the same structural safety standards as one in Toronto — except for information security practices. The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) publishes globally recognized frameworks and standards that define how organizations should manage security, quality, and risk. In cybersecurity, ISO standards provide measurable, auditable benchmarks rather than vague best-practice suggestions.

## Why it matters
During a third-party vendor audit, a company can demand ISO 27001 certification as proof that a supplier has formally implemented an Information Security Management System (ISMS) — this happened repeatedly after the Target breach exposed how weak vendor security became an attack vector. Without a recognized standard like ISO 27001, there's no consistent way to verify that a third party actually controls data securely. Certification means an accredited external auditor has tested the controls, not just reviewed a self-assessment checklist.

## Key facts
- **ISO/IEC 27001** is the most security-relevant standard — it specifies requirements for establishing, implementing, and maintaining an ISMS
- **ISO/IEC 27002** is a companion document providing a catalog of security controls (not a certifiable standard itself — organizations certify against 27001, not 27002)
- **ISO/IEC 27701** extends 27001 to privacy information management, relevant for GDPR compliance programs
- **ISO 31000** covers enterprise risk management broadly and underpins how ISO 27001 approaches risk assessment
- ISO certification requires a **Statement of Applicability (SoA)** — a document declaring which controls from Annex A apply and why others are excluded
