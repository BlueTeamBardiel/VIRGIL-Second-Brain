# Cybercriminal Groups

## What it is
Like organized crime families running protection rackets, but instead of neighborhoods they extort hospitals and corporations — cybercriminal groups are structured organizations that coordinate attacks for financial gain, espionage, or disruption. Unlike lone hackers, they operate with defined roles (developers, money mules, negotiators), shared infrastructure, and profit-sharing models.

## Why it matters
In 2021, the REvil ransomware group attacked Kaseya VSA, a managed service provider platform, simultaneously compromising over 1,500 downstream businesses through a single supply chain exploit. This demonstrated how organized cybercriminal groups leverage third-party trust relationships to multiply damage far beyond what a solo attacker could achieve, forcing organizations to evaluate vendor risk as a primary attack surface.

## Key facts
- **RaaS (Ransomware-as-a-Service)** is the dominant business model — groups like LockBit and BlackCat provide ransomware tools to affiliates and take 20–30% of ransom proceeds
- **Attribution** uses TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures) mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework; groups are assigned tracking labels like APT41 (China-linked) or FIN7 (financially motivated)
- **Nation-state vs. criminal distinction**: APT groups are state-sponsored with political goals; cybercriminal groups are primarily profit-driven — though the line blurs when states use criminal proxies (e.g., Lazarus Group/North Korea)
- **Threat intelligence sharing** via ISACs (Information Sharing and Analysis Centers) helps defenders correlate IOCs across sectors and attribute attacks to known groups
- **Takedowns are temporary** — Operation Cronos disrupted LockBit in 2024, but the group resumed operations within weeks, demonstrating the resilience of distributed criminal infrastructure
