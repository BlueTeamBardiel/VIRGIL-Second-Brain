# Security Program Management

## What it is
Think of it like being the head coach of a sports team: you don't play every position yourself, but you design the playbook, allocate resources, measure performance, and ensure every player knows their role. Security Program Management is the structured, ongoing process of planning, governing, and coordinating an organization's entire cybersecurity effort — aligning people, processes, and technology to measurable business objectives.

## Why it matters
In 2020, SolarWinds suffered a supply chain compromise that went undetected for months — partly because security responsibilities were fragmented across teams with no unified program oversight. A mature security program with clearly defined ownership, continuous monitoring metrics, and executive accountability (a CISO with authority) would have reduced detection time and limited the blast radius through enforced vendor risk management processes.

## Key facts
- A security program is governed by a **Security Charter** that defines scope, authority, roles, and reporting structure — it's the foundational governance document.
- **KPIs and KRIs** (Key Performance Indicators / Key Risk Indicators) are used to measure program health; examples include mean time to detect (MTTD) and patch compliance rate.
- The **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)** — with its five functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover — is the most commonly referenced structure for building a security program.
- Security programs must align with **business objectives** and demonstrate ROI; risk-based prioritization (not compliance-only) is considered a program maturity marker.
- Maturity is often measured using models like **CMMI or the SSE-CMM**, ranging from Level 1 (ad hoc) to Level 5 (optimized/continuous improvement).
