# CWE-521

## What it is
Like accepting "1234" as a valid PIN for a bank vault, weak password requirements invite trivial compromise. CWE-521 (Weak Password Requirements) occurs when a system fails to enforce sufficient complexity, length, or other constraints on user-created passwords, making credential guessing attacks far more viable.

## Why it matters
In the 2012 LinkedIn breach, millions of SHA-1 hashed passwords were cracked within hours because the platform had no enforced complexity requirements — users had set passwords like "linkedin" and "123456." A proper policy requiring minimum 12 characters with mixed character classes would have dramatically increased cracking time even after the hash dump occurred.

## Key facts
- NIST SP 800-63B recommends **minimum 8 characters** for user-selected passwords and up to 64 characters maximum; modern guidance deprioritizes forced complexity in favor of **length and blacklisting known breached passwords**
- CWE-521 is distinct from CWE-307 (improper restriction of authentication attempts) — weak requirements are a *creation* flaw, not a *lockout* flaw
- Common enforcement failures include: no minimum length, allowing all-numeric passwords, permitting the username as the password, or accepting single-character inputs
- Password spraying attacks (e.g., trying "Summer2024!" across thousands of accounts) are directly enabled when organizations lack complexity requirements and have no MFA
- Security+ and CySA+ treat this under the **authentication hardening** domain; remediations include password filters, real-time breach database checks (HaveIBeenPwned API), and enforced change policies for default credentials
