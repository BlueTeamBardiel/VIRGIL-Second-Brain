# incident response plan

## What it is
Like a fire drill rehearsed before the building burns, an incident response plan (IRP) is a documented, pre-approved playbook that tells an organization exactly who does what, in what order, when a security incident occurs. It eliminates the paralysis of improvising under pressure by establishing roles, communication channels, and decision trees in advance.

## Why it matters
During the 2017 NotPetya attack, companies without IRPs lost days to confusion about whether to pay ransoms, who had authority to take systems offline, and how to notify customers — while competitors with mature IRPs contained damage within hours. A tested IRP is the difference between a bad week and a catastrophic quarter.

## Key facts
- The NIST SP 800-61 framework defines four IRP phases: **Preparation → Detection & Analysis → Containment/Eradication/Recovery → Post-Incident Activity**
- **Preparation** is the only phase that happens *before* an incident — all others are reactive
- The plan must identify a designated **Incident Response Team (IRT)** with clearly assigned roles: IR lead, legal counsel, communications officer, and technical analysts
- **Tabletop exercises** are low-cost simulations used to test the IRP without real-world disruption; they are distinct from live penetration tests
- After every incident, a **lessons learned report** (post-mortem) is required to update the plan — IRPs are living documents, not static PDFs
- CISA recommends IRPs include explicit **notification timelines** for regulatory bodies (e.g., GDPR's 72-hour breach disclosure requirement)
