# Social Proof

## What it is
Like a restaurant that plants fake reviews on Yelp to make empty tables look like missed opportunities, social proof is the psychological principle that people assume the actions or opinions of others are correct in ambiguous situations. In cybersecurity, attackers exploit this by fabricating legitimacy through manufactured consensus — fake testimonials, spoofed sender counts, or impersonated authority figures — to lower a target's critical defenses.

## Why it matters
Phishing emails frequently weaponize social proof by claiming "thousands of employees have already updated their credentials" or displaying logos of trusted vendors to imply widespread adoption. A BEC (Business Email Compromise) attacker might forge email threads showing that multiple executives have "already approved" a fraudulent wire transfer, pressuring the final approver to comply without independent verification. Recognizing fabricated consensus is a core defensive skill in security awareness training.

## Key facts
- Social proof is classified as a **social engineering principle** alongside authority, urgency, scarcity, familiarity, and intimidation — all testable on Security+
- Attackers use **fake sender lists, CC'd executive names, or spoofed reply chains** to simulate organizational buy-in
- Vishing campaigns sometimes reference "your coworkers have already confirmed" to bypass skepticism over the phone
- Countermeasure: **out-of-band verification** — independently confirm requests through a separate, known-good channel rather than trusting the message's own context
- Social proof is most effective under **time pressure and ambiguity** — attackers deliberately combine it with urgency to prevent rational evaluation
