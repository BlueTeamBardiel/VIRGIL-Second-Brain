# Privacy Policy

## What it is
Think of a privacy policy like the ingredient label on a food package — it tells you exactly what's collected, how it's processed, and where it goes, so you can decide whether to consume it. Precisely, a privacy policy is a legal document that discloses how an organization collects, uses, stores, shares, and protects personally identifiable information (PII) from users or customers. It establishes the contractual and regulatory baseline for data handling obligations.

## Why it matters
In 2018, Facebook faced intense scrutiny when Cambridge Analytica harvested data from 87 million users — data collection that technically fell within Facebook's privacy policy, which most users had never read. This scenario illustrates how privacy policies can be weaponized through obscure language to legitimize data exploitation, and why security professionals must audit third-party data-sharing clauses during vendor risk assessments.

## Key facts
- **GDPR (Article 13/14)** requires privacy policies to be written in "clear and plain language" and must explicitly state the legal basis for processing data
- Privacy policies are legally distinct from **privacy notices** — a policy is internal governance; a notice is the external-facing disclosure to users
- Under **CCPA**, California residents have the right to opt-out of data sale, and privacy policies must include a "Do Not Sell My Personal Information" link
- Failure to adhere to stated privacy policy terms can constitute a **deceptive trade practice**, triggering FTC enforcement action in the U.S.
- Security+ and CySA+ treat privacy policies as part of **data governance frameworks**, alongside data classification, retention policies, and data handling procedures
