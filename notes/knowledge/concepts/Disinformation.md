# Disinformation

## What it is
Like a forged map handed to an enemy general — the information looks legitimate, but it's designed to steer decisions toward disaster. Disinformation is the deliberate creation and spread of false information intended to deceive a target audience, distinguishing itself from misinformation (accidental falsehood) by the presence of **intent**. It weaponizes trust in information channels to manipulate beliefs, behaviors, or policy.

## Why it matters
During the 2016 U.S. election cycle, state-sponsored actors ran coordinated disinformation campaigns across social media platforms, creating fake personas (sockpuppet accounts) to amplify divisive content and suppress voter confidence. Security teams now monitor for influence operations as part of threat intelligence programs, recognizing that disinformation can precede or accompany technical cyberattacks — softening defenses by eroding institutional trust before the breach even happens.

## Key facts
- Disinformation is a component of **influence operations** and is tracked under the MITRE ATT&CK for Enterprise framework under the Reconnaissance and Resource Development tactics
- **Sockpuppet accounts** (fake identities) and **astroturfing** (fabricating grassroots movements) are primary delivery mechanisms
- The **CIA triad** is indirectly attacked — disinformation primarily targets **integrity** of information at the human/organizational layer
- Security+ and CySA+ exams distinguish disinformation from **misinformation** (no intent) and **malinformation** (true info weaponized harmfully) — all three fall under **information warfare**
- Countermeasures include **media literacy training**, **OSINT-based attribution**, and platform-level **behavioral analysis** to detect coordinated inauthentic behavior
