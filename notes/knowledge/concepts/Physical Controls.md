# Physical Controls

## What it is
Think of physical controls like the moat, drawbridge, and guards surrounding a medieval castle — layers of barriers that stop an attacker from ever reaching the treasure, regardless of how clever their software tricks are. Precisely defined, physical controls are security measures that restrict, detect, or deter unauthorized physical access to systems, facilities, and assets. They operate in meatspace: locks, fences, cameras, and guards rather than firewalls and encryption.

## Why it matters
In 2013, attackers physically accessed a Target HVAC vendor's network terminal to eventually pivot into the POS network — a breach that cost $162 million. A properly segmented server room with badge-only access and monitored entry logs would have significantly raised the bar for that initial foothold. Physical security failures routinely nullify every logical control a security team puts in place.

## Key facts
- Physical controls fall into three categories: **deterrent** (warning signs, fences), **preventive** (locks, mantraps/airlocks), and **detective** (cameras, motion sensors, guards)
- A **mantrap** (access control vestibule) prevents tailgating by allowing only one person through at a time between two interlocking doors — a common exam scenario
- **Faraday cages** block electromagnetic signals and are used to prevent wireless exfiltration from sensitive areas (e.g., SCIFs)
- Cable locks, equipment safes, and tamper-evident seals are compensating physical controls when stronger measures aren't feasible
- The **Defense in Depth** model treats physical security as the outermost layer — if it fails, all inner logical controls face direct exposure
