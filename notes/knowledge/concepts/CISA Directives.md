# CISA Directives

## What it is
Think of CISA Directives like mandatory safety recalls from the NTSB — when a critical flaw is discovered across federal systems, CISA issues binding orders that agencies *must* comply with by specific deadlines, no exceptions. Formally, these are legally enforceable instructions issued by the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency to federal civilian executive branch (FCEB) agencies, requiring specific security actions within defined timeframes. There are two types: Binding Operational Directives (BODs) for ongoing practices and Emergency Directives (EDs) for urgent, active-threat situations.

## Why it matters
When the SolarWinds supply chain attack was discovered in December 2020, CISA issued Emergency Directive 21-01 ordering all federal agencies to immediately disconnect affected SolarWinds Orion products — agencies had *hours* to comply, not weeks. This demonstrated how EDs function as a federal incident response mechanism, forcing coordinated action across hundreds of agencies simultaneously rather than leaving each to respond independently and inconsistently.

## Key facts
- **BODs** address systemic, long-term hygiene issues (e.g., BOD 22-01 established the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog, requiring agencies to patch listed CVEs within set windows)
- **Emergency Directives** are reserved for active, high-risk threats and require faster compliance — typically 24–96 hours for initial action
- CISA Directives apply *only* to FCEB agencies; state, local, and private sector entities are not legally bound but are strongly encouraged to follow them
- The **KEV (Known Exploited Vulnerabilities) catalog**, born from BOD 22-01, is publicly available and widely used by private organizations as a prioritization tool
- Non-compliance can result in budget restrictions and mandated reporting to the Office of Management and Budget (OMB)
