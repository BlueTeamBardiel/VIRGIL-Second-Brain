# CWE-312

## What it is
Like writing your diary entries in plain English and leaving the notebook on a park bench — anyone who finds it can read everything. CWE-312 is the weakness where sensitive data (passwords, tokens, PII, keys) is stored in cleartext, without encryption, in files, databases, logs, or memory. An attacker with read access to the storage medium gets the data with zero additional effort.

## Why it matters
In the 2013 Adobe breach, approximately 130 million passwords were stored using reversible encryption with password hints stored in plaintext — effectively cleartext storage. Attackers who exfiltrated the database could immediately use the hints to crack passwords, and the reversible scheme meant the originals were recoverable without brute force. Proper hashing with bcrypt or Argon2 would have made the stolen data computationally useless.

## Key facts
- **Cleartext ≠ encoded**: Base64-encoded passwords still count as cleartext storage — encoding is not encryption
- **Common locations**: config files, log files, SQLite databases on mobile devices, environment variables, and Windows Registry keys
- **Compliance impact**: Violates PCI-DSS Requirement 3 (protect stored cardholder data) and HIPAA's technical safeguard requirements
- **Mobile-specific risk**: iOS and Android apps frequently commit this flaw by writing tokens to SharedPreferences or NSUserDefaults without encryption
- **Detection methods**: Static analysis tools (Semgrep, Bandit), filesystem searches for known patterns (e.g., `password=`), and SAST scanning in CI/CD pipelines
