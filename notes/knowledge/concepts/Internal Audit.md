# Internal Audit

## What it is
Think of it like a mechanic inspecting your own car before a road trip — not to sell you services, but to catch problems before they strand you on the highway. An internal audit is a formal, independent evaluation conducted by an organization's own staff (or an in-house audit team) to assess whether security controls, policies, and processes are functioning as intended and complying with regulations.

## Why it matters
In 2013, Target suffered its massive breach partly because an internal audit had flagged vendor access control weaknesses months earlier — warnings that went unaddressed. A robust internal audit program would have tracked remediation of those findings, potentially preventing 40 million stolen card numbers. Internal audits create accountability loops: findings must be assigned owners and resolved within defined timeframes.

## Key facts
- Internal auditors must maintain **independence** from the teams they audit — a sysadmin cannot audit their own systems; this separation prevents self-reporting bias
- Produces a formal **audit report** containing findings, risk ratings (Critical/High/Medium/Low), and remediation recommendations with deadlines
- Differs from an **external audit**: internal audits are ongoing/proactive; external audits (e.g., PCI-DSS QSA assessments) are periodic, third-party, and often compliance-mandatory
- Follows frameworks like **ISACA's COBIT** or **IIA (Institute of Internal Auditors) standards** to structure scope and methodology
- Internal audits assess the **three lines of defense model**: business operations (1st line), risk/compliance functions (2nd line), and audit itself (3rd line)
