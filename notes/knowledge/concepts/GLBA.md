# GLBA

## What it is
Think of GLBA as a "vault construction code" for financial institutions — just as building codes mandate how banks must physically protect their vaults, GLBA mandates how they must protect your financial data. The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999) is a U.S. federal law requiring financial institutions — banks, insurers, brokers, and tax preparers — to protect the confidentiality and integrity of nonpublic personal information (NPI) they collect from customers.

## Why it matters
In 2014, JPMorgan Chase suffered a breach exposing 83 million customer records — names, addresses, phone numbers, and email addresses. GLBA's Safeguards Rule requires institutions to have written information security programs with designated coordinators, risk assessments, and vendor oversight; failure to implement these controls is precisely what regulators scrutinize after such breaches, potentially triggering FTC enforcement actions and civil penalties.

## Key facts
- **Three core rules**: Privacy Rule (requires privacy notices and opt-out rights), Safeguards Rule (mandates a written information security program), and Pretexting Protection (prohibits social engineering to obtain customer financial data)
- **Safeguards Rule updated in 2023**: Now requires MFA, encryption of data in transit and at rest, penetration testing, and a designated CISO or equivalent
- **Enforced by multiple agencies**: FTC enforces for non-bank financial institutions; OCC, FDIC, and Federal Reserve enforce for banks
- **NPI scope**: Covers any personally identifiable financial information — account numbers, credit history, Social Security numbers — not already publicly available
- **Vendor management requirement**: Institutions must contractually require third-party service providers to implement appropriate safeguards — making supply chain security a compliance obligation
