# RFC 5280

## What it is
Think of RFC 5280 as the engineering blueprint for a digital passport — it specifies exactly which fields must exist, what they mean, and how they must be validated. RFC 5280 is the Internet standard that defines the format and processing rules for X.509 v3 digital certificates and Certificate Revocation Lists (CRLs) used in PKI. It governs everything from how certificate chains are built to how validity periods and extensions must be interpreted.

## Why it matters
In 2011, the DigiNotar CA breach allowed attackers to issue fraudulent certificates for Google.com — certificates that technically *looked* valid because they conformed to RFC 5280 structure. Browsers trusted them because the chain validated correctly per the RFC's path validation rules, which is exactly why certificate transparency and stricter CA controls became critical — the format was sound, but the trust anchor was compromised.

## Key facts
- Defines **X.509 v3 certificate fields**: Version, Serial Number, Issuer, Subject, Validity (NotBefore/NotAfter), Public Key, and extensions
- Specifies **certificate path validation** — the algorithm a relying party must follow to verify a chain from end-entity certificate up to a trusted root CA
- Defines critical vs. non-critical **extensions**: if a critical extension is unrecognized, the certificate *must* be rejected
- **CRL (Certificate Revocation List)** format and distribution points (CDP) are defined here — the predecessor mechanism to OCSP
- The **Basic Constraints** extension (defined in RFC 5280) determines whether a certificate can act as a CA — misconfiguring this enabled the famous "null prefix" and intermediate CA attacks
