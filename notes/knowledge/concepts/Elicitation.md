# Elicitation

## What it is
Like a skilled bartender who learns your secrets by asking "so what do you do for work?" rather than "tell me classified information," elicitation is the art of extracting sensitive data through seemingly innocent conversation. Precisely defined: elicitation is a social engineering technique where an attacker obtains confidential information by engaging a target in casual, natural-sounding dialogue — never asking directly for what they actually want.

## Why it matters
A security researcher posing as a job recruiter calls a network engineer and, through friendly questions about "what tools you use day-to-day," learns the organization runs unpatched Cisco IOS versions with default credentials still in place. No phishing link was clicked, no malware deployed — the entire attack surface was mapped over a 15-minute phone call. This is why security awareness training explicitly teaches employees to recognize "innocent" technical questions from strangers.

## Key facts
- Elicitation exploits psychological triggers like flattery, reciprocity, and the natural human desire to appear competent and helpful
- Common pretexts include posing as recruiters, auditors, vendors, journalists, or new employees asking "naive" questions
- Differs from interrogation — the target never realizes they've been compromised; the conversation feels entirely normal
- Information harvested typically includes infrastructure details, software versions, org charts, security policies, or employee names — used in follow-on attacks
- Defense includes need-to-know communication policies, labeling information sensitivity levels, and training staff to say "I'll have someone authorized call you back"
