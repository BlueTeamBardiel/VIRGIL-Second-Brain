# Third-Party Risk Management

## What it is
Like a hospital that sterilizes surgical instruments from outside suppliers — you don't manufacture the scalpel, but you're responsible if it causes an infection. Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) is the formal process of identifying, assessing, and continuously monitoring security risks introduced by vendors, suppliers, contractors, and partners who have access to your systems, data, or infrastructure.

## Why it matters
The 2020 SolarWinds attack demonstrated catastrophic third-party risk: attackers compromised SolarWinds' build pipeline, embedding malicious code into a legitimate software update that approximately 18,000 organizations — including US federal agencies — automatically trusted and installed. No amount of internal hardening protects you when you've handed implicit trust to a vendor's update process.

## Key facts
- **Due diligence** before onboarding a vendor includes reviewing SOC 2 reports, penetration test results, and questionnaires like the Standardized Information Gathering (SIG) form
- **Right-to-audit clauses** in contracts give organizations legal authority to assess a vendor's security controls directly
- **Supply chain attacks** target the weakest link upstream — compromising a small vendor to pivot into high-value targets downstream
- **Vendor tiering** categorizes suppliers by data access and criticality (e.g., Tier 1 = access to PII/sensitive systems) to prioritize review frequency
- Under frameworks like **NIST SP 800-161** and **ISO 27036**, organizations must extend their risk management practices across the entire supply chain, not just internal assets
