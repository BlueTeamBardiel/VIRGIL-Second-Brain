# Threat Actor Attributes

## What it is
Like a detective building a criminal profile from modus operandi, tools left at the scene, and organizational backing, analysts profile threat actors using measurable characteristics. Threat actor attributes are the defining traits — motivation, capability, resources, and sophistication — used to classify and anticipate adversary behavior before, during, or after an attack.

## Why it matters
During the 2016 DNC breach, analysts attributed the attack to APT28 (Fancy Bear) not by catching anyone red-handed, but by recognizing the actor's consistent attributes: nation-state funding, custom malware (X-Agent), and geopolitical motivation. Correct attribution shaped the U.S. government's proportional response and informed future defensive posture against Russian state-sponsored operations.

## Key facts
- **Motivation types**: Financial gain (cybercriminals), espionage (nation-states), ideological disruption (hacktivists), notoriety (script kiddies), and sabotage (insider threats) — each predicts *what* an adversary will target
- **Sophistication spectrum**: Ranges from unskilled script kiddies using pre-built tools to advanced threat actors writing zero-day exploits and conducting multi-year campaigns
- **Resources/funding**: Nation-state actors have near-unlimited resources; organized crime has significant but finite budgets; hacktivists typically operate with minimal funding
- **Intent vs. capability**: A highly capable actor with low intent (e.g., ethical nation-state ally) is lower risk than a low-capability actor with destructive intent (e.g., motivated insider)
- **Attribution challenge**: Threat actors use false flag techniques — deliberately mimicking another actor's TTPs — making confident attribution extremely difficult and legally consequential
