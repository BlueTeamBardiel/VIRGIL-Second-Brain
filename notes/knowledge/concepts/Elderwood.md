# Elderwood

## What it is
Like a master locksmith who builds a private collection of skeleton keys and never sells them publicly, Elderwood is an advanced persistent threat (APT) platform and operation attributed to a Chinese state-sponsored group that hoards zero-day exploits for highly targeted attacks. Specifically, it refers to both the threat actor (also tracked as APT1-adjacent groups, particularly tied to Beijing) and the sophisticated supply-chain and watering hole attack infrastructure they developed, first exposed by Symantec in 2012.

## Why it matters
The Elderwood group pioneered the "zero-day exploit assembly line" — they burned through an unprecedented number of zero-days (over 8 in a single year) targeting defense contractors and human rights organizations. In one documented campaign, they compromised a defense supply-chain vendor's website as a watering hole, so that tier-1 defense contractors visiting it would automatically receive exploits — reaching hard targets through soft intermediaries.

## Key facts
- First publicly documented by Symantec in 2012; the name "Elderwood" comes from a source-code variable found in the malware
- Group demonstrated an extraordinary zero-day stockpile, exploiting vulnerabilities in Internet Explorer, Adobe Flash, and Adobe Reader in rapid succession
- Primary attack vectors: **watering hole attacks** and **spear phishing** targeting defense industrial base (DIB) and NGO sectors
- Attributed to a threat actor group operating out of China, suspected ties to the People's Liberation Army (PLA)
- Represents an early, prominent example of **supply chain compromise** as an attack strategy, predating the SolarWinds model by nearly a decade
