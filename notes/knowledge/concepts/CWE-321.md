# CWE-321

## What it is
Imagine a locksmith who gives every house in the city the same master key, then publishes that key in the instruction manual. CWE-321 is the use of a hard-coded cryptographic key — a secret key embedded directly in source code or binaries that cannot be changed without modifying and redeploying the application.

## Why it matters
In 2012, researchers discovered that hundreds of home routers from multiple vendors shared identical private SSH and TLS keys hard-coded into their firmware. An attacker who extracted the key from one device's firmware could impersonate or decrypt traffic from *any* device running that firmware — affecting millions of routers simultaneously. The fix required a full firmware update that most users never applied.

## Key facts
- Hard-coded keys are trivially extractable using `strings`, `binwalk`, or reverse engineering tools like Ghidra — no expertise required
- This weakness violates the principle of **key management separation**: secrets must live outside the codebase in secure vaults or HSMs
- NIST SP 800-57 explicitly requires that cryptographic keys have defined lifecycles and rotation mechanisms — impossible with hard-coded keys
- CWE-321 is a child of CWE-798 (Use of Hard-coded Credentials), which also covers passwords; the distinction is cryptographic key material specifically
- Static analysis tools (Semgrep, Bandit, Checkmarx) can detect common patterns of hard-coded key assignments during CI/CD pipeline scanning
