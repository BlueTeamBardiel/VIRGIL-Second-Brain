# APT5

## What it is  
Imagine a spy that leaves a unique, invisible fingerprint on every trail it cuts—APT5 is that spy in cyberspace, a North‑Korean threat actor (also known as OceanLotus) that crafts custom malware, weaponizes legitimate tools, and uses spear‑phishing to infiltrate networks. Technically, it is a persistent advanced persistent threat (APT) group focused on military and defense contractors, employing stealthy, tailored payloads and lateral movement to exfiltrate sensitive data.

## Why it matters  
In 2021, APT5 compromised a U.S. defense contractor’s network, using a spear‑phishing email with a malicious PDF. The payload installed a backdoor that allowed attackers to move laterally, harvest credentials, and exfiltrate classified documents to secure command‑and‑control servers. This incident highlighted the need for robust email filtering, continuous monitoring, and zero‑trust network segmentation to counter such sophisticated, tailored attacks.

## Key facts  
- Operated by North Korea, active since ~2013.  
- Uses custom malware plus legitimate tools (e.g., PowerShell, WMI).  
- Relies heavily on spear‑phishing with document attachments.  
- Conducts persistent, long‑term espionage against defense and aerospace sectors.  
- Employs encrypted C2 channels and obfuscated code to evade detection.
