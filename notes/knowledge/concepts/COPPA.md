# COPPA

## What it is
Think of it as a locked door on the internet with a sign reading "You must be *this tall* (13 years old) to enter without parental consent." The Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) is a U.S. federal law (1998, enforced by the FTC) that restricts how websites and online services can collect, use, or disclose personal information from children under 13. It places legal obligations on operators to obtain verifiable parental consent before gathering any data from young users.

## Why it matters
In 2019, TikTok (then Musical.ly) was fined $5.7 million by the FTC — the largest COPPA penalty at the time — for illegally collecting names, email addresses, and locations from children without parental consent. This case illustrates that COPPA compliance isn't just an ethics checkbox; failure to implement proper age-verification mechanisms and data minimization controls creates direct legal and financial liability for organizations.

## Key facts
- COPPA applies to operators of websites or online services **directed at children**, and to general-audience sites that have **actual knowledge** they are collecting data from users under 13
- Requires **verifiable parental consent** (VPC) before collecting personal information — a checkbox alone does not satisfy this requirement
- Organizations must provide a **clear, accessible privacy policy** specifically describing data practices for children
- Parents have the right to **review, delete, and refuse further collection** of their child's personal data
- The FTC can impose civil penalties of up to **$51,744 per violation per day** (amount adjusted periodically for inflation)
- COPPA compliance involves **data minimization** — collect only what is strictly necessary for the service
