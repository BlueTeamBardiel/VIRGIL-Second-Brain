# T1110.003

## What it is
Like a locksmith who knows most people reuse the same 10 popular key cuts, an attacker takes a single common password and sprays it across hundreds of accounts rather than hammering one account repeatedly. Password Spraying is a brute-force variant where one or a few commonly used passwords (e.g., "Winter2024!") are attempted against many usernames, deliberately staying below account lockout thresholds.

## Why it matters
In the 2019–2020 SolarWinds-related compromises, threat actors used password spraying against Microsoft 365 and Azure AD tenants to harvest valid credentials without triggering lockout policies. Because each account only receives one or two attempts, traditional lockout defenses are completely blind to the attack — only anomaly detection across the entire user population reveals the pattern.

## Key facts
- **Lockout evasion by design**: Attackers attempt each password once per account, staying under the typical 3–5 attempt lockout threshold
- **High-value targets**: Cloud identity providers (Azure AD, Okta, Google Workspace) are primary targets because a single valid credential can access many services
- **Detection signature**: Look for a spike in authentication failures distributed across many accounts at the same timestamp — the inverse pattern of traditional brute force
- **MITRE tactic**: Falls under **Credential Access (TA0006)**, sub-technique of Brute Force (T1110)
- **Mitigation priority**: Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is the most effective single control; even valid sprayed credentials become useless without the second factor
