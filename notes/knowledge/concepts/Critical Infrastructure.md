# Critical Infrastructure

## What it is
Think of critical infrastructure like the bones of a city — invisible until broken, but everything collapses without them. Formally, critical infrastructure refers to the systems and assets so vital to national security, public health, safety, or economic stability that their disruption would have a debilitating effect on society. CISA identifies 16 sectors, including energy, water, transportation, and healthcare.

## Why it matters
In 2021, the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack (DarkSide group) forced a shutdown of 5,500 miles of fuel pipeline serving the U.S. East Coast, causing fuel shortages across multiple states within days. This demonstrated that a single ransomware intrusion against an IT network could cascade into physical, real-world consequences affecting millions of people — a textbook example of why OT/ICS environments demand hardened, segmented defenses.

## Key facts
- CISA defines **16 critical infrastructure sectors**; Energy, Water, and Healthcare are most frequently targeted and tested
- Critical infrastructure often runs **Operational Technology (OT)** systems (SCADA, ICS) that were designed for availability over security, making patching difficult
- **NERC CIP** (North American Electric Reliability Corporation Critical Infrastructure Protection) is the compliance framework governing the energy sector
- The **Purdue Model** is a reference architecture used to segment IT and OT networks in industrial control environments
- Presidential Policy Directive 21 (**PPD-21**) establishes U.S. policy for critical infrastructure security and resilience, assigning sector-specific agencies
