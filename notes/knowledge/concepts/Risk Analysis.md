# Risk Analysis

## What it is
Like a chess player calculating which pieces are most likely to be captured and how badly losing each one would hurt, risk analysis is the process of identifying threats, estimating the probability they occur, and quantifying the damage if they do. It produces a prioritized picture of organizational exposure so resources go where they matter most. Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Asset Value.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax failed to patch a known Apache Struts vulnerability (CVE-2017-5638) for months — a proper risk analysis would have flagged that an unpatched internet-facing server holding 147 million SSNs represented catastrophic residual risk. A quantitative risk analysis would have calculated the Annual Loss Expectancy (ALE) in the hundreds of millions, likely forcing immediate remediation action.

## Key facts
- **Quantitative analysis** uses numbers: Asset Value (AV) × Exposure Factor (EF) = Single Loss Expectancy (SLE); SLE × Annual Rate of Occurrence (ARO) = Annual Loss Expectancy (ALE)
- **Qualitative analysis** uses labels (High/Medium/Low) and expert judgment — faster but less precise; often used when hard data is unavailable
- **Risk appetite** is how much risk leadership *accepts*; **risk tolerance** is the operational variance around that threshold — these are distinct terms that appear on exams
- Four risk responses: **Avoid** (stop the activity), **Transfer** (insurance/contract), **Mitigate** (reduce likelihood/impact), **Accept** (document and monitor)
- Residual risk is what remains *after* controls are applied; inherent risk is exposure *before* any controls — both must be documented
