# NDA

## What it is
Like a magician's assistant swearing never to reveal how the tricks work, an NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement) is a legally binding contract that prohibits one or more parties from sharing specified confidential information with unauthorized third parties. It establishes legal liability for breaches of confidentiality before technical controls can even be deployed.

## Why it matters
Before a penetration tester begins an engagement, they sign an NDA with the client organization — without it, discovered vulnerabilities, network diagrams, and credential data could be legally shared or sold with no recourse. If a tester later publishes findings about a zero-day discovered during that engagement, the NDA becomes the legal mechanism for enforcement and damages recovery.

## Key facts
- NDAs are a **personnel security control** and appear in Security+ under administrative/managerial control categories
- They can be **unilateral** (one party agrees to protect the other's information) or **mutual/bilateral** (both parties protect each other's secrets)
- NDAs are standard during **onboarding** (protecting company IP from new hires) and **offboarding** (preventing departing employees from sharing trade secrets)
- A violated NDA does not recover leaked data — it provides **legal remedy after the fact**, making it a detective/corrective control, not preventive for data loss itself
- Common in **third-party and vendor agreements** (supply chain security), requiring partners to protect shared sensitive data — failure here contributed to breaches like the Target attack via HVAC vendor access
