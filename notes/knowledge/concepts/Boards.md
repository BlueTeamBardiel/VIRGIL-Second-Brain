# Boards

## What it is
Like a ship's bridge where the captain and officers oversee every system across the vessel, a board of directors sits at the top of an organization's governance structure, setting strategic direction and accountability for risk — including cybersecurity risk. Precisely, a board is the senior governing body responsible for oversight of organizational policy, risk appetite, and executive accountability, and in modern enterprises this explicitly includes information security posture.

## Why it matters
In the 2017 Equifax breach, the board faced intense scrutiny for failing to enforce adequate cybersecurity oversight despite known vulnerabilities — ultimately costing the CEO his position and the company $575 million in FTC settlements. This incident became the canonical example of why boards must receive regular, meaningful security briefings rather than treating cybersecurity as purely a technical concern. The SEC now requires public companies to disclose board-level cybersecurity expertise and oversight processes.

## Key facts
- The **board sets risk appetite** — the threshold of acceptable risk the organization will tolerate — which directly constrains security team decisions and budgets
- **SEC rules (2023)** mandate that public companies disclose material cybersecurity incidents within 4 business days and annually describe board oversight of cyber risks
- Boards are distinct from management: the **CISO reports up through management** (often to CIO or CEO), who then reports security posture *to* the board
- **Governance frameworks** like NIST CSF and ISO 27001 explicitly require board-level accountability and sponsorship for security programs to be effective
- On Security+/CySA+ exams, boards appear in **governance, risk, and compliance (GRC)** questions — know that boards own governance while management owns operations
