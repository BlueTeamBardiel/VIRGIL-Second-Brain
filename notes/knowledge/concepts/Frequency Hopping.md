# Frequency Hopping

## What it is
Like a spy who changes meeting locations every five minutes according to a pre-agreed schedule, frequency hopping rapidly switches a radio signal across many different frequencies in a pseudo-random sequence known only to the sender and receiver. Formally, it is a spread-spectrum technique where both transmitter and receiver synchronize to the same pseudo-random frequency-hopping pattern, making the signal appear as brief bursts of noise to anyone not holding the "schedule."

## Why it matters
Bluetooth uses frequency hopping spread spectrum (FHSS), cycling through 79 channels in the 2.4 GHz band up to 1,600 times per second. An attacker attempting a jamming or eavesdropping attack against a static-frequency device can simply blast that one channel, but against FHSS they must jam the entire band simultaneously — a far more expensive and detectable effort. This is why Bluetooth retains reasonable resilience in congested RF environments despite operating on unlicensed spectrum.

## Key facts
- **FHSS vs. DSSS**: Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum hops channels; Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum chews the signal across a wide band simultaneously — both resist interference but differently.
- **Bluetooth Classic** uses FHSS across 79 × 1 MHz channels; **Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE)** uses only 40 channels with a different hopping algorithm.
- The hopping sequence is generated by a **pseudo-random number generator (PRNG)** seeded with a shared secret — security depends entirely on keeping that seed secret.
- FHSS provides **LPI/LPD** (Low Probability of Intercept/Detection) properties, making it valuable in military and covert communications.
- Wi-Fi originally supported FHSS in the **IEEE 802.11-1997** standard before DSSS and OFDM replaced it for higher throughput needs.
