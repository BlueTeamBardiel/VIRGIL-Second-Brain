# ISO 27001

## What it is
Think of it like a building code for information security — just as a contractor must follow structural standards before a city inspector signs off, ISO 27001 defines the requirements an organization must meet before an accredited auditor certifies their Information Security Management System (ISMS). It is an international standard published by the International Organization for Standardization that specifies a systematic, risk-based framework for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving how an organization manages information security risks.

## Why it matters
When a healthcare SaaS vendor bids on a NHS contract, the procurement team demands proof of ISO 27001 certification rather than accepting a self-written security policy document. The certification demonstrates that an independent auditor has verified the vendor actually conducts formal risk assessments, enforces access controls, and runs a change management process — reducing the buyer's vendor risk without requiring a full on-site audit of their own.

## Key facts
- ISO 27001 is **certifiable** — organizations undergo a two-stage audit by an accredited Certification Body (CB); this distinguishes it from ISO 27002, which is a non-certifiable code of practice providing implementation guidance
- The standard uses the **Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)** cycle to drive continual improvement of the ISMS
- **Annex A** contains 93 controls (as of the 2022 revision) organized into 4 themes: Organizational, People, Physical, and Technological
- Organizations must produce a **Statement of Applicability (SoA)** documenting which Annex A controls apply, which are excluded, and the justification for each decision
- Certification must be **renewed every 3 years**, with mandatory surveillance audits in years 1 and 2 to verify ongoing compliance
