# T1499

## What it is
Like a mob flooding a restaurant with fake reservations so real customers can't get a table, Endpoint Denial of Service involves overwhelming a specific system's resources — CPU, memory, disk, or network stack — until legitimate users can no longer reach it. Unlike volumetric network floods, T1499 targets the *host itself*, exhausting application-layer or OS-level resources rather than saturating a pipe.

## Why it matters
During the 2016 Mirai botnet campaigns, attackers didn't just flood ISP infrastructure — they also sent application-layer HTTP floods that exhausted web server thread pools on individual endpoints, taking down sites even when raw bandwidth wasn't fully saturated. Defenders who only monitored network throughput missed the attack because the servers were dying from connection exhaustion, not packet volume.

## Key facts
- T1499 has four sub-techniques: OS Exhaustion Flood (T1499.001), Service Exhaustion Flood (T1499.002), Application Exhaustion Flood (T1499.003), and Application or System Exploitation (T1499.004)
- T1499.002 includes SYN floods and SSL renegotiation attacks that exhaust connection state tables on the target host
- T1499.004 is distinct: it uses *vulnerabilities* (not just volume) to crash a service — a logic bomb or memory corruption bug that forces a crash counts here
- Availability is the CIA triad component targeted — this maps directly to Denial of Service impact on the Security+ exam
- Detection relies on monitoring for resource exhaustion patterns: CPU spikes, connection queue saturation, and process crash logs — not just firewall-level traffic anomalies
