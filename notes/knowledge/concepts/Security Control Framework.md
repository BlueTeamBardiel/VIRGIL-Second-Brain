# Security Control Framework

## What it is
Think of it like a building code for skyscrapers — architects don't invent structural rules from scratch; they follow standardized specifications that guarantee safety across the industry. A security control framework is a structured set of guidelines, best practices, and standards that organizations use to systematically select, implement, and measure security controls. It provides a common language and repeatable process for managing risk rather than improvising defenses ad hoc.

## Why it matters
When Target suffered its 2013 breach (41 million cards compromised), investigators found the company had passed a PCI-DSS audit just months earlier — revealing that checkbox compliance within a framework doesn't automatically equal security. However, organizations using NIST CSF alongside PCI-DSS as a risk-based framework (not just a compliance checklist) are better positioned to detect lateral movement and third-party vendor risks, exactly the attack vectors Target missed.

## Key facts
- **NIST CSF** organizes controls into five functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover — the exam loves this order
- **NIST SP 800-53** provides a catalog of specific controls mapped to three impact levels: Low, Moderate, High
- **ISO/IEC 27001** is the international standard; achieving certification requires a formal third-party audit of the ISMS
- **CIS Controls** (18 controls) are prioritized by attack prevalence — Controls 1–6 are "Implementation Group 1" basics that stop ~85% of common attacks
- Frameworks differ from **regulations**: NIST CSF is voluntary guidance; HIPAA and PCI-DSS carry legal/financial penalties for non-compliance
