# T1110.004

## What it is
Like a thief who steals a master key ring instead of picking individual locks, credential stuffing skips the guessing game entirely. It is the automated injection of previously breached username/password pairs against a target service, exploiting the reality that users reuse credentials across multiple sites. The attacker doesn't crack anything — they simply *try known valid credentials* harvested from prior breaches elsewhere.

## Why it matters
After the 2013 Adobe breach exposed ~153 million credentials, attackers loaded those combos into tools like Sentry MBA and hammered banking and email services — successfully accessing accounts because users had reused the same passwords. A defender watching these login attempts would see geographically dispersed IPs, high request volume, and a distinctive pattern: near-normal success rates (1–3%) versus near-zero rates in true brute-force attacks.

## Key facts
- **Distinguishing characteristic**: Uses *known* credential pairs from breach databases, NOT random guesses — this separates it from brute force (T1110.001)
- **Success rate**: Typically 0.1%–3% hit rate, but against large credential lists this yields thousands of valid logins
- **Tools used**: Sentry MBA, OpenBullet, and SNIPR are purpose-built for high-velocity stuffing attacks
- **Detection signal**: Unusual geographic login diversity, high volume of failed logins followed by successful ones, and login attempts outside normal hours
- **Mitigations**: Multi-factor authentication (MFA) is the most effective control — stolen passwords alone cannot complete login; also consider CAPTCHA, rate limiting, and breach-credential watchlists (e.g., HaveIBeenPwned API integration)
