# Continuity Planning

## What it is
Like a jazz musician who knows every standard cold so the band keeps playing even when the bassist walks off stage mid-set — continuity planning is the organizational discipline of ensuring critical business functions survive disruption. It is the overarching framework (documented in a Business Continuity Plan, or BCP) that identifies essential processes, defines recovery priorities, and establishes procedures to maintain operations during and after an incident.

## Why it matters
In 2021, a ransomware attack on Colonial Pipeline forced the company to shut down 5,500 miles of fuel pipeline — not because the OT systems were directly crippled, but because billing systems were compromised and operators lacked a continuity plan to run operations manually. Organizations with mature BCPs pre-identify manual workarounds, alternate suppliers, and communication trees so that one compromised system doesn't collapse the entire operation.

## Key facts
- **BCP vs. DRP**: Business Continuity Planning covers the *entire organization* staying operational; Disaster Recovery Planning (DRP) focuses specifically on restoring *IT systems and data* after failure.
- **BIA first**: A Business Impact Analysis (BIA) must precede the BCP — it identifies critical functions, dependencies, and the financial/operational cost of downtime.
- **RTO and RPO**: Recovery Time Objective (how long you can be down) and Recovery Point Objective (how much data loss is acceptable) are the two core metrics that drive continuity decisions.
- **Tabletop exercises** test the BCP without operational disruption; full **failover tests** validate it under realistic conditions.
- NIST SP 800-34 is the authoritative federal guide for IT Contingency Planning, covering BCPs, DRPs, and COOPs (Continuity of Operations Plans).
