# FISMA

## What it is
Think of FISMA as the building code for federal IT systems — just as a building inspector ensures every federal structure meets safety standards before occupancy, FISMA ensures every federal information system meets security standards before handling government data. The Federal Information Security Modernization Act (2014 update to the 2002 original) requires federal agencies to develop, document, and implement agency-wide programs to secure their information systems, with NIST providing the technical framework.

## Why it matters
In 2015, the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) breach exposed records of 21.5 million federal employees — a catastrophic failure partly rooted in poor FISMA compliance, including unpatched systems and inadequate access controls that auditors had flagged but agencies failed to remediate. This breach illustrates why FISMA's continuous monitoring requirement exists: annual checkbox compliance isn't enough; agencies must maintain real-time awareness of system security posture.

## Key facts
- FISMA requires agencies to categorize systems using **FIPS 199** (Low/Moderate/High impact) and implement controls from **NIST SP 800-53**
- The **Authorization to Operate (ATO)** is FISMA's gate mechanism — systems must receive formal authorization from an authorizing official before processing federal data
- **OMB** oversees FISMA compliance and receives annual agency reports; **CISA** provides operational support and threat intelligence
- FISMA applies to federal agencies AND contractors/vendors who operate systems on behalf of the government
- The **Risk Management Framework (RMF)** — defined in NIST SP 800-37 — is the six-step process agencies follow to achieve and maintain FISMA compliance
