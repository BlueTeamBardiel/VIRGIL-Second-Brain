# DarkSide

## What it is
Think of DarkSide like a criminal franchise: a central organization provides the ransomware toolkit and infrastructure, then recruits independent contractors to do the actual break-ins and split the profits. DarkSide is a Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) group that emerged in August 2020, operating an affiliate model where developers lease their malware to partners in exchange for a percentage of ransom payments.

## Why it matters
In May 2021, DarkSide affiliates attacked Colonial Pipeline, the operator supplying roughly 45% of the US East Coast's fuel. The attack forced a six-day pipeline shutdown, triggered a national emergency declaration, caused fuel shortages across multiple states, and resulted in Colonial paying approximately $4.4 million in Bitcoin ransom — demonstrating how ransomware against operational technology (OT) and critical infrastructure can cascade into physical-world consequences.

## Key facts
- DarkSide operated a **double extortion** model: encrypting data AND threatening to publish stolen data on their leak site if ransom was unpaid
- The group maintained a **public PR presence**, claiming to avoid hospitals, schools, and nonprofits — positioning themselves as "ethical criminals"
- DarkSide affiliates gained initial access to Colonial Pipeline through a **compromised VPN account** that lacked multi-factor authentication (MFA)
- The U.S. government recovered approximately **$2.3 million** of the ransom by seizing a Bitcoin wallet, illustrating that cryptocurrency payments are NOT fully anonymous or unrecoverable
- DarkSide announced shutdown shortly after the Colonial attack, likely due to law enforcement pressure and loss of infrastructure — a common pattern after high-profile incidents
