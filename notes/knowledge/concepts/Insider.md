# Insider

## What it is
Like a bank employee who already has the vault combination — no lockpicking required — an insider is any person who has *legitimate authorized access* to an organization's systems, networks, or data and who poses a security risk through misuse of that access. This includes current employees, contractors, business partners, and former employees whose credentials weren't revoked.

## Why it matters
In 2022, a Cisco engineer deleted 456 virtual machines after resigning, causing $2.4 million in damages — a textbook malicious insider incident. Detection was difficult precisely because his actions initially *looked like normal administrative work*, illustrating why behavioral analytics and least privilege enforcement are critical countermeasures.

## Key facts
- **Three insider categories**: Malicious (intentional harm), Negligent (accidental misuse, e.g., clicking phishing links), and Compromised (legitimate credentials stolen by an outsider)
- Negligent insiders account for approximately **62% of insider incidents** according to Ponemon Institute research — making human error the dominant insider threat vector
- Key detection controls include **User and Entity Behavior Analytics (UEBA)**, Data Loss Prevention (DLP), and audit log review
- The **principle of least privilege** is the primary preventive control — users should have only the minimum access required for their job function
- **Separation of duties** limits any single insider's ability to complete a damaging action alone (e.g., requiring two people to authorize large financial transfers)
- Former employees with *unrevoked access* are classified as insider threats; **offboarding procedures** (immediate credential termination) are a critical mitigation
