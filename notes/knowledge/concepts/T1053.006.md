# T1053.006

## What it is
Like a building janitor who programs the elevator to run automatically every night at 2am without needing to press any buttons, systemd timers let Linux processes schedule themselves to execute at precise intervals. Specifically, T1053.006 describes adversaries abusing systemd's `.timer` unit files to persistently execute malicious payloads on a schedule, bypassing traditional cron-based monitoring.

## Why it matters
In the 2021 Sysdig threat research findings, attackers deploying cryptominers on compromised Linux containers used systemd timers to re-launch their mining process every 15 minutes, ensuring the miner would restart even if defenders killed the process manually. Security teams focused only on cron jobs missed the persistence entirely because they hadn't extended their monitoring to systemd unit directories.

## Key facts
- Systemd timer units (`.timer` files) are paired with service units (`.service` files); the timer triggers the service, so analysts must examine **both** to understand what executes
- Malicious timers are typically dropped into `/etc/systemd/system/`, `~/.config/systemd/user/`, or `/lib/systemd/system/` for persistence across reboots
- Running `systemctl list-timers --all` reveals all active and inactive timers — a critical triage command during Linux incident response
- Unlike cron, systemd timers support **calendar events and monotonic timers** (e.g., run 30 minutes after boot), making detection harder with time-based correlation rules
- Detection focus: audit file creation events in systemd unit directories using auditd rules or EDR file integrity monitoring, and alert on unexpected `systemctl enable` commands
