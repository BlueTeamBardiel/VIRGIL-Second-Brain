# ISO 22301

## What it is
Think of ISO 22301 as a fire drill program for an entire organization — not just knowing where the exits are, but rehearsing who calls whom, who has the keys, and how fast the building gets back to work. Precisely, it is the international standard for **Business Continuity Management Systems (BCMS)**, providing a framework for organizations to anticipate, prepare for, respond to, and recover from disruptive incidents. It specifies requirements to plan, establish, implement, operate, monitor, review, maintain, and continually improve a documented BCMS.

## Why it matters
When a ransomware attack hit Maersk in 2017 (NotPetya), the shipping giant lost ~$300 million and had to reinstall 45,000 PCs because their continuity planning had critical gaps. Organizations certified against ISO 22301 are required to maintain tested recovery procedures, defined Recovery Time Objectives (RTOs) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPOs), and documented crisis communication plans — exactly the infrastructure that limits blast radius during attacks like NotPetya.

## Key facts
- ISO 22301:2019 is the current version, replacing ISO 22301:2012, aligned with the **Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)** cycle
- Requires organizations to define and document **RTOs** (how quickly systems must recover) and **RPOs** (maximum acceptable data loss)
- Certification involves third-party audits — it is a *certifiable* standard, unlike many guidelines
- Closely related to **NIST SP 800-34** (Contingency Planning Guide) but is internationally recognized and industry-agnostic
- Requires a **Business Impact Analysis (BIA)** as a foundational step — identifying critical functions and their dependencies before any recovery plan is written
