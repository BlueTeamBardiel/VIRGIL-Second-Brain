# manage_agendas

## What it is
Like a city council meeting where the chairperson controls which topics get discussed and in what order, managing agendas in security contexts refers to the practice of controlling the scope, priority, and sequencing of security tasks, meetings, and incident response activities to ensure critical items receive proper attention. It encompasses the governance of security program activities, from vulnerability triage queues to change advisory board (CAB) reviews.

## Why it matters
During a ransomware incident, a disorganized response team without a managed agenda will thrash between containment, forensics, and communications simultaneously — achieving none effectively. A structured incident command agenda ensures containment actions are completed before evidence collection begins, preventing evidence destruction while also stopping spread. Attackers exploit disorganized responses by creating noise (fake alerts) to knock high-priority items off the response agenda.

## Key facts
- Change Advisory Board (CAB) agendas are a formal security control — unauthorized changes that bypass CAB review are a leading cause of outages and security misconfigurations
- NIST SP 800-61 (Incident Response) implicitly requires agenda management through its defined phases: Preparation → Detection → Containment → Eradication → Recovery
- Risk register reviews and vulnerability prioritization meetings require managed agendas to ensure CVSS-critical items are addressed before lower-severity findings
- In security governance frameworks (ISO 27001), documented meeting agendas and minutes are auditable evidence of due diligence
- Social engineering attacks sometimes target meeting agendas — inserting malicious agenda items or impersonating executives to redirect security priorities (a form of pretexting)
