# Legal Holds

## What it is
Like a museum curator suddenly roping off a gallery and declaring "nothing moves until investigators are done," a legal hold is a formal directive to suspend normal data destruction or modification policies. Precisely: a legal hold (also called a litigation hold) is a documented process requiring an organization to preserve all relevant electronically stored information (ESI), emails, logs, and records when litigation, investigation, or audit is reasonably anticipated.

## Why it matters
During a data breach investigation, a company's automated log rotation policy deleted 30 days of firewall logs before a legal hold was issued — destroying critical evidence of how attackers moved laterally. The failure to implement a timely hold resulted in spoliation sanctions, meaning the court assumed the missing evidence was unfavorable to the company, costing them the case before a single argument was made.

## Key facts
- Legal holds **override** standard retention and destruction schedules — even scheduled backups and routine purges must stop for covered data
- Triggered by **reasonable anticipation** of litigation, not just when a lawsuit is filed — acting too late can constitute spoliation of evidence
- Custodians (employees holding relevant data) must receive **written notification** of their preservation obligations
- Failure to comply can result in **spoliation sanctions**, adverse inference instructions, or monetary penalties from courts
- Legal holds are distinct from **retention policies** — retention governs normal data lifecycle; holds are exceptional freezes that supersede retention rules
- Chain of custody documentation must be maintained throughout the hold to ensure evidence remains **forensically sound and admissible**
