# Corrective controls

## What it is
Like a doctor setting a broken bone after a fall — not preventing the injury, not merely documenting it, but actively restoring the body to a functional state — corrective controls respond to a security incident by reducing damage and returning systems to normal operation. Precisely defined: corrective controls are security measures activated *after* a threat has been detected to limit impact, remediate harm, and restore business functionality.

## Why it matters
During the 2017 WannaCry ransomware outbreak, organizations that had corrective controls in place — specifically tested backup restoration procedures and incident response playbooks — recovered in hours rather than weeks. Those without them paid ransoms or rebuilt from scratch. The corrective control (offline, verified backups) didn't stop the infection but made the difference between a bad day and a catastrophic loss.

## Key facts
- Corrective controls are one of three primary control *functions* alongside **preventive** and **detective** controls (per NIST and CompTIA frameworks)
- Examples include: patch deployment after exploitation, system reimaging, revoking compromised credentials, and activating backup systems
- Corrective controls map to the **Respond** and **Recover** functions of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework
- An **IPS** (Intrusion Prevention System) is considered both detective *and* corrective — it detects and actively blocks or terminates malicious sessions in real time
- On Security+/CySA+ exams, distinguish corrective from **compensating controls**: corrective *fixes* the problem; compensating *substitutes* for a missing primary control
