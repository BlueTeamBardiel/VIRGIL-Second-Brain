# data governance

## What it is
Think of data governance like a city's zoning laws — it defines who can build what, where, and under what conditions, so that a hospital doesn't end up next to a nightclub. Data governance is the formal framework of policies, roles, and processes that control how an organization collects, stores, accesses, shares, and disposes of data throughout its lifecycle. It assigns accountability for data quality, privacy, and compliance — establishing who *owns* the data versus who merely *uses* it.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax exposed 147 million records partly because sensitive data was stored in systems that lacked proper access controls and retention limits — failures of governance, not just patching. A mature data governance program would have enforced data minimization (don't keep SSNs longer than needed) and role-based access, reducing the blast radius of the breach. Without governance, technical controls often protect the wrong things or miss critical assets entirely.

## Key facts
- **Data steward vs. data owner**: The data owner (usually a business executive) has accountability; the data steward manages day-to-day quality and access — both roles are distinct and exam-testable.
- **Data classification** is the foundation of governance: you cannot protect what you haven't categorized (Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted).
- **Retention and destruction policies** are governance artifacts — NIST 800-88 provides media sanitization standards aligned with these policies.
- **GDPR and CCPA** are legal drivers of data governance, mandating documented data inventories, lawful processing bases, and breach notification timelines.
- **Data sovereignty** — the principle that data is subject to the laws of the country where it resides — is a governance constraint critical for cloud deployments.
