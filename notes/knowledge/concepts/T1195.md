# T1195

## What it is
Like poisoning a city's water supply at the reservoir instead of individual taps, supply chain compromise attacks the trusted source that distributes software or hardware to thousands of downstream targets. Precisely, T1195 is a MITRE ATT&CK technique where adversaries infiltrate the build pipeline, update mechanism, or hardware manufacturing process of a trusted vendor to deliver malicious code to victims who never question a legitimate-looking update or product.

## Why it matters
In the 2020 SolarWinds attack, threat actors (APT29/Cozy Bear) injected the SUNBURST backdoor into SolarWinds' Orion build process, pushing it to ~18,000 organizations via a signed, legitimate software update. This bypassed perimeter defenses entirely because the malicious code arrived with a valid digital certificate from a trusted vendor — defenders had no reason to block it.

## Key facts
- T1195 has three sub-techniques: **T1195.001** (compromise software dependencies/libraries), **T1195.002** (compromise software supply chain), and **T1195.003** (compromise hardware supply chain)
- Attackers target build servers, CI/CD pipelines, and code-signing infrastructure — not the end-user's environment
- Detection relies heavily on **software bill of materials (SBOM)** analysis, build integrity verification, and behavioral monitoring of trusted applications post-install
- Supply chain attacks are classified as **Initial Access** in ATT&CK, meaning they establish the first foothold before any lateral movement
- Defense-in-depth controls like **zero-trust architecture** and **allowlisting with hash verification** reduce but do not eliminate risk, since legitimate signed binaries may still be malicious
