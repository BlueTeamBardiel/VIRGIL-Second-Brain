# social engineering

## What it is
Like a con artist wearing a UPS uniform to walk right past a locked door, social engineering bypasses technical controls entirely by manipulating the *person* holding the keys. It is the art of exploiting human psychology — trust, fear, urgency, or authority — to trick individuals into revealing credentials, granting access, or performing actions that compromise security.

## Why it matters
In the 2011 RSA SecurID breach, attackers sent a phishing email with a spreadsheet titled "2011 Recruitment Plan" to low-level employees. One employee retrieved it from spam, opened it, and an embedded Flash exploit installed a backdoor — ultimately compromising the two-factor authentication tokens protecting U.S. defense contractors. The technical perimeter was irrelevant; the human was the attack surface.

## Key facts
- **Phishing** is the most common vector; spear phishing targets specific individuals using personalized context to increase believability
- **Pretexting** involves fabricating a scenario (e.g., impersonating IT support or a vendor) to establish false trust before making a malicious request
- **Vishing** (voice phishing) and **smishing** (SMS phishing) are phone/text variants tested directly on Security+
- **Tailgating/piggybacking** is the physical variant — following an authorized person through a secured door without using credentials
- **Authority, urgency, scarcity, and social proof** are the six Cialdini influence principles most exploited; urgency ("your account will be locked in 10 minutes") is the most effective disruptor of rational thinking
- Security awareness training is the primary countermeasure; simulated phishing campaigns measure and reduce susceptibility rates over time
