# CIP

## What it is
Think of CIP like the building codes and inspectors that protect a city's water supply, power grid, and bridges — except instead of concrete and pipes, it governs the cybersecurity rules protecting America's most critical systems. The **Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP)** standards, developed by NERC (North American Electric Reliability Corporation), are a set of mandatory regulatory requirements designed to secure the bulk electric system from cyber and physical threats.

## Why it matters
In 2021, attackers breached the water treatment facility in Oldsmar, Florida, and remotely attempted to increase sodium hydroxide levels to dangerous concentrations. This attack highlighted exactly why CIP-style frameworks matter — industrial control systems (ICS) managing critical infrastructure are directly life-safety systems, not just data assets. NERC CIP compliance forces utilities to maintain asset inventories, patch management, and incident response plans specifically to prevent such scenarios.

## Key facts
- **NERC CIP** applies specifically to entities operating the North American bulk electric system; non-compliance carries fines up to **$1 million per violation per day**
- CIP standards are numbered (e.g., **CIP-002** covers asset identification; **CIP-007** covers systems security management; **CIP-011** covers information protection)
- Assets are classified by **BES Cyber System** impact levels: High, Medium, or Low — determining the rigor of controls required
- CIP requires **Electronic Security Perimeters (ESP)** around critical cyber assets, analogous to a network DMZ but for OT environments
- The framework mandates **15-minute** reporting to the Electricity Information Sharing and Analysis Center (E-ISAC) for certain severe incidents
