# Legal Hold

## What it is
Like a museum suddenly roping off an exhibit and forbidding anyone to touch, move, or discard anything inside — a legal hold is a formal directive that suspends normal data destruction and retention schedules to preserve electronically stored information (ESI) relevant to anticipated or active litigation. It overrides routine data lifecycle policies and requires organizations to freeze specific logs, emails, files, and communications until the matter is resolved.

## Why it matters
During a breach investigation, a company receives notice of a lawsuit alleging negligence in protecting customer PII. If the security team continues its standard 90-day log rotation and deletes firewall logs covering the breach window, the organization faces **spoliation of evidence** — a court can instruct the jury to assume the destroyed evidence was damaging. Proper legal hold procedures, triggered immediately upon litigation notice, prevent this catastrophic outcome.

## Key facts
- A legal hold is initiated by **legal counsel** (in-house or external) via a formal written notification to custodians — IT cannot trigger one unilaterally
- It must preserve **all relevant ESI**: emails, logs, backups, endpoint data, chat records, and cloud storage — not just what seems obviously relevant
- Failure to comply can result in **sanctions, adverse inference instructions, or default judgment** against the organization
- Legal holds survive personnel changes — if a custodian leaves, their data must still be preserved
- FRCP (Federal Rules of Civil Procedure) **Rule 37(e)** governs sanctions for failure to preserve ESI in U.S. federal cases — a frequent exam reference
