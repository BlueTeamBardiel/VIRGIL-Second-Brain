# Organized Crime

## What it is
Think of organized crime as a Fortune 500 company — except its product line is ransomware, fraud, and stolen data. It refers to structured, profit-driven criminal groups that operate cybercrime at scale, with defined roles (developers, money mules, negotiators), supply chains, and even customer support desks for ransomware victims.

## Why it matters
The Conti ransomware group — a classic organized crime operation — ran a full business hierarchy complete with HR, payroll, and an R&D department. They attacked hundreds of organizations including the Irish Health Service in 2021, demanding $20M and causing months of healthcare disruption. Defenders must recognize that these aren't lone hackers; they're fighting a staffed, funded adversary with operational resilience.

## Key facts
- Organized crime groups are financially motivated (not nation-state espionage), making them a distinct threat actor type on the Security+ exam
- They frequently operate **Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS)** models — selling ransomware toolkits to affiliates in exchange for a cut of profits
- **Money laundering** via cryptocurrency mixers and money mules is a core operational component, not an afterthought
- Groups like REvil, LockBit, and Lazarus Group (North Korea — a hybrid state/criminal actor) are commonly cited exam examples
- Organized crime often purchases initial access from **Initial Access Brokers (IABs)** rather than hacking in themselves — a clear division of labor
- They use the **dark web** for recruiting, selling stolen data, and publishing victim information to pressure payment (double extortion)
