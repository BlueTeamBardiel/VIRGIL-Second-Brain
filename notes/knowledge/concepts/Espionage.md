# Espionage

## What it is
Like a rival chef sneaking into a Michelin-star kitchen to photograph secret recipes, espionage is the covert collection of sensitive information from an organization or government without authorization. Precisely defined, it is the act of obtaining confidential data — trade secrets, military intelligence, or political strategies — through deception, infiltration, or technical means for competitive or geopolitical advantage.

## Why it matters
In 2015, Chinese state-sponsored group APT10 penetrated managed service providers (MSPs) to harvest intellectual property from aerospace, satellite, and pharmaceutical companies — affecting organizations in 45+ countries. The attack demonstrated how nation-state espionage leverages trusted third-party relationships as pivot points, bypassing direct perimeter defenses entirely.

## Key facts
- **Nation-state actors** are the primary espionage threat; they have long time horizons, significant funding, and accept low-and-slow operational tempos to avoid detection
- **Cyber espionage vs. traditional espionage**: both seek intelligence, but cyber methods (spear phishing, supply chain compromise, zero-days) scale without physical presence
- **Indicators of espionage** include large outbound data transfers at odd hours, beaconing traffic to uncommon geolocations, and access to files outside a user's job function
- **Economic espionage** is a federal crime under the U.S. Economic Espionage Act (1996), carrying penalties up to 15 years imprisonment and $5M in fines
- Espionage campaigns frequently use **living-off-the-land (LotL)** techniques — abusing legitimate tools like PowerShell or WMI — to blend into normal traffic and evade signature-based detection
