# T1566.004

## What it is
Like a con artist slipping a forged invoice under your hotel room door instead of calling you directly, spearphishing via voice (vishing) delivers social engineering attacks through phone calls rather than email. Precisely defined: T1566.004 covers adversary use of voice communication to manipulate targets into revealing credentials, executing malware, or granting access — often impersonating IT support, banks, or government agencies.

## Why it matters
During the 2020 Twitter Bitcoin scam, attackers called Twitter employees directly, impersonating internal IT staff to harvest VPN credentials — then used those credentials to access admin tools and hijack high-profile accounts. This attack bypassed every email filter and endpoint control in place, demonstrating that vishing can circumvent technical defenses entirely by targeting the human layer.

## Key facts
- Vishing frequently pairs with pretexting — the attacker constructs a believable backstory (fake helpdesk ticket, urgent security alert) before calling to establish legitimacy
- Caller ID spoofing tools allow adversaries to display trusted numbers (e.g., your company's IT department) at near-zero cost, dramatically increasing success rates
- Hybrid attacks combine vishing with smishing (T1566.004 + T1566.003): a text message primes the target, then a follow-up call closes the manipulation
- Unlike phishing emails, vishing leaves minimal forensic artifacts — no email headers, no malicious URLs — making post-incident attribution harder
- MITRE classifies this under Initial Access; common outcomes include credential harvesting, MFA bypass (attacker requests the victim read back their OTP), and remote access tool installation
