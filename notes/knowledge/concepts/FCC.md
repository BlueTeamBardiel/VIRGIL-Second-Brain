# FCC

## What it is
Think of the FCC like a zoning authority for the electromagnetic spectrum — it decides who gets to broadcast where, at what power, and on which frequencies. The Federal Communications Commission is the U.S. regulatory agency responsible for overseeing interstate and international communications via radio, television, wire, satellite, and cable, including setting standards for device emissions and network security practices.

## Why it matters
When a vulnerability in cellular infrastructure exposes SS7 (Signaling System 7) protocols to interception attacks, the FCC is the regulatory body with authority to mandate carrier-level security improvements. In 2024, after Salt Typhoon's breach of major U.S. telecom carriers, the FCC issued emergency rules requiring carriers to implement network security plans and certify their defenses — demonstrating how the FCC directly shapes defensive posture across critical communications infrastructure.

## Key facts
- The FCC enforces CPNI (Customer Proprietary Network Information) rules, which restrict how carriers can use or share subscriber data — a direct privacy and data protection control
- FCC Part 15 governs unlicensed spectrum devices (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth); any device operating in these bands must comply with emissions limits to prevent interference-based denial of service
- The FCC's Communications Security, Reliability and Interoperability Council (CSRIC) produces cybersecurity best practices specifically for network operators
- FCC penalties for data breaches and unauthorized disclosure can reach millions of dollars — making compliance a business-critical security driver
- The FCC collaborates with CISA and NSA on securing telecommunications against nation-state threats under the broader national critical infrastructure protection framework
