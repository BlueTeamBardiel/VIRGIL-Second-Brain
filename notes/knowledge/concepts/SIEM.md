# SIEM

## What it is
Think of a SIEM as the air traffic control tower for your entire network — it pulls radar feeds from dozens of sources simultaneously, correlates flight paths, and alerts controllers when two planes are on a collision course. A Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) system aggregates log data from across an environment (firewalls, endpoints, servers, applications), normalizes it into a common format, and applies correlation rules to detect threats in near-real-time. It combines the historical storage of SIM with the real-time alerting of SEM.

## Why it matters
During the Target breach of 2013, alerts were generated by security tools but went unnoticed among thousands of low-priority events — a problem a well-tuned SIEM with proper alert prioritization is designed to solve. A SIEM would correlate the initial phishing compromise, lateral movement, and unusual POS traffic into a single high-confidence incident alert rather than scattered noise. This is precisely why SIEM sits at the center of most SOC workflows.

## Key facts
- **Log aggregation sources** include firewalls, IDS/IPS, Windows Event Logs, DNS servers, and authentication systems — normalization maps these into a unified schema
- **Correlation rules** define patterns across multiple events (e.g., 5 failed logins followed by a success = brute force alert)
- **Retention** is critical for forensics and compliance — SIEM typically stores logs for 90 days to 1+ year depending on regulatory requirements (PCI-DSS, HIPAA)
- **Baseline behavior** enables anomaly detection; deviations from normal trigger alerts (user logging in at 3 AM from a new country)
- **Common SIEM products**: Splunk, Microsoft Sentinel, IBM QRadar, and ArcSight — Splunk uses SPL (Search Processing Language) for queries
