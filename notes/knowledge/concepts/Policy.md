# Policy

## What it is
Like the rulebook a bank gives every teller — not the vault itself, but the written law governing who can open it, when, and how — a security policy is a formal, documented set of rules that defines acceptable behavior, responsibilities, and controls within an organization. Policies are management-level directives that establish *what* must be done, leaving procedures and standards to explain *how*.

## Why it matters
In the 2014 Target breach, attackers pivoted through a third-party HVAC vendor that had excessive network access. A well-enforced third-party access policy with least-privilege requirements and network segmentation rules would have limited that vendor's reach, containing the blast radius before 40 million credit card records were compromised.

## Key facts
- Policies sit at the **top of the security documentation hierarchy**: Policy → Standard → Guideline → Procedure
- An **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)** is one of the most common policy types and defines permitted uses of organizational systems — employees typically sign it at onboarding
- Policies must have **executive/board-level sponsorship** to be enforceable; without authority behind them, they're just suggestions
- **NIST SP 800-53** and **ISO/IEC 27001** both require documented policies as foundational controls for compliance frameworks
- Policies are **technology-neutral** by design — they state intent and obligation, not specific tools or configurations, so they remain valid as technology changes
