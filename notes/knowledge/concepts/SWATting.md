# SWATting

## What it is
Imagine calling the fire department to a rival's house with a fake report of a gas explosion — except the "fire department" is an armed SWAT team. SWATting is a form of harassment where an attacker makes a false emergency report (bomb threat, hostage situation, active shooter) to law enforcement, directing a heavily armed tactical response to a victim's physical location. It weaponizes emergency services as a proxy tool for real-world violence and intimidation.

## Why it matters
In 2017, a SWATting incident in Wichita, Kansas resulted in the death of an innocent man when police shot him after an attacker called in a false hostage situation to his address. The attacker obtained the victim's address through social engineering and doxing — demonstrating how OSINT and personal data exposure directly enable physical-world harm. This case led to federal charges under 18 U.S.C. § 1038 (false emergency reports), establishing serious criminal precedent.

## Key facts
- SWATting is classified as a **cyberharassment-to-physical threat escalation** — it bridges digital attacks and real-world violence
- Attackers typically use **VoIP spoofing** and **caller ID spoofing** to mask their identity and location when making false reports
- **Doxing** (publishing private personal information) is almost always a prerequisite — victims are targeted after their home address is exposed
- Penalties under federal law can include up to **5 years imprisonment**, escalating to life imprisonment or death penalty charges if the incident results in a fatality
- Law enforcement agencies use **SWATting registries** (e.g., programs in several U.S. states) allowing potential targets to pre-register and alert dispatchers to their vulnerability
