# Fencing

## What it is
Like a pawn shop that buys stolen goods and resells them to unknowing customers, fencing in cybersecurity is the process of selling stolen digital assets — credentials, data, access — through intermediary marketplaces that obscure the original theft. Precisely, fencing refers to the monetization layer of cybercrime where threat actors convert stolen data or system access into profit via darknet markets, forums, or private brokers.

## Why it matters
After a large-scale credential breach, attackers rarely use the stolen data themselves — they sell it in bulk on platforms like Genesis Market or Russian Market, where buyers purchase credential packages to conduct account takeover attacks. This separation between the initial breach and downstream fraud complicates attribution and stretches the timeline of victim harm well beyond the original incident.

## Key facts
- Fencing operations use cryptocurrency (primarily Monero for anonymity, Bitcoin for liquidity) to obscure financial transactions between sellers and buyers
- Stolen data is typically tiered by value: financial credentials and corporate VPN access fetch premium prices, while raw email/password dumps sell cheaply in bulk
- Initial Access Brokers (IABs) are a specialized fencing role — they breach networks, then sell persistent access to ransomware groups rather than exploiting it themselves
- Law enforcement takedowns (e.g., Genesis Market in 2023) are cyber-physical operations requiring international coordination under frameworks like Europol's J-CAT
- Defenders monitor fencing markets via threat intelligence feeds to detect when organizational credentials appear for sale, enabling proactive incident response
