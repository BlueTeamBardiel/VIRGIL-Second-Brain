# Operational Controls

## What it is
Like a restaurant's health inspection checklist — enforced by humans following daily procedures rather than by the stove's thermostat — operational controls are security measures implemented through people and processes rather than technology or policy documents alone. They are the day-to-day administrative actions organizations take to maintain security: training programs, background checks, incident response procedures, and security awareness campaigns. Unlike technical controls (firewalls) or physical controls (badge readers), operational controls live in human behavior and workflow.

## Why it matters
In the 2011 RSA SecurID breach, the technical controls were intact — but an employee opened a phishing email disguised as an HR spreadsheet. A robust operational control — mandatory phishing simulation training with verified completion — could have changed that outcome. This illustrates that operational controls are often the last line of defense when technical layers fail, and also the most frequently bypassed layer due to human error.

## Key facts
- Operational controls are one of three control **categories** in NIST SP 800-53 alongside **technical** and **management** controls
- Examples include: security awareness training, job rotation, separation of duties enforcement, media sanitization procedures, and incident response drills
- **Job rotation** and **mandatory vacation** are operational controls specifically designed to detect fraud or collusion by ensuring no single person holds a role indefinitely
- Operational controls address **ongoing operations** — they require continuous human effort to remain effective, unlike a firewall rule set once
- On Security+ exams, operational controls are often the correct answer when a scenario involves **people-based processes** rather than software or hardware solutions
