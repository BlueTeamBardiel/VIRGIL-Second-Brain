# insider threat

## What it is
Like a bank teller who knows where the cameras have blind spots and which vault codes changed last Tuesday — the danger isn't the locked door, it's the person who already has the key. An insider threat is a security risk that originates from within the organization: employees, contractors, or partners who abuse legitimate access to harm systems, data, or operations, either maliciously or through negligence.

## Why it matters
In 2022, a Cisco engineer deleted 456 virtual machines after resignation by accessing a personal Google Cloud account he had configured on company infrastructure before leaving — costing Cisco $2.4 million in recovery. This illustrates why offboarding procedures and monitoring of privileged access are not paperwork exercises but critical controls; the attacker needed zero external exploitation because he already had authorized credentials.

## Key facts
- Insider threats are categorized as **malicious** (intentional sabotage/theft), **negligent** (accidental data exposure), or **compromised** (legitimate account hijacked by an outsider)
- The **principle of least privilege** is the primary preventive control — users should have only the minimum access required for their role
- **User and Entity Behavior Analytics (UEBA)** detects insider threats by flagging anomalous behavior patterns like large after-hours data transfers
- **Data Loss Prevention (DLP)** tools block or alert on unauthorized exfiltration attempts via email, USB, or cloud uploads
- Security+ classifies insider threats under the **human threat actor** category; the most dangerous insiders are those with **privileged access** (sysadmins, DBAs)
