# intellectual property

## What it is
Like a secret recipe locked in a vault — the value isn't the paper it's written on, but the knowledge itself. Intellectual property (IP) refers to intangible creations of the mind — source code, trade secrets, proprietary algorithms, product designs — that organizations own and must legally protect. Unlike physical assets, IP can be stolen without the original ever going missing, making detection and protection uniquely challenging.

## Why it matters
A disgruntled engineer at a semiconductor company exfiltrates chip design files via USB to a competitor — the company loses millions in R&D investment and competitive advantage before anyone notices the data is gone. This is a classic insider threat IP theft scenario tested in CySA+ contexts, where Data Loss Prevention (DLP) tools, endpoint monitoring, and user behavior analytics are the primary defensive controls. Nation-state actors specifically target IP in industries like aerospace, pharmaceuticals, and defense as part of economic espionage campaigns.

## Key facts
- Trade secrets are a category of IP with no expiration — protection lasts as long as the secret is kept (unlike patents, which expire after ~20 years)
- The Economic Espionage Act (1996) makes theft of trade secrets a federal crime in the U.S., with penalties up to 15 years imprisonment
- DLP solutions classify and monitor IP movement across endpoints, email, and cloud storage to prevent unauthorized exfiltration
- IP theft is frequently motivated by nation-state actors; the FBI identifies China as the most significant long-term IP theft threat to the U.S.
- Data classification policies are the foundational control for IP protection — if data isn't labeled, it can't be protected appropriately
