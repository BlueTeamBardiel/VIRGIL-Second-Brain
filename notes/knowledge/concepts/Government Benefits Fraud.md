# Government Benefits Fraud

## What it is
Like a pickpocket who steals your wallet to cash a check in your name, government benefits fraud occurs when attackers steal or fabricate identities to illegitimately claim public assistance programs. It is the unauthorized filing for or diversion of government benefits — such as unemployment insurance, SNAP, Social Security, or COVID relief funds — using stolen PII, synthetic identities, or compromised accounts. It is classified as a form of identity theft and financial fraud under federal law.

## Why it matters
During the COVID-19 pandemic, attackers used credential-stuffing tools and purchased breach dumps to file fraudulent unemployment claims across all 50 U.S. states, stealing an estimated **$135–$400 billion** in pandemic relief funds. Security analysts defending state labor agencies had to deploy behavioral analytics to flag impossible geography patterns — like a single SSN filing claims in five states within minutes — as indicators of compromise.

## Key facts
- **Synthetic identity fraud** — combining a real SSN (often a child's) with fabricated name/DOB — is the fastest-growing variant and hardest to detect because no real victim immediately notices
- CISA classifies benefit fraud infrastructure attacks as targeting **critical government services**, making them relevant to both criminal and nation-state threat actors
- Common attack vectors: phishing, credential stuffing, dark web PII purchases, and **account takeover (ATO)** of existing benefit portals
- Defense controls include **multi-factor authentication (MFA)**, identity proofing (ID.me-style verification), anomaly detection on claim velocity, and cross-agency data sharing
- Under **18 U.S.C. § 1343** (wire fraud) and **§ 1028A** (aggravated identity theft), perpetrators face federal prosecution with mandatory 2-year minimums for identity theft charges
