# Standard Contractual Clauses

## What it is
Think of SCCs as a pre-approved passport for data crossing international borders — instead of negotiating customs rules from scratch, both parties stamp the same government-approved document and the data travels legally. Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) are legally binding contract templates issued by the European Commission that allow organizations to transfer personal data from the EU/EEA to third countries lacking an adequacy decision, by contractually obligating the receiving party to GDPR-equivalent data protections.

## Why it matters
After the 2020 *Schrems II* ruling invalidated the EU-US Privacy Shield, thousands of companies transferring user data to US cloud providers suddenly had no legal basis for those transfers. Organizations that had implemented SCCs as a fallback mechanism could continue operations, while those relying solely on Privacy Shield faced regulatory exposure and potential fines — demonstrating that SCCs function as a critical compliance continuity control, not just legal paperwork.

## Key facts
- SCCs are issued by the European Commission under GDPR Article 46(2)(c) and cannot be modified — any alteration voids their legal validity
- The 2021 modernized SCCs introduced four modules covering: Controller-to-Controller, Controller-to-Processor, Processor-to-Processor, and Processor-to-Controller transfers
- Organizations must conduct a **Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA)** alongside SCCs to evaluate whether the destination country's laws undermine the contractual protections
- SCCs do not automatically satisfy GDPR compliance — the data exporter retains accountability for verifying the importer's ability to uphold obligations
- Non-EU countries with adequacy decisions (e.g., Japan, Canada, UK post-Brexit via UK GDPR) don't require SCCs — understanding this distinction appears in data governance exam questions
