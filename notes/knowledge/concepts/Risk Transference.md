# Risk Transference

## What it is
Like a homeowner who can't fireproof their house but buys fire insurance instead — the house can still burn, but the financial pain shifts to someone else. Risk transference is a risk management strategy where an organization shifts the financial or legal burden of a potential loss to a third party, most commonly through insurance or contracts. The risk itself isn't eliminated; ownership of the consequences is simply reassigned.

## Why it matters
After the 2017 NotPetya ransomware attack devastated Merck's operations, the company filed a $1.4 billion claim under its property insurance policy — only to be initially denied because insurers argued it qualified as an "act of war" exclusion. This case exposed a critical gap: organizations assuming risk was transferred via standard insurance policies discovered their cyber losses might not actually be covered, making the precise language of cyber insurance policies a strategic security decision, not just a finance department checkbox.

## Key facts
- Cyber liability insurance is the most common vehicle for risk transference in cybersecurity contexts
- Contracts with vendors (e.g., SLAs, indemnification clauses) are a legal form of risk transference, shifting liability for breaches to the service provider
- Risk transference does **not** reduce the likelihood of an incident — it only affects financial impact after the fact
- On Security+ exams, risk transference is one of four core risk responses: **Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate (Reduce), Accept**
- Transferring risk still requires due diligence — insurers may require proof of specific controls (MFA, encryption, patching cadence) before issuing a policy
