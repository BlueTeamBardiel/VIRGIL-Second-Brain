# Computer Fraud and Abuse Act

## What it is
Think of the CFAA as a "No Trespassing" sign with federal teeth — just as crossing a fenced property line is illegal regardless of whether you break anything, accessing a computer "without authorization" is a federal crime regardless of intent or damage caused. The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (1986, amended multiple times) is the primary U.S. federal law criminalizing unauthorized access to computers and networks, covering everything from hacking government systems to exceeding authorized access on systems you legitimately use.

## Why it matters
In the 2013 case of Aaron Swartz, a researcher was prosecuted under the CFAA for mass-downloading academic articles from JSTOR using MIT's network — a network he was technically authorized to access — demonstrating how "exceeding authorized access" is a notoriously vague and powerful prosecutorial tool. For defenders, the CFAA is also the legal foundation for pursuing criminal charges after a breach, making proper logging and access controls critical for building a prosecutable case.

## Key facts
- Covers seven categories of computer crimes, including unauthorized access, fraud, extortion, and trafficking in passwords
- "Protected computer" is defined broadly — any computer used in interstate commerce, which essentially includes anything connected to the internet
- Civil and criminal penalties: criminal charges can reach 10–20 years for repeat/aggravated offenses; civil suits allow victims to seek damages
- The "exceeds authorized access" clause is controversial — it has been used against employees who misuse credentials they legitimately hold
- The 2021 Van Buren v. United States Supreme Court ruling narrowed the CFAA's scope, ruling that using authorized access for unauthorized *purposes* alone does not violate the act
