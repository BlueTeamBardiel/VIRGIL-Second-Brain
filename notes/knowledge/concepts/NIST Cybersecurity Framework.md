# NIST Cybersecurity Framework

## What it is
Think of it as a universal electrical outlet standard — it doesn't tell you what to plug in, but it ensures every device speaks the same language and fits the same socket. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) is a voluntary, risk-based framework published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology that gives organizations a common taxonomy and methodology for managing cybersecurity risk across six core functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover.

## Why it matters
During the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack, post-incident analysis revealed gaps in the company's ability to *Detect* anomalous activity and *Respond* rapidly — two explicit CSF functions. Organizations using the CSF as a structured baseline can map those gaps before an attacker exploits them, rather than discovering them during a breach.

## Key facts
- **CSF 2.0 (2024)** added a sixth function — **Govern** — making organizational leadership and risk strategy an explicit pillar alongside the original five
- The framework uses **Tiers (1–4)** to describe an organization's cybersecurity maturity: Partial → Risk Informed → Repeatable → Adaptive
- **Profiles** are customized snapshots: a *Current Profile* (what you do now) vs. a *Target Profile* (what you want to achieve), with the gap driving the roadmap
- CSF is **not a compliance mandate** — it is voluntary, though federal agencies are directed by Executive Order 13800 to use it
- CSF maps directly to other standards, including **NIST SP 800-53**, **ISO 27001**, and **CIS Controls**, enabling cross-framework translation
