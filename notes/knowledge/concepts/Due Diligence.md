# Due diligence

## What it is
Like a home inspector who crawls under the floorboards *before* you sign the mortgage — not after the pipes burst — due diligence is the proactive, systematic investigation of security risks before committing to a decision. It is the process of thoroughly researching, verifying, and assessing the security posture of a system, vendor, partner, or acquisition to ensure known risks are identified and understood prior to taking action.

## Why it matters
When Target was breached in 2013, attackers entered through an HVAC vendor that had network access. Target failed to perform adequate due diligence on third-party vendor security controls before granting that access — a lapse that exposed 40 million credit card records. Proper vendor security assessments, contract security requirements, and access control reviews are all outputs of due diligence.

## Key facts
- Due diligence is **proactive** (before a decision); due care is **ongoing** (after a decision) — this distinction is a classic exam trap on Security+
- Third-party risk management programs require due diligence on vendors via questionnaires, SOC 2 reports, and penetration test summaries before onboarding
- In mergers and acquisitions (M&A), cybersecurity due diligence must assess inherited vulnerabilities, data liabilities, and compliance gaps in the target company
- Failure to perform due diligence can create **legal liability** — courts have held organizations negligent for ignoring foreseeable risks they had reasonable ability to investigate
- Due diligence documentation (risk assessments, vendor reviews, audit reports) serves as evidence of a good-faith security program during regulatory investigations
