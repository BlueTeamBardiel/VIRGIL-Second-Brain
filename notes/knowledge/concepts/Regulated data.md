# Regulated data

## What it is
Think of regulated data like radioactive material in a lab — it's not inherently dangerous to possess, but governments mandate strict handling procedures, containment protocols, and incident reporting because mishandling it causes serious societal harm. Regulated data is any category of information that law or industry standards legally require organizations to protect, handle, and disclose breaches of in specific ways. Common categories include PII, PHI (health records), PCI DSS cardholder data, and educational records under FERPA.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax exposed 147 million Americans' Social Security numbers, dates of birth, and addresses — all regulated PII/financial data. Because this data falls under regulations like FCRA and state breach notification laws, Equifax faced $700 million in FTC settlements and mandatory security overhauls — consequences far exceeding what a breach of non-regulated data would have triggered. Compliance failures compound technical failures into legal catastrophe.

## Key facts
- **PHI** is governed by HIPAA; covered entities must report breaches affecting 500+ individuals within 60 days to HHS and affected individuals
- **PCI DSS** applies to any organization that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data — not just banks; non-compliance can result in card processing privilege revocation
- **GDPR** defines regulated personal data broadly and requires breach notification within **72 hours** of discovery — one of the strictest timelines globally
- **Data sovereignty** means regulated data may be prohibited from crossing national borders (e.g., EU data cannot be freely stored on US servers without safeguards)
- Security+ distinguishes regulated data categories (PII, PHI, financial) from **non-regulated sensitive data** (trade secrets, IP) — both need protection, but legal obligations differ sharply
