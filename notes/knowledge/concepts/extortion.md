# extortion

## What it is
Like a loan shark who breaks your kneecap *before* you've paid — just to show they mean business — extortion is the act of threatening harm to coerce a victim into paying money or performing actions. Precisely: extortion is a cybercrime where an attacker threatens to cause damage (publish data, destroy systems, continue an attack) unless the victim complies with demands.

## Why it matters
In a ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) attack, operators deploy Conti or LockBit to encrypt a hospital's patient records, then demand $2M in Bitcoin with a 72-hour countdown. Modern "double extortion" escalates this by *also* threatening to publish stolen patient data on a leak site — meaning paying the ransom doesn't fully resolve the threat, forcing organizations to treat every ransomware incident as a data breach under HIPAA.

## Key facts
- **Double extortion** = encrypt data AND threaten to leak it; **triple extortion** adds DDoS attacks or contacting customers/partners directly
- Ransomware is the most common extortion vehicle; the FBI's IC3 reported ransomware as a top-cost cybercrime category in its 2023 Internet Crime Report
- Paying ransoms is legal in most U.S. jurisdictions but OFAC (Treasury) prohibits payments to sanctioned entities — creating legal exposure for victims
- Extortion differs from blackmail only technically; in cybersecurity, both terms are used interchangeably for threats to expose sensitive data
- Incident response plans must include a **do-not-pay policy decision tree** — Security+ expects you to know that paying does not guarantee data recovery or non-publication
