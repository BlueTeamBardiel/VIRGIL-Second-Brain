# T1498.002

## What it is
Like hiring thousands of people to simultaneously call a single phone line until it's permanently busy, Reflection Amplification turns small requests into massive floods by exploiting third-party servers that return responses far larger than the original query. An attacker spoofs the victim's IP address when sending small requests to open resolvers or misconfigured servers, which then send disproportionately large replies directly to the target — amplifying bandwidth without the attacker needing to generate it themselves.

## Why it matters
In the 2018 GitHub attack, attackers abused misconfigured Memcached servers to generate a peak of 1.35 Tbps of traffic — at the time the largest DDoS ever recorded. A single 203-byte request to Memcached could return a 100KB response, yielding an amplification factor of roughly 51,000x, making even a small botnet devastatingly effective against enterprise infrastructure.

## Key facts
- **Amplification factor** measures the ratio of response size to request size; DNS amplification yields ~54x, NTP can reach ~556x, Memcached ~51,000x
- **UDP is the enabler**: attackers exploit connectionless UDP protocols because source IP spoofing is trivial — TCP's three-way handshake prevents this technique
- **Common abused protocols**: DNS (port 53), NTP (port 123), SSDP (port 1900), Memcached (port 11211), CLDAP (port 389)
- **Detection indicator**: victims see massive inbound UDP traffic from diverse, legitimate third-party IPs — not directly from the attacker
- **Mitigation**: BCP38 network ingress filtering (anti-spoofing), disabling open resolvers, rate-limiting UDP responses, and scrubbing centers/CDN-based DDoS protection
