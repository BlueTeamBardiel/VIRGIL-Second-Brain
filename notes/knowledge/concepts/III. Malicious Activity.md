# III. Malicious Activity

## What it is
Like a burglar who might pick a lock, smash a window, or tailgate an employee — the *method* varies, but the criminal intent is constant. Malicious activity refers to any deliberate action taken by a threat actor to compromise the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of systems, data, or networks. It spans a spectrum from automated malware campaigns to hands-on-keyboard intrusions by nation-state actors.

## Why it matters
In 2021, the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack (attributed to DarkSide) began with a single compromised VPN credential and escalated to a full operational shutdown affecting fuel supply across the U.S. East Coast. Recognizing malicious activity early — through behavioral anomalies in authentication logs or unusual lateral movement — is exactly the skill CySA+ candidates must demonstrate. Detection lag directly translates to blast radius.

## Key facts
- **Malicious activity categories** include malware, social engineering, network-based attacks, application attacks, physical attacks, and insider threats — all testable on Security+
- **Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)** such as unusual outbound traffic, unexpected privileged account usage, and known-bad hashes are primary signals used to identify malicious activity in progress
- **MITRE ATT&CK** framework maps malicious activity to specific Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs), enabling defenders to anticipate attacker behavior beyond simple signatures
- **Insider threats** account for roughly 20–25% of malicious incidents (Verizon DBIR data), making them a high-priority category despite often being underweighted by organizations
- Malicious activity is distinct from **accidental incidents** — *intent* is the defining legal and forensic differentiator, affecting incident response, chain of custody, and law enforcement involvement
