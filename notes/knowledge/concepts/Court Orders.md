# Court Orders

## What it is
Like a judge handing a locksmith a legal mandate to open a specific safe, a court order is a binding judicial directive requiring an individual or organization to take a specific action — including producing data, preserving evidence, or granting access. In cybersecurity contexts, court orders compel organizations to hand over logs, emails, or encryption keys to law enforcement while establishing a legally defensible chain of custody.

## Why it matters
In a ransomware investigation, the FBI may obtain a court order requiring an ISP to preserve and surrender server logs before the attacker can delete them — this is called a **preservation order** or **litigation hold**. Without this legal mechanism, evidence gathered by law enforcement could be inadmissible, and the suspect could walk free on Fourth Amendment grounds. Organizations that comply improperly — or ignore orders — face contempt charges and civil liability.

## Key facts
- **Search warrants** are a type of court order authorizing law enforcement to seize digital evidence from a specific device or location; they require probable cause.
- **Subpoenas** compel testimony or document production but have a lower legal bar than warrants — no judge signature required in many jurisdictions.
- **Preservation orders** require organizations to freeze and retain specific data even if their normal retention policy would delete it.
- Organizations must maintain **chain of custody** when responding to court orders — any gap can render digital evidence inadmissible.
- The **Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA)** governs when U.S. law enforcement can compel ISPs and cloud providers to hand over stored communications without notifying the user.
