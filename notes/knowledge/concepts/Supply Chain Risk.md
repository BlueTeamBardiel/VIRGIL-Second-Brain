# Supply Chain Risk

## What it is
Like a restaurant that sources ingredients from dozens of farms — if one farm ships contaminated produce, every dish made with it gets poisoned regardless of how clean the kitchen is. Supply chain risk is the threat that hardware, software, or services acquired from third-party vendors introduce vulnerabilities into your environment before you ever touch them.

## Why it matters
In the 2020 SolarWinds attack, threat actors compromised the build pipeline of SolarWinds' Orion software update mechanism, embedding malicious code (SUNBURST) into a legitimate, digitally-signed update. Roughly 18,000 organizations — including U.S. federal agencies — installed the backdoor themselves by trusting a vendor they considered secure. The attack succeeded precisely because defenders focused on perimeter security while the threat entered through a trusted software update.

## Key facts
- **Due diligence** before vendor selection includes reviewing SOC 2 reports, penetration test results, and third-party audit findings — not just questionnaires
- **Hardware supply chain attacks** can include counterfeit components, firmware implants, or tampered devices (e.g., malicious chips soldered onto server boards)
- **Trusted Foundry Program** (U.S. DoD) addresses supply chain risk by certifying chip manufacturers for use in sensitive systems
- **End-of-life (EOL) software/hardware** from vendors creates supply chain risk because patches stop arriving — the vendor's support pipeline dries up
- Security+ exam expects you to know that **vendor agreements** (SLA, MOU, BPA, NDA) are primary contractual controls for managing third-party supply chain risk
