# Risk Transfer

## What it is
Like paying a moving company to carry your grandmother's priceless china instead of hauling it yourself — you still own the risk of it breaking, but now someone else is financially liable if it does. Risk transfer is a risk management strategy where the financial or legal burden of a potential loss is shifted to a third party, typically through insurance policies, contracts, or service-level agreements, without eliminating the underlying threat.

## Why it matters
After the 2017 NotPetya ransomware attack devastated Merck's operations, the company filed a $1.4 billion insurance claim — only to have insurers initially deny it as an "act of war." This real battle exposed a critical lesson: cyber insurance policies contain exclusions that can nullify your transfer strategy entirely, meaning organizations must audit policy language as carefully as their firewall rules.

## Key facts
- Cyber liability insurance is the most common risk transfer mechanism in cybersecurity, covering breach notification costs, legal fees, and ransomware payouts
- Risk transfer does **not** eliminate the operational disruption or reputational damage — only the financial liability shifts
- Third-party contracts (SLAs, indemnification clauses) are a non-insurance form of risk transfer — a vendor accepts liability for their security failures
- On the Security+/CySA+ exam, risk transfer is one of four core risk responses: **Avoid, Mitigate, Transfer, Accept**
- Outsourcing IT to an MSSP is a hybrid of risk transfer and risk mitigation — liability may shift contractually, but residual risk remains with the organization
