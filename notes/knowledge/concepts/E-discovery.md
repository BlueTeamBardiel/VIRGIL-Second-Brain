# E-discovery

## What it is
Like an archaeologist sifting through layers of sediment to find artifacts that tell a legal story, e-discovery is the process of identifying, collecting, and preserving electronically stored information (ESI) for use in litigation or investigations. It encompasses emails, documents, logs, databases, and even deleted files that may serve as evidence in legal proceedings.

## Why it matters
During a data breach lawsuit, opposing counsel can subpoena an organization's email servers, security logs, and internal Slack messages. If the company failed to issue a **litigation hold** — freezing deletion of relevant data the moment litigation was reasonably anticipated — they can face spoliation sanctions, meaning the court may instruct the jury to assume the destroyed evidence was damaging.

## Key facts
- **FRCP Rule 37(e)** governs federal courts' response to lost ESI; failure to preserve can result in adverse inference instructions or case-terminating sanctions
- The **EDRM (Electronic Discovery Reference Model)** defines the standard e-discovery workflow: Information Governance → Identification → Preservation → Collection → Processing → Review → Production
- A **litigation hold** (also called a legal hold) is a mandatory suspension of normal data retention/destruction schedules for potentially relevant data
- ESI includes not just files but also metadata, system logs, voicemails, and data in cloud storage — metadata timestamps are frequently used to verify or dispute timelines
- Security teams must balance **data minimization** principles (privacy regulations like GDPR) against e-discovery obligations, as over-deletion can violate litigation hold duties
