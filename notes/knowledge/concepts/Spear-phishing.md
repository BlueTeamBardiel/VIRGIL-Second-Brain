# Spear-phishing

## What it is
Like a hunter who studies a specific deer's habits, favorite trails, and feeding times before the hunt — rather than just firing blindly into the forest — spear-phishing is a *targeted* social engineering attack crafted around a specific individual or organization. Unlike generic phishing blasts, the attacker researches the victim (job role, colleagues, recent projects) to craft a convincing, personalized lure delivered via email or message.

## Why it matters
In the 2016 DNC breach, attackers sent John Podesta an email spoofing Google's security team, warning of unauthorized account access. Because the message referenced his real account and used familiar branding, he clicked — and adversaries exfiltrated thousands of emails. This illustrates how personalization collapses skepticism even among technically aware users.

## Key facts
- **Reconnaissance-driven**: Attackers use OSINT (LinkedIn, social media, company websites) to tailor content — referencing real project names, managers, or vendors increases open rates dramatically.
- **BEC subset**: Business Email Compromise (BEC) is a financially motivated spear-phishing variant targeting executives or finance staff to authorize fraudulent wire transfers.
- **Whaling** refers specifically to spear-phishing aimed at high-value targets: C-suite executives, board members, or system administrators.
- **Indicators**: Unusual sender domain (look-alike domains like "micros0ft.com"), unexpected urgency, requests for credentials or wire transfers, and internal-sounding language from external addresses.
- **Mitigation controls**: DMARC/DKIM/SPF email authentication, security awareness training with simulated phishing campaigns, and MFA reduce spear-phishing success rates significantly.
