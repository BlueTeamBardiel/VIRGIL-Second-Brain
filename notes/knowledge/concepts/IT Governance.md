# IT Governance

## What it is
Think of IT governance like a city's zoning board: it doesn't lay the bricks itself, but it decides *where* buildings go, *what* they're used for, and *who* answers when something burns down. IT governance is the framework of policies, roles, and processes that ensures an organization's technology decisions align with business objectives and accountability structures. It defines *who decides*, *who executes*, and *who monitors* — separating decision rights from operational duties.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax suffered a breach exposing 147 million records partly because no one clearly *owned* the responsibility for patching a known Apache Struts vulnerability. This is a governance failure, not a technical one — the patch existed, but accountability gaps meant no one acted. Strong IT governance assigns explicit ownership so that "someone else's job" never becomes a critical vulnerability.

## Key facts
- **COBIT** (Control Objectives for Information and Related Technologies) is the most widely referenced IT governance framework; it maps IT activities to business goals.
- **Separation of duties** is a governance control — it prevents any single individual from controlling an entire critical process (e.g., the person approving firewall changes shouldn't also be implementing them).
- Governance differs from **management**: governance sets direction and accountability; management executes within those constraints.
- **RACI matrices** (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) are a common governance tool for defining roles in security processes.
- Regulatory frameworks like **SOX**, **HIPAA**, and **PCI-DSS** impose external governance requirements — organizations must demonstrate controls exist *and* are enforced, not just documented.
