# Platform Diversity

## What it is
Like a farmer who plants corn, wheat, and soybeans instead of a single crop — so one blight can't wipe out the entire harvest — platform diversity means deploying multiple different operating systems, vendors, or technologies across an environment. Precisely defined: it is the security strategy of using heterogeneous systems so that a single exploit, vulnerability, or supply chain compromise cannot simultaneously take down all assets.

## Why it matters
In 2017, the NotPetya ransomworm devastated organizations running homogeneous Windows environments, propagating laterally via EternalBlue with little resistance. Organizations that maintained Linux or macOS endpoints alongside Windows — or segmented their Windows versions — saw the blast radius contained. A diverse platform strategy forces attackers to develop and deploy multiple exploits rather than one universal weapon.

## Key facts
- Platform diversity is a **defense-in-depth** control; it increases attacker cost and complexity rather than eliminating vulnerabilities outright.
- It applies not just to OS (Windows vs. Linux vs. macOS) but also to hypervisors, firmware vendors, network device manufacturers, and cloud providers (multi-cloud).
- A key trade-off: diversity **increases operational complexity and cost** — patching, training, and tooling must cover multiple platforms, which can introduce management gaps.
- Under **NIST SP 800-160**, platform diversity is recognized as a cyber-resiliency technique aimed at limiting the impact of common-mode failures and supply chain attacks.
- On Security+ and CySA+ exams, platform diversity is frequently paired with **redundancy** and **vendor diversity** as complementary resilience strategies — know the distinction: vendor diversity means different suppliers for the same function; platform diversity means different underlying technologies.
