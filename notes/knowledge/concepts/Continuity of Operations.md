# Continuity of Operations

## What it is
Like a hospital with backup generators, redundant oxygen supplies, and a triage plan that kicks in automatically when the power grid fails — Continuity of Operations (COOP) is an organization's pre-engineered ability to maintain essential functions during and after a disruption. It is the formal planning framework that ensures critical business processes survive disasters, cyberattacks, or infrastructure failures without requiring improvisation under pressure.

## Why it matters
During the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack, the company shut down operations not because the industrial control systems were compromised, but because their billing and business systems were — they had no COOP plan to operate under degraded conditions. A mature COOP plan with predefined fallback procedures and isolated OT/IT segmentation could have kept fuel flowing while the incident was contained.

## Key facts
- COOP is distinct from Disaster Recovery (DR): DR focuses on restoring IT systems; COOP focuses on sustaining *business functions* regardless of whether systems are restored
- **Recovery Time Objective (RTO)** defines the maximum tolerable downtime; **Recovery Point Objective (RPO)** defines the maximum acceptable data loss — both are core COOP metrics
- COOP plans must identify **Mission Essential Functions (MEFs)** — the minimum operations an organization must maintain to survive
- NIST SP 800-34 provides the federal standard for IT Contingency Planning, which underpins COOP development
- COOP testing methods include **tabletop exercises**, **parallel tests** (running backup systems alongside production), and **full interruption tests** (cutting over entirely to backup systems)
