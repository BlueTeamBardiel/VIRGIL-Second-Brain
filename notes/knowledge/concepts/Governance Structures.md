# Governance Structures

## What it is
Like a city's legal system — zoning laws, a mayor, city council, and courts all working in concert — governance structures are the layered framework of policies, roles, standards, and oversight bodies that direct how an organization manages cybersecurity risk. Precisely: governance structures define *who* makes security decisions, *by what authority*, and *how accountability is enforced* across an enterprise.

## Why it matters
In the 2017 Equifax breach, a governance failure was as damaging as the technical vulnerability itself — no single person owned responsibility for patching the Apache Struts flaw, and the security committee lacked authority to enforce remediation deadlines. A mature governance structure would have assigned a CISO with board-level accountability and a documented patch management policy with enforceable SLAs, potentially stopping the breach that exposed 147 million records.

## Key facts
- The **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)** and **ISO/IEC 27001** are the two most common governance frameworks tested on Security+ and CySA+; ISO 27001 is certifiable, NIST CSF is not
- **Separation of duties** is a governance control — no single person should be able to both initiate *and* approve a sensitive transaction
- Governance operates at three tiers: **Strategic** (board/CISO), **Tactical** (managers/policies), and **Operational** (technical controls/daily enforcement)
- A **RACI matrix** (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) is a governance tool used to prevent accountability gaps like the Equifax scenario
- **Regulatory compliance** (HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOX) is enforced *through* governance structures — lack of governance is itself a compliance violation
