# Security Policy Framework

## What it is
Like a city's legal code — zoning laws, traffic ordinances, building codes — that turns abstract civic values into enforceable rules, a Security Policy Framework translates an organization's risk tolerance into documented, actionable controls. It is a hierarchical collection of policies, standards, procedures, and guidelines that govern how an organization protects its information assets. The framework defines *what* must be done, *why*, and delegates *how* to lower-level documents.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax suffered a breach exposing 147 million records partly because no policy enforced timely patch application — the vulnerable Apache Struts component sat unpatched for 78 days despite a known CVE. A mature framework with a documented patch management policy and measurable compliance checkpoints would have created accountability and likely prevented the breach. Policy without enforcement is just intention; the framework provides the structure that makes enforcement possible.

## Key facts
- **Hierarchy matters**: Policies (senior leadership, "what") → Standards (measurable requirements) → Procedures (step-by-step "how") → Guidelines (discretionary recommendations)
- **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)** is the most commonly tested policy type on Security+ — defines permitted employee use of organizational systems
- **Separation of duties** and **least privilege** are policy controls, not just technical ones — they must appear in written policy to be auditable
- A policy framework typically references external standards: **ISO 27001**, **NIST SP 800-53**, or **CIS Controls** as the compliance backbone
- **Policy exceptions** must be formally documented with risk acceptance sign-off — undocumented exceptions become unmanaged vulnerabilities
