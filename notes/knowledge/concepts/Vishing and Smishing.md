# Vishing and Smishing

## What it is
Like a con artist wearing a phone company uniform to talk their way into your building, vishing and smishing use voice calls and SMS text messages respectively as the costume for social engineering attacks. Vishing (voice phishing) involves attackers calling victims while impersonating trusted entities like banks or IT helpdesks to extract credentials or sensitive data. Smishing (SMS phishing) delivers the same deception via text message, typically using urgent fake alerts containing malicious links or callback numbers.

## Why it matters
In 2020, Twitter's high-profile breach began when attackers called Twitter employees while impersonating IT support, convincing them to hand over VPN credentials — ultimately leading to the compromise of celebrity accounts including Barack Obama and Elon Musk. This demonstrates that even technically sophisticated organizations fall to voice-based deception because humans are the vulnerability, not the firewall.

## Key facts
- **Vishing red flags**: caller creates urgency, requests credentials verbally, refuses to use official ticketing systems, or asks you to bypass normal security procedures
- **Smishing red flags**: unexpected texts with shortened URLs, requests to confirm account details, fake package delivery notices with tracking links
- **Caller ID spoofing** makes vishing particularly dangerous — attackers can display legitimate bank or government phone numbers using VoIP services
- **Pretexting** is the companion technique — attackers fabricate a believable scenario (e.g., "your account shows suspicious activity") to make the request feel legitimate
- For Security+: both are classified under **social engineering** attacks; defenses include security awareness training, callback verification procedures, and out-of-band authentication
