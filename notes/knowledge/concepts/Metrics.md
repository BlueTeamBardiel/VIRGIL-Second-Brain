# Metrics

## What it is
Like a car's dashboard telling you speed, fuel, and engine temp at a glance, security metrics are quantifiable measurements that tell you the *health* of your security program at any given moment. Precisely defined: metrics are numerical or statistical values used to evaluate the effectiveness of security controls, track trends over time, and support data-driven risk decisions.

## Why it matters
During a post-incident review after a ransomware outbreak, a CISO needs to answer: "How long did it take us to detect this?" and "How long to contain it?" Without pre-established metrics like Mean Time to Detect (MTTD) and Mean Time to Respond (MTTR), the organization is guessing instead of improving. Poor visibility into these numbers is why breaches that should take hours to contain drag on for weeks.

## Key facts
- **MTTD (Mean Time to Detect):** Average time between when a breach begins and when it is discovered — a lower value indicates better detection capability
- **MTTR (Mean Time to Respond/Remediate):** Average time to contain or fix an incident after detection; directly impacts breach cost
- **KPIs vs. KRIs:** Key Performance Indicators measure how well controls *are working*; Key Risk Indicators signal when risk is *trending upward* before a failure occurs
- **Benchmarking:** Metrics gain meaning by comparison — either against your own historical baseline or industry standards (e.g., CIS Benchmarks, NIST frameworks)
- **Goodhart's Law risk:** When a metric becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure — teams may game numbers (e.g., closing tickets without fixing root causes) to hit KPIs
