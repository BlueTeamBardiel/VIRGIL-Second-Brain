# x509.log

## What it is
Think of it as a passport control ledger at an airport — every certificate that gets "checked" during a TLS handshake gets logged here. In Zeek (formerly Bro), `x509.log` records detailed metadata about X.509 certificates observed in network traffic, including subject, issuer, validity dates, and key information.

## Why it matters
During the 2011 DigiNotar breach, attackers issued fraudulent certificates for Google.com — had defenders been monitoring `x509.log`, they would have spotted certificates with unexpected issuers or serial numbers targeting their users. Today, threat hunters use this log to detect self-signed certificates used by malware C2 infrastructure, which often lack proper CA chains and have suspiciously short validity windows or mismatched subject/issuer fields.

## Key facts
- **File location in Zeek:** Generated alongside `ssl.log`; linked via the `cert_chain_fuids` field which ties certificates to specific connections in `conn.log`
- **Key fields:** `subject`, `issuer`, `certificate.not_valid_before`, `certificate.not_valid_after`, `san.dns` (Subject Alternative Names), and `certificate.key_alg`
- **Self-signed detection:** When `subject` == `issuer`, the certificate is self-signed — a significant red flag for C2 or MitM activity
- **SAN abuse:** Attackers registering wildcard or lookalike domains often appear in `san.dns` fields (e.g., `*.micosoft.com`) — searchable directly in this log
- **Zeek UID linkage:** Each certificate entry links back to `ssl.log` via `id` fields, enabling full connection reconstruction across multiple Zeek logs
