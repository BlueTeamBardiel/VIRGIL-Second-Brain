# Due Care

## What it is
Like a surgeon who washes their hands and checks the patient's chart before operating — not because the law demands it step-by-step, but because a reasonable professional *should* — due care is the ongoing, active effort an organization takes to protect its assets and act responsibly. Precisely: due care is the implementation of reasonable security measures and the continuous maintenance of those measures to reduce risk, demonstrating that an organization is actively fulfilling its duty of protection.

## Why it matters
When the Equifax breach exposed 147 million records in 2017, investigators found that a known Apache Struts vulnerability had gone unpatched for months after a patch was available. This failure to act on a known risk is a textbook due care violation — the organization *knew* the threat existed and failed to take reasonable corrective action, creating legal and regulatory liability beyond the breach itself.

## Key facts
- Due care focuses on **doing** — it's the actual implementation of security policies and controls, not just writing them
- Often contrasted with **due diligence** (the research/investigation phase); due diligence asks "what should we do?" while due care asks "are we actually doing it?"
- Failure to exercise due care can constitute **negligence** in legal proceedings, making the organization liable for damages
- Due care is continuous — it's not a one-time checkbox but an ongoing obligation (patching, monitoring, updating controls)
- In CISSP/Security+ context, a company that creates a security policy but never enforces or audits it has exercised due diligence but **failed** due care
