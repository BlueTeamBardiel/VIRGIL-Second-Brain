# Quid Pro Quo Attack

## What it is
Like a stranger offering to fix your printer in exchange for your login credentials "just to test the connection," a quid pro quo attack is a social engineering technique where an attacker offers a service, benefit, or favor in return for sensitive information or access. Unlike baiting (which uses physical lures), this attack trades a perceived service for trust.

## Why it matters
A classic real-world scenario involves attackers cold-calling employees while posing as IT support staff, offering to resolve a "detected virus" on their workstation — in exchange, the employee disables antivirus software or hands over their password. This exact method has been used in corporate espionage campaigns to bypass technical controls entirely through human manipulation.

## Key facts
- The defining characteristic is the **exchange dynamic** — the victim believes they are receiving something valuable (help, a prize, IT assistance) in return for compliance
- Most commonly impersonates **IT helpdesk staff**, making it highly effective in large organizations where employees don't know every technician
- Differs from **pretexting** (fabricating a scenario) in that quid pro quo specifically requires the attacker to *offer* something — it's transactional
- Low-tech and scalable: attackers can phone-blast hundreds of employees with minimal resources, requiring no technical skill
- Mitigation relies on **callback verification procedures**, security awareness training, and strict policies against sharing credentials with anyone — including internal IT
