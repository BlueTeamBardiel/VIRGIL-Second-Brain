# Misinformation

## What it is
Like a broken telephone game played at scale across social media, misinformation is false or inaccurate content spread without necessarily malicious intent — but weaponized in cybersecurity as a deliberate influence operation. Precisely defined: misinformation is factually incorrect information that circulates and distorts perception, whether the originator intended harm or not.

## Why it matters
During the 2020 U.S. election cycle, coordinated misinformation campaigns spread false voting dates and locations across social platforms, suppressing legitimate voter participation — a direct attack on democratic infrastructure without touching a single firewall. Defenders counter this through threat intelligence feeds that track narrative manipulation and coordinated inauthentic behavior at the network level, treating disinformation as a security domain, not just a political one.

## Key facts
- **Misinformation vs. Disinformation**: Misinformation lacks malicious intent; disinformation is deliberately crafted false content — Security+ distinguishes these under social engineering subcategories
- **Malinformation** completes the triad: true information weaponized to cause harm (e.g., leaking private data publicly)
- Social engineering attacks frequently use misinformation as a precursor — fake news about a company can prime employees for spear-phishing
- CISA classifies influence operations targeting critical infrastructure narratives as a Tier 1 threat alongside ransomware
- Misinformation spreads 6x faster than accurate information on social networks, per MIT research — a key threat amplifier
