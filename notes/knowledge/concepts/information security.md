# information security

## What it is
Like a medieval castle with walls, guards, a drawbridge, and secret codes — information security is the complete system of controls protecting data, not just any single barrier. It is the practice of defending information and information systems from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. It encompasses people, processes, and technology working together to preserve the CIA triad: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax failed to patch a known Apache Struts vulnerability (CVE-2017-5638), exposing 147 million Americans' personal records — a failure of process and patch management, not just technology. A mature information security program would have included vulnerability scanning, patch cycles, and network segmentation to limit the blast radius. This breach cost Equifax over $700 million in settlements, illustrating that InfoSec failures are business failures.

## Key facts
- The **CIA Triad** is the foundational model: Confidentiality (only authorized access), Integrity (data is accurate and unaltered), Availability (systems are accessible when needed)
- Information security is broader than cybersecurity — it includes physical security, personnel security, and paper records
- **Non-repudiation** is often added as a fourth pillar: ensuring actions can be attributed to a specific entity and cannot be denied
- The **McCumber Cube** (1991) extends CIA across three dimensions: information states (storage, processing, transmission) and security measures (technology, policy, people)
- NIST defines information security under **FISMA** as protecting information systems from unauthorized access to ensure confidentiality, integrity, and availability
