# T1069.003

## What it is
Like a new employee sneaking a peek at the company org chart to figure out who has the keys to the executive bathroom, attackers enumerate cloud groups to map out who holds privileged access. This sub-technique involves discovering group membership within cloud environments (AWS IAM groups, Azure AD groups, GCP IAM policies) to identify high-value accounts and permission boundaries.

## Why it matters
During the 2020 SolarWinds supply chain compromise, attackers moved into victim Azure AD tenants and enumerated privileged groups — including Global Administrators — to identify which accounts could grant persistent access without raising alarms. Defenders who monitor for bulk `Get-AzureADGroup` or AWS `iam:ListGroupsForUser` API calls can detect this reconnaissance before lateral movement begins.

## Key facts
- Attackers commonly use native CLI tools: `aws iam list-groups`, `az ad group list`, and `gcloud iam list` require minimal permissions and blend into normal admin activity
- Even **read-only** or **low-privileged** accounts can often enumerate group memberships in misconfigured tenants — no admin rights required
- Azure AD's default configuration historically allowed **any authenticated user** to enumerate all groups, making this nearly noiseless in legacy environments
- Key artifact: CloudTrail logs will show `ListGroups`, `ListGroupsForUser`, and `GetGroup` API calls in rapid succession from a single identity
- MITRE places this under **Discovery** tactic — the attacker's goal is situational awareness, not immediate exploitation
