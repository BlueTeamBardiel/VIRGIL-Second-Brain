# Directive controls

## What it is
Think of a directive control like a "No Trespassing" sign — it doesn't physically stop anyone, but it establishes rules, communicates expectations, and creates legal standing for consequences. Precisely defined, directive controls are administrative security measures that direct, constrain, or influence human behavior through policies, procedures, laws, and guidelines. They set the framework within which all other controls operate.

## Why it matters
When a disgruntled employee exfiltrates customer data, prosecutors need an Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) — a classic directive control — to demonstrate that the employee knowingly violated established rules. Without documented directives, organizations struggle to pursue legal action or enforce disciplinary measures, even when technical evidence is airtight. This is why HR onboarding security training and signed policy acknowledgments carry serious legal weight.

## Key facts
- Directive controls are **administrative** by nature — examples include security policies, AUPs, hiring procedures, data classification standards, and compliance mandates like HIPAA or PCI-DSS
- They are **proactive**, designed to prevent incidents by establishing expectations *before* a violation occurs — contrasted with detective controls that identify incidents after the fact
- On Security+/CySA+ exams, directive controls sit within the **administrative control category**, alongside deterrent and compensating controls
- A **security awareness training program** is one of the most commonly tested examples of a directive control
- Directive controls are foundational — they give legal and organizational authority to technical controls like firewalls and access logs
