# data subject

## What it is
Like the patient whose medical chart travels between doctors, insurers, and hospitals — the data subject is the living, identifiable person that data is *about*, not the one holding or processing it. Under GDPR and similar privacy frameworks, a **data subject** is any natural person who can be identified, directly or indirectly, through data such as a name, ID number, location, or online identifier.

## Why it matters
During a 2019 breach of a UK hotel chain, attackers exfiltrated records on roughly 500 million guests — each one a data subject with enforceable rights. The organization faced a £99 million GDPR fine partly because it failed to honor data subject rights (like the right to erasure) and lacked controls to even *know* what data it held on whom, demonstrating that poor data mapping creates both legal and technical liability.

## Key facts
- A data subject must be a **living natural person** — companies and deceased individuals are not data subjects under GDPR.
- Data subjects hold specific rights: **access, rectification, erasure ("right to be forgotten"), portability, restriction of processing, and objection**.
- **Pseudonymized data** can still relate to a data subject if re-identification is reasonably possible; fully anonymized data does not.
- Organizations must respond to a **Data Subject Access Request (DSAR)** within **30 days** under GDPR (extendable to 90 days for complex requests).
- The data subject is distinct from the **data controller** (who decides *why/how* data is processed) and the **data processor** (who processes it on the controller's behalf).
