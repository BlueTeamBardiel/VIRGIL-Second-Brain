# Reputational Damage

## What it is
Like a restaurant that survives a grease fire but closes six months later because Yelp reviews destroyed customer trust — reputational damage is the long-tail consequence of a security incident where loss of public confidence outlasts the technical breach itself. It is the measurable erosion of stakeholder trust, brand value, and market position resulting from a security failure, data breach, or disclosed vulnerability.

## Why it matters
After the 2013 Target breach (40 million credit cards compromised), Target's CEO and CIO resigned, the company paid $18.5 million in settlements — but the deeper wound was a 46% profit drop in Q4 2013 as customers abandoned their stores during peak holiday season. Reputational damage converted a technical failure into a business continuity crisis, demonstrating why risk assessments must quantify intangible losses alongside direct costs.

## Key facts
- Reputational damage is classified under **impact analysis** in risk management frameworks; it is an *intangible asset loss* alongside intellectual property and competitive advantage
- The Ponemon Institute consistently finds reputational damage accounts for **~40% of total breach cost** when customer churn and brand devaluation are included
- Disclosure *timing* critically affects severity — delayed breach notification legally required under GDPR/HIPAA amplifies reputational harm when regulators announce it instead of the company
- Organizations with a pre-established **Incident Response Plan (IRP)** including a communications playbook demonstrably reduce reputational loss by controlling narrative speed
- On Security+/CySA+ exams, reputational damage appears as a **non-financial impact** category alongside data loss, fines, and loss of life in risk scenario questions
