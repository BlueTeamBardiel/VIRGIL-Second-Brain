# incident response

## What it is
Like a hospital trauma team that follows a practiced protocol the moment a patient crashes — assess, stabilize, treat, document — incident response is the structured process an organization follows when a security breach or attack occurs. It transforms chaos into coordinated action through a predefined lifecycle. NIST defines it as preparation, detection, containment, eradication, recovery, and lessons learned.

## Why it matters
In the 2017 Equifax breach, attackers exfiltrated data on 147 million people over 78 days — largely because incident response was slow and disorganized. A faster detection-to-containment cycle would have dramatically limited the blast radius. Effective IR teams using playbooks and predefined communication chains can cut breach dwell time from months to hours.

## Key facts
- **NIST SP 800-61** defines the four IR phases: Preparation → Detection & Analysis → Containment, Eradication & Recovery → Post-Incident Activity
- **PICERL** is the SANS framework variant: Preparation, Identification, Containment, Eradication, Recovery, Lessons Learned
- **Containment strategies** split into short-term (isolate the host now) and long-term (patch/rebuild before reconnecting)
- **Chain of custody** must be maintained during evidence collection — forensic images, not live files, preserve legal admissibility
- **The IR plan must be tested** via tabletop exercises or simulations; an untested plan is treated as no plan on Security+/CySA+ exams
