# Trade Secrets

## What it is
Like a master chef who never writes down their signature recipe — keeping it locked in their head rather than patenting it — a trade secret is confidential business information that derives economic value precisely *because* it isn't publicly known. Legally, it's any formula, process, design, or business strategy that a company takes reasonable steps to keep secret and that provides a competitive advantage. Unlike patents, trade secrets have no expiration date — protection lasts as long as secrecy is maintained.

## Why it matters
In 2019, a former Apple engineer was charged with stealing autonomous vehicle schematics — trade secrets worth billions — before jumping to a Chinese competitor. This illustrates the core threat vector: **insider theft**, where a trusted employee with legitimate access exfiltrates IP through USB drives, personal email, or cloud uploads. DLP (Data Loss Prevention) tools and strict offboarding procedures exist specifically to counter this scenario.

## Key facts
- Trade secrets are protected under the **Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA) of 2016** at the federal level in the US, enabling civil lawsuits in federal court
- Protection requires demonstrating **reasonable measures** were taken to maintain secrecy (NDAs, access controls, encryption — not just hoping nobody looks)
- Unlike patents, trade secrets are **not registered** with any government body — the protection is entirely dependent on maintaining confidentiality
- **Economic espionage** — stealing trade secrets for a foreign government — is a federal crime under the Economic Espionage Act, carrying up to 15 years imprisonment
- For Security+/CySA+, trade secret theft is classified as a **data exfiltration** incident requiring both technical response and legal notification procedures
