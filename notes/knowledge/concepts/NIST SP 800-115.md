# NIST SP 800-115

## What it is
Like a flight manual that tells pilots exactly how to safely test an aircraft's limits without crashing it, NIST SP 800-115 is the federal government's technical guide for planning and executing information security assessments. It provides standardized methodology for penetration testing, vulnerability scanning, and security review activities conducted against organizational systems.

## Why it matters
When a federal contractor was hired to assess a healthcare agency's network, using SP 800-115 ensured their phishing simulations, port scanning, and exploitation attempts followed a legally defensible, rules-of-engagement framework — protecting both the tester and the organization from scope creep or accidental system damage. Without it, testers risk overstepping authorization boundaries and triggering Computer Fraud and Abuse Act violations.

## Key facts
- Published by NIST in **2008**, it covers four assessment phases: **Planning, Discovery, Attack, and Reporting**
- Defines three core testing techniques: **network scanning**, **vulnerability scanning**, and **penetration testing** — each with escalating intrusiveness
- Distinguishes between **passive information gathering** (no direct target interaction, e.g., OSINT) and **active testing** (direct interaction with target systems)
- Emphasizes the **Rules of Engagement (ROE)** document as a prerequisite — defining scope, authorized targets, timing windows, and emergency contacts
- The **attack phase** explicitly includes social engineering, wireless testing, and application testing as sub-categories — not just network exploitation
