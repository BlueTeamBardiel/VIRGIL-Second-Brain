# Computer Security Incident Response Team

## What it is
Like a hospital trauma team — each member has a defined role and springs into coordinated action the moment a patient (system) crashes — a CSIRT is a dedicated group responsible for detecting, containing, analyzing, and recovering from cybersecurity incidents. It provides an organization with a structured, pre-planned capability to respond to breaches rather than improvising under pressure.

## Why it matters
During the 2017 Equifax breach, attackers exfiltrated 147 million records over 78 days partly because the incident response process was slow and disorganized — a properly functioning CSIRT with defined escalation paths and monitoring responsibilities could have dramatically shortened that dwell time. CSIRTs operationalize the NIST IR lifecycle (Prepare → Detect → Contain → Eradicate → Recover → Lessons Learned) turning it from a document into a practiced muscle memory.

## Key facts
- CSIRT roles typically include an Incident Commander, Security Analysts, Forensic Investigators, Legal/Compliance liaisons, and Communications leads
- A CSIRT differs from a SOC: the SOC monitors continuously; the CSIRT activates and leads formal incident response when a confirmed incident crosses a severity threshold
- CSIRTs must maintain a **contact list and escalation matrix** — knowing *who to call* in the first 15 minutes is as critical as technical skill
- First priority upon activation is **containment**, not eradication — preserving evidence and stopping spread before cleaning up
- Organizations can use internal CSIRTs, outsourced/retainer-based CSIRTs, or hybrid models; all should conduct tabletop exercises at least annually
