# Identity Theft

## What it is
Like a skilled forger stealing a master key and then carving a perfect copy to open every door the original owner could access — identity theft occurs when an attacker fraudulently acquires and uses another person's personally identifiable information (PII), such as SSN, date of birth, or financial credentials, to impersonate them. The goal is typically financial gain, unauthorized access, or evading accountability.

## Why it matters
In the 2017 Equifax breach, attackers exfiltrated PII on 147 million Americans — including SSNs, birth dates, and addresses. That data became raw material for synthetic identity fraud, where criminals combined real SSNs with fabricated names to open credit accounts that took victims years to unwind. This illustrates why PII exposure isn't just a privacy incident — it's a long-fuse financial weapon.

## Key facts
- **Synthetic identity fraud** combines real PII fragments (e.g., a valid SSN) with fake names — it's harder to detect than traditional identity theft because no single victim directly reports fraud
- Identity theft is enabled by **data breaches, phishing, social engineering, dumpster diving, and skimming** — spanning both digital and physical attack vectors
- Organizations defend against identity theft enablement through **data minimization, encryption at rest, and strict access controls** on PII databases
- The **FTC's Identity Theft Affidavit** is the U.S. standard recovery document — relevant in incident response and legal contexts
- Under **GDPR and HIPAA**, failure to protect PII/PHI that enables identity theft carries significant regulatory penalties, making this a compliance issue, not just an ethical one
