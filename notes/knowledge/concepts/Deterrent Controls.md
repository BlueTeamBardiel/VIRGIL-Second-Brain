# Deterrent Controls

## What it is
Like a "Beware of Dog" sign that may or may not back a real dog, a deterrent control discourages attackers by raising the *perceived* cost or risk of an attack — without necessarily stopping anything. Precisely defined: deterrent controls reduce the likelihood of a threat by influencing attacker behavior through psychological or visible signals, rather than through technical blocking.

## Why it matters
A financial institution posts visible security camera signage and displays login banners warning that "unauthorized access is monitored and prosecuted." A would-be attacker probing their systems sees these signals and moves to a softer target instead — the bank's actual defenses never had to fire. Deterrents work best as a first layer, buying time and redirecting threats before other controls engage.

## Key facts
- Deterrent controls are **not preventive** — they discourage rather than block; a determined attacker can ignore them entirely
- Common examples: warning banners, visible CCTV cameras, security guard presence, "armed response" signage, and published security policies
- On Security+, deterrent controls are classified under **administrative, physical, or technical** categories — a login warning banner is a *technical deterrent*
- Login warning banners serve a dual legal purpose: deterrence *and* removing the "I didn't know" defense in court, satisfying legal requirements for prosecution
- Deterrents are most effective against **opportunistic attackers** (low skill, low motivation) and nearly useless against determined, targeted threat actors
