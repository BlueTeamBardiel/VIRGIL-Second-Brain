# Committees

## What it is
Like a jury that must reach unanimous (or majority) verdict before executing someone, a committee in security is a governance structure where multiple stakeholders must collectively approve decisions, policies, or changes before they take effect. Examples include Change Advisory Boards (CABs), Security Steering Committees, and Risk Committees — formal bodies that distribute authority across roles rather than concentrating it in one person.

## Why it matters
In 2016, the Bangladesh Bank heist succeeded partly because a single point of authority in the SWIFT transfer process was exploited — had a proper multi-stakeholder committee reviewed large transfers, $81 million might not have vanished. Committees enforce separation of duties at the governance level, ensuring no single insider can unilaterally approve destructive or fraudulent actions.

## Key facts
- **Change Advisory Board (CAB)** reviews and approves IT changes to prevent unauthorized or risky modifications to production systems — directly supports change management in ITIL frameworks
- **Separation of duties** is enforced institutionally through committees; no single member can approve, implement, *and* audit the same action
- **Quorum requirements** (minimum members to vote) prevent decisions from being pushed through with a skeleton audience — a key anti-manipulation control
- **Security Steering Committees** typically include CISO, CIO, legal, and business unit leads — ensuring security decisions reflect risk tolerance across the whole organization
- Committees support **due diligence and due care** — documentation of collective decisions creates an audit trail demonstrating reasonable governance to regulators and auditors
