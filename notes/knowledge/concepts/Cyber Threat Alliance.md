# Cyber Threat Alliance

## What it is
Like competing hospitals sharing patient pathogen data so everyone can fight the same epidemic faster, the Cyber Threat Alliance (CTA) is a nonprofit consortium where rival cybersecurity vendors formally share threat intelligence with each other. Founded in 2014, members exchange malware samples, indicators of compromise (IOCs), and threat actor TTPs under a structured framework that requires reciprocal contribution. Think of it as a threat intelligence credit union — you get out what you put in.

## Why it matters
When the SamSam ransomware campaign was actively targeting healthcare and government networks, CTA members pooled IOCs and behavioral signatures that allowed collective defenses to be raised faster than any single vendor could achieve alone. Without this coordinated sharing, each vendor would have independently reverse-engineered the same malware while victims continued to be hit in the window of delay. The alliance compresses that detection-to-defense gap industry-wide.

## Key facts
- Founded by Palo Alto Networks, McAfee, Symantec, and Fortinet; membership has since grown to include dozens of major vendors
- Members must **contribute** threat data, not just consume it — the model enforces reciprocity to prevent free-riding
- Operates under a **structured sharing framework** using STIX/TAXII standards for machine-readable intelligence exchange
- Distinct from government-led ISACs (Information Sharing and Analysis Centers); CTA is **private-sector and vendor-focused**
- CTA published research on over **500,000 unique malware samples** within its first few years, demonstrating the scale advantage of collective intelligence
