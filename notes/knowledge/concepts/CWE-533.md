# CWE-533

## What it is
Like leaving your diary open on a park bench with your passwords scrawled in the margins — CWE-533 describes the exposure of sensitive information through server-side log files. Specifically, it occurs when an application writes confidential data (credentials, session tokens, PII) into logs that are not adequately protected or sanitized.

## Why it matters
In 2022, researchers discovered that Apache Log4j-vulnerable systems were logging attacker-controlled JNDI strings — but the deeper lesson was that many organizations also logged full HTTP request bodies, inadvertently storing plaintext passwords submitted via login forms. An attacker who compromised read access to log aggregation systems (like a misconfigured Splunk or ELK stack) could harvest thousands of credentials without ever touching the application database.

## Key facts
- CWE-533 is classified under the broader **CWE-532 (Insertion of Sensitive Information into Log File)** family; 533 specifically targets **server log files** rather than debug logs or error logs
- Common culprits include logging full request/response bodies, SQL query strings with embedded credentials, and JWT tokens in URL parameters that get captured in access logs
- OWASP explicitly lists "Insufficient Logging & Monitoring" as a top-10 risk; improper log content is the root cause, not just missing logs
- Mitigation requires **log scrubbing/masking** before write, **least-privilege access** to log storage, and encrypting log files at rest
- PCI-DSS Requirement 10 mandates audit log protection and prohibits logging full PANs (Primary Account Numbers) — a direct regulatory hook for this CWE
