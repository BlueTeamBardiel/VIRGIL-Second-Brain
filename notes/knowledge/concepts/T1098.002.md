# T1098.002

## What it is
Like a thief who doesn't pick the lock but instead adds their own key to the landlord's keyring — Exchange Account Manipulation grants persistent access by adding attacker-controlled accounts or permissions to Exchange email infrastructure, specifically assigning roles like ApplicationImpersonation to service accounts or backdoor identities.

## Why it matters
In the SolarWinds/SUNBURST campaign, attackers used compromised service accounts to grant themselves Exchange `ApplicationImpersonation` rights, allowing them to silently read any mailbox in the organization without triggering per-mailbox login alerts. Defenders who lacked Exchange audit logging missed months of email exfiltration because the access looked like normal service account behavior.

## Key facts
- **ApplicationImpersonation** is the highest-value target role — it lets one account read, send, and delete mail as any user in the organization
- Attackers commonly abuse **Exchange Web Services (EWS)** and the **Exchange Management Shell** to assign these roles programmatically
- This technique is frequently paired with **T1114 (Email Collection)** — persistence is the setup, exfiltration is the payoff
- Detection requires auditing **Role Based Access Control (RBAC) changes** in Exchange/M365 via the Unified Audit Log, specifically `New-ManagementRoleAssignment` events
- Microsoft 365 defenders should alert on service principals or non-admin accounts suddenly receiving `Exchange Administrator` or impersonation roles, especially outside business hours
