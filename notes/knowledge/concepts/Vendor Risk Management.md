# Vendor Risk Management

## What it is
Like hiring a contractor to renovate your kitchen — you're ultimately responsible if they cut corners and flood your neighbor's apartment. Vendor Risk Management (VRM) is the systematic process of identifying, assessing, and continuously monitoring the security risks introduced by third-party suppliers, partners, and service providers who access your systems, data, or infrastructure.

## Why it matters
The 2013 Target breach began not with Target itself, but through stolen credentials from Fazio Mechanical, an HVAC vendor with network access. Attackers pivoted from the vendor's foothold to Target's point-of-sale systems, exposing 40 million credit card numbers — a textbook example of why third-party access requires the same scrutiny as internal systems.

## Key facts
- **Right-to-audit clauses** in contracts give organizations legal authority to assess vendor security posture; without them, you're trusting without verifying
- VRM typically follows a tiered model: **Tier 1** vendors (critical/high data access) require full security assessments; lower tiers get questionnaires or passive reviews
- **SOC 2 Type II reports** are the standard artifact vendors provide to evidence their security controls over time (not just a point-in-time snapshot like Type I)
- The **Shared Responsibility Model** defines what the vendor secures vs. what the customer secures — critical for cloud providers like AWS or Azure
- Vendor onboarding should include a **Business Impact Analysis (BIA)** component to determine what operations fail if that vendor goes dark
