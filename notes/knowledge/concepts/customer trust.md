# customer trust

## What it is
Like a bank vault where customers hand you the combination — customer trust is the implicit agreement that an organization will handle sensitive data responsibly and securely. It represents the accumulated confidence users place in a company's ability to protect their personal, financial, and behavioral data from breach, misuse, or unauthorized disclosure.

## Why it matters
When Target suffered its 2013 POS malware breach (40 million credit cards compromised), the immediate financial damage was dwarfed by long-term trust erosion — Q4 profits dropped 46% as customers actively avoided stores. This illustrates that security failures don't just cost remediation dollars; they trigger customer exodus, regulatory scrutiny, and brand damage that can outlast the technical incident by years.

## Key facts
- **Breach notification laws** (GDPR Article 33, HIPAA Breach Notification Rule) exist precisely because regulators recognized that customers cannot protect themselves from harms they don't know about — transparency is now legally mandated, not optional
- **Third-party risk** is a top trust killer: organizations inherit reputational damage from vendor breaches even when their own controls are sound (see SolarWinds supply chain attack)
- **Privacy by design** (embedding data minimization and purpose limitation into systems from inception) is the engineering approach that operationalizes trust commitments
- **Mean Time to Disclose (MTTD)** is a key metric — delayed breach disclosure (e.g., Uber's 2016 breach hidden for a year) compounds legal liability and trust damage multiplicatively
- Trust is asymmetric: years to build, destroyed in a single incident — making proactive controls cheaper than reactive reputation repair
