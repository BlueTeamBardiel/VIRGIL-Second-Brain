# T1498.001

## What it is
Imagine thousands of fire hoses all aimed at a single garden spigot simultaneously — the pipe doesn't just overflow, it explodes. Direct Network Flood is a volumetric DDoS sub-technique where an attacker sends massive amounts of traffic directly from controlled hosts (botnets) to a target, overwhelming bandwidth or processing capacity without any amplification trick. The goal is pure saturation: consume all available resources so legitimate users receive nothing.

## Why it matters
During the 2016 Mirai botnet attacks, hundreds of thousands of compromised IoT devices launched direct UDP and TCP floods against Dyn DNS, taking down major websites including Twitter, Netflix, and Reddit for hours. Defenders who had deployed upstream traffic scrubbing centers (like Cloudflare or Akamai) were largely protected, while unprotected infrastructure buckled under terabit-scale traffic volumes.

## Key facts
- Uses **botnet-sourced traffic** directly — no third-party reflectors or amplifiers involved, unlike T1498.002
- Common flood types include **UDP floods, ICMP floods, SYN floods**, and HTTP GET floods
- Measured in **Gbps or Tbps** of bandwidth consumption; modern attacks regularly exceed 1 Tbps
- Defense relies on **rate limiting, blackhole routing (RTBH), BGP flowspec**, and CDN-based scrubbing
- Attackers prefer large botnets of IoT/OT devices because they have legitimate-looking IPs and limited security controls, making filtering harder
