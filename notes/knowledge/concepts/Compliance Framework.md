# Compliance Framework

## What it is
Think of a compliance framework like a building code for houses — it doesn't design your specific house, but it mandates the minimum safety standards every house must meet before anyone can live in it. A compliance framework is a structured set of guidelines, controls, and requirements that organizations follow to meet legal, regulatory, or industry security standards. Frameworks define *what* must be protected and *how* to demonstrate that protection to auditors and regulators.

## Why it matters
In 2013, Target's breach exposed 40 million credit card numbers — investigators found that while Target was technically PCI DSS compliant at audit time, they had failed to act on IDS alerts from a segmented vendor network. This case illustrates that compliance sets a *floor*, not a ceiling: meeting checkbox requirements doesn't guarantee security, but failing compliance audits can trigger massive fines, loss of payment processing rights, and legal liability.

## Key facts
- **PCI DSS** applies to any organization that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data — 12 core requirements, including network segmentation and encryption
- **HIPAA** mandates administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for Protected Health Information (PHI); violations can reach $1.9M per violation category per year
- **NIST CSF** (Cybersecurity Framework) organizes controls into five functions: **Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover** — commonly tested on Security+ and CySA+
- **SOC 2** is an auditing standard for service providers, evaluating Trust Service Criteria: security, availability, confidentiality, processing integrity, and privacy
- Compliance frameworks distinguish between **prescriptive** (HIPAA, PCI DSS — specific rules) and **risk-based** (NIST CSF, ISO 27001 — flexible, scalable controls)
