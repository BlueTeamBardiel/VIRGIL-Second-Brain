# T1496

## What it is
Like a squatter who breaks into your house not to steal your furniture, but to run an illegal bitcoin mine in your basement — Resource Hijacking is when an attacker compromises a system to consume its CPU, GPU, or bandwidth for their own financial gain. Precisely: T1496 describes adversaries abusing victim-owned compute resources to mine cryptocurrency or perform other resource-intensive tasks without authorization.

## Why it matters
In 2019, attackers compromised misconfigured Kubernetes clusters at Tesla and injected cryptomining malware that throttled CPU usage intentionally to stay under monitoring thresholds. The breach was discovered not through alerting but through a third-party security scan — demonstrating how resource hijacking is specifically engineered to evade detection while silently draining cloud compute costs.

## Key facts
- **Cryptojacking** is the most common implementation: malware like XMRig mines Monero because its algorithm (RandomX) is CPU-friendly and transaction tracing is difficult
- Attackers deliberately cap CPU usage (e.g., 50-70%) to avoid triggering performance alerts — stealth is the primary operational concern
- Cloud environments are high-value targets because victims pay per compute unit, meaning the attacker's gain directly maps to the victim's financial loss
- Indicators include sustained abnormal CPU/GPU spikes, unexpected outbound connections to mining pools (ports 3333, 4444, 14444), and degraded application performance
- This technique often follows Initial Access via exposed APIs, web shell deployment, or credential theft — it is a **post-exploitation** behavior, not an initial foothold method
