# GRC

## What it is
Think of GRC like the nervous system, skeleton, and rulebook of an organization all fused together — it tells the body what's allowed, gives it structure, and keeps it feeling pain when something goes wrong. Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) is the integrated framework through which an organization establishes policies (governance), identifies and manages threats to objectives (risk management), and adheres to laws, regulations, and standards (compliance). These three pillars work together to ensure security decisions align with business goals and legal obligations.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax suffered a breach exposing 147 million records — auditors later found that patch management policies existed but were never enforced, a direct GRC failure where governance was disconnected from operations. A mature GRC program would have tracked that Apache Struts vulnerability through a risk register, mandated patching timelines via policy, and verified compliance through continuous monitoring. The breach cost Equifax over $700 million in settlements.

## Key facts
- **Governance** defines *who* makes security decisions and *how* policies are created and enforced (e.g., security steering committees, CISO authority)
- **Risk Management** follows frameworks like NIST RMF or ISO 31000 — identify, assess, treat (accept/avoid/mitigate/transfer), and monitor risks
- **Compliance** maps to external mandates: PCI-DSS (payment card data), HIPAA (healthcare), SOX (financial reporting), GDPR (EU privacy)
- A **risk register** is the primary artifact of GRC — it catalogs risks with likelihood, impact, owner, and treatment status
- GRC tools (e.g., Archer, ServiceNow GRC) automate control tracking, evidence collection, and audit readiness
- Non-compliance penalties are concrete: GDPR fines reach **4% of annual global revenue** or €20M, whichever is higher
