# Risk Avoidance

## What it is
Like a restaurant that simply stops serving raw oysters after a hepatitis outbreak rather than improving food safety protocols, risk avoidance means eliminating the activity that creates the risk entirely. It is the deliberate decision to not engage in a business process, technology, or activity because the potential harm outweighs any conceivable benefit. Unlike mitigation, there is no residual risk — the threat surface is removed, not reduced.

## Why it matters
A healthcare company discovers their legacy telnet-based medical device management system cannot be patched and transmits patient credentials in cleartext. Rather than building compensating controls around an unfixable vulnerability, they decommission the devices entirely and replace them with a secure alternative — avoiding the risk rather than accepting ongoing exposure of PHI under HIPAA liability.

## Key facts
- Risk avoidance is one of four core risk response strategies: **avoid, transfer, mitigate (reduce), and accept** — expect all four on Security+ exams
- It is the **most complete** risk response because it eliminates the risk vector entirely, leaving zero residual risk from that specific threat
- Avoidance is often chosen when **cost of mitigation exceeds asset value** or when regulatory/legal liability makes any exposure unacceptable
- Common examples include: discontinuing a vulnerable service, prohibiting BYOD, or blocking entire geographic IP ranges from accessing systems
- Avoidance has a **business cost** — it typically means forfeiting functionality or revenue, making it inappropriate when the activity has high strategic value
