# piggybacking

## What it is
Like slipping through a secure door right behind someone who just badged in — smiling and carrying coffee so they hold it open — piggybacking is when an unauthorized person gains physical access to a restricted area by following an authorized person through a secured entry point, with the authorized person's knowledge or passive consent.

## Why it matters
In a 2019 penetration test at a financial firm, a red-team operative simply carried a large cardboard box to a badge-access door at shift change. A well-meaning employee held the door open, granting access to server rooms containing unencrypted backup drives. No technical exploit was needed — the human tendency to be polite defeated a $50,000 access control system.

## Key facts
- **Piggybacking vs. tailgating**: Piggybacking involves the authorized user's (implicit or explicit) consent; tailgating does not. Security+ treats these as distinct terms.
- The primary countermeasure is a **mantrap (airlock vestibule)** — a two-door entry system that allows only one person through at a time.
- Security awareness training is the most cost-effective preventive control; employees must be trained to *politely challenge* unknown followers.
- CCTV alone is a **detective**, not preventive, control — it records the breach but doesn't stop it.
- Piggybacking is categorized as a **social engineering** attack because it exploits human psychology (politeness, authority, distraction) rather than technical vulnerability.
