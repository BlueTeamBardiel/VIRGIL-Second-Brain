# Self-Assessment

## What it is
Like a doctor doing their own annual physical before recommending treatments to patients, self-assessment is an organization examining its own security posture internally — without hiring an outside auditor. Precisely, it is a structured internal review process where an organization evaluates its controls, policies, and practices against a defined standard or framework to identify gaps and measure compliance maturity.

## Why it matters
Before a healthcare provider undergoes a formal HIPAA audit, their security team conducts a self-assessment using the HHS Security Risk Assessment Tool to identify unencrypted PHI on laptops and misconfigured access controls. This internal discovery allows remediation before regulators find violations that carry fines up to $1.9 million per category annually. Without this proactive review, attackers or auditors find the gaps first.

## Key facts
- Self-assessments are **not independent** and therefore carry lower assurance than third-party audits — important distinction on CompTIA exams
- Frameworks like **NIST SP 800-53A** and **CMMC** explicitly include self-assessment tiers as a legitimate (but lower) form of compliance validation
- A **Security Risk Assessment (SRA)** is the most common self-assessment artifact, mapping threats to existing controls and quantifying residual risk
- Self-assessments feed directly into the **Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M)** document, which tracks identified weaknesses and remediation timelines
- Under **CMMC Level 1**, DoD contractors are permitted to self-assess annually rather than requiring a third-party assessor organization (C3PAO) — this changes at higher CMMC levels
