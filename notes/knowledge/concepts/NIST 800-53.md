# NIST 800-53

## What it is
Think of it as the master recipe book for federal kitchen security — every dish (system), every chef (agency), and every ingredient (control) must meet strict standards before food leaves the kitchen. Formally, NIST Special Publication 800-53 is a catalog of security and privacy controls for U.S. federal information systems, providing a structured framework organizations use to select safeguards based on system risk categorization defined by FIPS 199.

## Why it matters
In 2020, the SolarWinds supply chain attack compromised multiple federal agencies. Agencies with mature NIST 800-53 implementations — particularly controls in the SA (System and Services Acquisition) family requiring vendor software integrity verification — had documented processes that aided rapid detection and response, while gaps in those controls directly mapped to where attackers moved laterally undetected.

## Key facts
- **Organized into 20 control families** (e.g., AC = Access Control, IR = Incident Response, SI = System and Information Integrity), each containing individual controls and enhancements
- **Three baseline impact levels** — Low, Moderate, and High — derived from FIPS 199 categorization; higher impact = more controls required
- **Revision 5 (2020)** made controls outcome-based and technology-neutral, and integrated privacy controls directly into the catalog rather than keeping them separate
- **Mandatory for federal agencies** under FISMA; private organizations adopt it voluntarily but it's referenced in FedRAMP cloud authorization requirements
- **Works alongside the RMF** (Risk Management Framework, NIST 800-37) — 800-53 provides the control catalog; the RMF provides the process for selecting, implementing, and assessing them
