# LinkedIn

## What it is
Think of LinkedIn as a giant public bulletin board where professionals voluntarily pin their job title, employer, skills, colleagues, and career history — all neatly indexed and searchable. It is a professional social networking platform that aggregates organizational and personal career data, making it an exceptionally rich source for open-source intelligence (OSINT) gathering against individuals and enterprises.

## Why it matters
Before launching a spear phishing campaign, attackers routinely harvest LinkedIn to identify a target company's IT staff, software vendors, and internal project names — then craft emails impersonating the CFO requesting a wire transfer from the finance manager they found in three clicks. Defenders use the same platform to monitor for employees posting sensitive internal tool names or technology stacks that reveal attack surface details to adversaries.

## Key facts
- LinkedIn is one of the primary OSINT sources used during the **reconnaissance phase** of the cyber kill chain and MITRE ATT&CK (T1591 – Gather Victim Org Information)
- Attackers create **fake recruiter personas** on LinkedIn to deliver malware-laden "job offer" documents — a tactic used in the 2020 North Korean Lazarus Group campaigns targeting defense contractors
- Employee profiles reveal **technology stacks, internal project names, and org hierarchies** — all usable for pretexting and social engineering attacks
- LinkedIn data has been involved in large-scale **credential stuffing risk**: the 2012 breach exposed ~117 million SHA-1 unsalted password hashes, later sold in 2016
- Organizations should enforce **social media security policies** limiting what employees disclose about internal systems, roles, and vendors on public profiles
