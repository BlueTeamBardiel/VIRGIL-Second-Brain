# clean desk policy

## What it is
Think of a surgeon's operating table — every instrument is put away when not in use, because leaving a scalpel lying around is an accident waiting to happen. A clean desk policy is an organizational security control requiring employees to clear sensitive materials — documents, notes, removable media, access badges — from their workstations at the end of each workday or when leaving their workspace unattended.

## Why it matters
In 2011, a hospital was fined after a cleaning crew member photographed patient records left on a nurse's desk overnight and sold the data. A clean desk policy is a direct countermeasure against insider threats and physical tailgating attacks — a social engineer who gains brief physical access to an office harvests far less intelligence when desks are bare rather than covered in org charts, passwords on sticky notes, and printed network diagrams.

## Key facts
- Clean desk policy is classified as a **physical/administrative control**, not a technical one — it relies on human compliance and culture
- It pairs directly with a **clear screen policy**, requiring workstations to lock automatically (typically after 5–15 minutes of inactivity)
- Sensitive documents left on desks are a primary vector for **dumpster diving** if they migrate to trash bins without shredding
- HIPAA, PCI-DSS, and ISO 27001 all reference or imply clean desk practices as part of physical security compliance requirements
- Enforcement mechanisms include **unannounced walk-throughs** by security staff and documented disciplinary procedures — policy without enforcement is theater
