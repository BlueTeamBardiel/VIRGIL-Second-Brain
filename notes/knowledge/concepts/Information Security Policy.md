# Information Security Policy

## What it is
Think of an information security policy like a building's fire code: it doesn't put out fires itself, but it mandates exactly who installs sprinklers, which exits must stay unlocked, and what happens when an alarm sounds. Precisely, an Information Security Policy is a formal, management-approved document that establishes an organization's rules, responsibilities, and expectations for protecting information assets. It sits at the top of a three-tier policy hierarchy: policies → standards → procedures/guidelines.

## Why it matters
In 2020, Twitter suffered an insider-threat breach where employees with admin access were socially engineered into resetting high-profile accounts. A properly enforced information security policy mandating least-privilege access controls, mandatory security awareness training, and a formal privileged-access management program would have directly reduced the attack surface. Policies create the legal and procedural backbone that makes technical controls enforceable.

## Key facts
- **Three-tier hierarchy**: Policy (strategic "what/why") → Standard (mandatory "how much") → Procedure/Guideline (tactical "how-to")
- **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)** is the most commonly implemented sub-policy, governing how employees use organizational systems and data
- Policies must be **management-sanctioned** to carry authority — a policy without executive sign-off is unenforceable during disciplinary action
- **Review cycle matters**: Security+ expects you to know policies should be reviewed at least annually or after significant incidents
- Non-compliance consequences must be explicitly stated; without defined penalties, policies are recommendations, not controls
