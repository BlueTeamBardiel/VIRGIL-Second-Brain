# SOX

## What it is
Think of SOX like a mandatory receipts-and-auditors rule for publicly traded companies — imagine if every restaurant had to prove, by law, that their cash register totals matched their bank deposits, with an independent inspector verifying the math. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002) is a U.S. federal law requiring publicly traded companies to implement strict internal controls over financial reporting and mandating executive accountability for the accuracy of financial statements. It was passed directly in response to the Enron and WorldCom accounting fraud scandals.

## Why it matters
A disgruntled IT administrator at a public company modifies financial database records to cover up embezzlement — SOX compliance requires audit logging, access controls, and separation of duties that would detect this tampering and preserve the forensic trail. Under SOX Section 404, companies must annually assess their internal controls, which forces IT teams to implement immutable audit logs, privileged access management, and change management procedures. Failure to comply carries criminal penalties including fines and imprisonment for executives.

## Key facts
- **Section 302**: CEOs and CFOs must personally certify the accuracy of financial reports — criminal liability if they knowingly sign false statements
- **Section 404**: Requires annual assessment of internal controls over financial reporting (ICFR), typically involving IT systems and databases
- **Retention requirement**: Financial records must be retained for **7 years**; destruction of audit-relevant records is a criminal offense
- **IT controls targeted**: Access controls, audit logging, change management, and segregation of duties are the primary technical focus areas
- **Auditor independence**: External auditors cannot provide certain non-audit services to companies they audit, preventing conflicts of interest
