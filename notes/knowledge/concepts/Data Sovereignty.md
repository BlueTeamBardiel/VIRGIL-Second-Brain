# Data sovereignty

## What it is
Think of data like a person traveling internationally — the moment they land in France, French laws apply to them, regardless of what country issued their passport. Data sovereignty is the legal principle that data is subject to the laws and governance of the nation where it is physically stored or processed, not necessarily where the owner is headquartered. A U.S. company storing customer data on servers in Germany must comply with German and EU law, full stop.

## Why it matters
In 2013, Microsoft was served a U.S. warrant demanding emails stored on servers in Ireland. Microsoft challenged this precisely because complying would violate Irish and EU data protection law — the data was sovereign to the EU. This case (Microsoft Corp. v. United States) directly led to the CLOUD Act (2018), which created a legal framework for cross-border data access requests, illustrating how sovereignty conflicts can paralyze incident response and legal compliance simultaneously.

## Key facts
- **GDPR (EU)** mandates that personal data on EU citizens cannot leave the EU without adequate legal protections (Standard Contractual Clauses, adequacy decisions, etc.)
- **CLOUD Act (2018)** allows U.S. law enforcement to compel U.S.-based providers to hand over data regardless of where it's stored
- Data residency (where data *lives*) and data sovereignty (what *laws govern* it) are related but distinct concepts
- Many nations now require **data localization** — storing data within national borders — as a sovereignty enforcement mechanism (e.g., Russia's Federal Law No. 242-FZ)
- Sovereignty conflicts are a primary driver of **multi-cloud and regional cloud deployment strategies** in enterprise architecture
