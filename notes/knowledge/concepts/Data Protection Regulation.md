# Data Protection Regulation

## What it is
Think of it like a building code for skyscrapers — nobody gets to decide *if* they'll follow structural safety rules, only *how* they'll comply. Data protection regulations are legally binding frameworks that mandate how organizations collect, store, process, and dispose of personal data, with enforceable penalties for violations.

## Why it matters
In 2019, British Airways was fined £183 million under GDPR after attackers skimmed payment card data from ~500,000 customers through a compromised booking script — a supply chain attack. The regulation forced accountability: BA couldn't just quietly patch the breach and move on; they had mandatory breach notification within 72 hours and faced proportional financial consequences for inadequate controls.

## Key facts
- **GDPR** (EU, 2018) mandates breach notification within **72 hours** of discovery and allows fines up to **4% of global annual revenue** or €20 million, whichever is greater
- **CCPA** (California, 2020) grants consumers the right to know, delete, and opt out of the sale of their personal data — key distinction from GDPR's consent-first model
- **HIPAA** governs Protected Health Information (PHI) in the U.S., requiring safeguards across administrative, physical, and technical domains
- **Data minimization** is a core principle across most frameworks: collect only what is necessary for a stated, lawful purpose
- **PCI-DSS** is technically a contractual standard (not law), but violations can result in fines from card networks and loss of processing privileges — often confused with statutory regulation
