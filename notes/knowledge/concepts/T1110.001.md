# T1110.001

## What it is
Like a locksmith methodically trying every key on a massive keyring until one finally turns the tumbler, password spraying's opposite — brute force password attack — tries every possible combination against a single account until the correct password is found. Precisely: T1110.001 is a credential access technique where an adversary systematically attempts all possible character combinations (or a wordlist) against one or more accounts until authentication succeeds.

## Why it matters
In the 2016 San Bernardino case, the FBI's legal battle with Apple centered on the inability to brute force an iPhone PIN without triggering the wipe mechanism — illustrating that lockout policies and hardware-enforced attempt limits are the primary defensive countermeasure. Defenders monitor for rapid sequential authentication failures against a single account as the core detection signal.

## Key facts
- **Online brute force** targets live authentication services (SSH, RDP, web logins); **offline brute force** cracks captured password hashes with no lockout risk
- Account lockout policies (e.g., 5 attempts → 30-minute lockout) are the primary mitigation for online attacks
- Tools commonly associated: Hydra, Medusa, Hashcat (offline), John the Ripper (offline)
- Offline attacks against weak hashing algorithms (MD5, unsalted SHA-1) can crack billions of hashes per second using GPU acceleration
- **Dictionary attacks** (wordlist-based) are a sub-variant of brute force, not a separate MITRE technique — both fall under T1110.001
