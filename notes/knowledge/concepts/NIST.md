# NIST

## What it is
Think of NIST as the FDA for cybersecurity — a federal agency that doesn't enforce laws but publishes the authoritative "nutrition labels" that the entire industry reads and follows. The National Institute of Standards and Technology is a U.S. Department of Commerce agency that develops voluntary cybersecurity frameworks, guidelines, and standards used by government agencies and private organizations worldwide.

## Why it matters
When a hospital gets hit with ransomware, incident responders don't improvise — they follow NIST SP 800-61 (Computer Security Incident Handling Guide), which prescribes the exact phases: Preparation → Detection & Analysis → Containment, Eradication & Recovery → Post-Incident Activity. Without this standardized playbook, every organization would handle breaches differently, making coordination between agencies and vendors chaotic and inconsistent.

## Key facts
- **NIST CSF (Cybersecurity Framework)** organizes security into five core functions: **Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover** — a favorite on Security+ exams
- **NIST SP 800-53** provides security and privacy controls for federal information systems; compliance is mandatory for agencies under FISMA
- **NIST SP 800-171** governs protection of Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) in non-federal systems — critical for defense contractors
- **NIST SP 800-30** covers risk assessment methodology, feeding directly into risk management frameworks
- NIST publications are **free, voluntary standards** (unless you're a federal contractor — then they become contractually mandatory)
