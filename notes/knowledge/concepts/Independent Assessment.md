# Independent Assessment

## What it is
Like hiring a home inspector who has no financial stake in whether you buy the house, an independent assessment brings in evaluators with no organizational ties to the system being reviewed. Precisely: it is a security evaluation conducted by a third party (or internal team with no ownership of the tested environment) to provide an unbiased measurement of security posture, control effectiveness, or compliance status.

## Why it matters
In 2020, the SolarWinds breach exposed a critical failure: internal teams had blind spots to their own supply chain weaknesses. A truly independent third-party assessment — with no vendor relationships or internal politics — would have been better positioned to scrutinize build pipeline integrity without the organizational pressure to downplay findings. Independent assessments are now explicitly required in many federal frameworks (FedRAMP, FISMA) specifically because insiders cannot objectively audit themselves.

## Key facts
- The **FedRAMP program** mandates a 3PAO (Third-Party Assessment Organization) to conduct independent assessments before cloud services receive government authorization
- **NIST SP 800-53A** defines independence as freedom from conflicts of interest — assessors must not have designed, implemented, or managed the controls they are testing
- Independent assessments produce an **SAR (Security Assessment Report)**, a formal artifact that feeds into authorization decisions and POA&Ms
- Security+/CySA+ distinguishes between **self-assessments** (internal, lower assurance) and **independent assessments** (external or structurally separated, higher assurance)
- **Attestation** is the formal declaration from an independent assessor confirming evaluated security controls meet specified requirements — it carries legal and contractual weight
