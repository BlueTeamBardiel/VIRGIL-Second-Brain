# data breaches

## What it is
Like a thief who doesn't just pick your pocket but photographs your entire wallet — account numbers, IDs, everything — a data breach is the unauthorized acquisition of sensitive information, not just access to a system. Precisely: a data breach occurs when confidential data is exposed to, copied by, or transmitted to an unauthorized party, whether through external attack, insider threat, or misconfiguration.

## Why it matters
In 2013, Target suffered a breach where attackers entered through a third-party HVAC vendor's credentials, ultimately exfiltrating 40 million credit card numbers and 70 million customer records. This incident demonstrates why supply chain access controls and network segmentation are critical — the attacker never needed to directly compromise Target's core systems. It also triggered mandatory PCI-DSS audits and reshaped how organizations evaluate third-party risk.

## Key facts
- The **breach notification rule** under HIPAA requires covered entities to notify affected individuals within 60 days of discovering a breach involving PHI
- Breaches are classified by **data sensitivity**: PII, PHI, PCI data, and intellectual property each trigger different regulatory obligations
- **Dwell time** — the gap between breach occurrence and detection — averaged 204 days globally (IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report), meaning attackers often exfiltrate data long before detection
- The **three vectors** most commonly exploited: stolen credentials, phishing, and misconfigured cloud storage (e.g., exposed S3 buckets)
- Under **GDPR**, organizations must notify supervisory authorities within **72 hours** of becoming aware of a breach — far stricter than U.S. standards
