# T1078.002

## What it is
Like a thief who steals a master key to a hotel and walks straight past the front desk with a smile, domain account abuse lets attackers use legitimate Active Directory credentials to move through an enterprise network without raising alarms. Precisely, T1078.002 refers to adversaries leveraging valid domain accounts — accounts authenticated against a Windows domain controller — to access systems, persist in an environment, or escalate privileges while blending into normal user activity.

## Why it matters
During the SolarWinds supply chain attack, threat actors obtained domain account credentials and used them to move laterally across victim networks for months, evading detection because their activity looked indistinguishable from legitimate administrator behavior. Defenders who implement tiered administrative account models and monitor for anomalous logon times or locations can catch this technique even when credentials are valid.

## Key facts
- Domain accounts are managed by Active Directory and authenticated via Kerberos or NTLM, making stolen credentials immediately useful across *all* systems in the domain
- Attackers commonly obtain domain credentials through credential dumping (e.g., `lsass.exe` via Mimikatz), phishing, or password spraying
- Detection focus: look for logons at unusual hours, logons from new source IPs, or a single account authenticating to an abnormally high number of hosts (lateral movement indicator)
- Privileged domain accounts (Domain Admins) are high-value targets — compromise of one can mean full domain takeover
- Mitigations include Privileged Access Workstations (PAWs), least privilege enforcement, and enabling Windows Event IDs 4624/4625/4648 for logon auditing
