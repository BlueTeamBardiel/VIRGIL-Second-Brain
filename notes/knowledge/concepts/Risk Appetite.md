# Risk Appetite

## What it is
Like a casino deciding exactly how much money it's willing to lose on a single hand before the dealer walks away, risk appetite is an organization's formal declaration of how much risk it's willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives. It is a strategic, board-level decision that defines the upper boundary of tolerable exposure before action is required. Risk appetite is broader than risk tolerance, which describes the acceptable variance around specific thresholds.

## Why it matters
A healthcare company with a low risk appetite for data breaches might spend heavily on zero-trust architecture and endpoint detection, refusing to deploy any third-party SaaS tool that stores PHI without SOC 2 Type II certification. When a vendor proposes a cost-saving cloud migration, the security team can immediately reject configurations that exceed the stated appetite — no ambiguity, no endless committee debates. Without this declaration, security decisions become reactive, inconsistent, and politically driven.

## Key facts
- Risk appetite is set by **executive leadership or the board**; it is a governance document, not an IT decision
- It is expressed **qualitatively** (e.g., "we avoid risks that could cause regulatory fines") or **quantitatively** (e.g., "maximum acceptable annual loss exposure is $2M")
- Risk appetite drives **security control selection**: a high-risk appetite organization may accept unpatched legacy systems; a low-risk appetite organization patches within 24 hours of critical CVE disclosure
- Distinct from **risk tolerance** (narrow operational variance) and **risk threshold** (the specific point triggering escalation)
- On Security+/CySA+ exams, risk appetite appears in questions about **risk management frameworks**, **policy development**, and distinguishing it from risk tolerance or risk acceptance
