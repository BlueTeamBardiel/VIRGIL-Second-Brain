# risk tolerance

## What it is
Like a tightrope walker who decides exactly how far they'll lean before grabbing the safety rope, risk tolerance is an organization's pre-defined threshold for how much risk it will accept before taking action. It defines the boundary between risks worth living with and risks requiring mitigation, transfer, or avoidance. Risk tolerance sits within a broader risk appetite statement and is usually expressed as quantifiable limits (e.g., acceptable downtime, maximum financial loss).

## Why it matters
A hospital might have near-zero risk tolerance for patient data breaches due to HIPAA liability, yet moderate tolerance for website defacement. When a ransomware group targets their network, this tolerance directly determines the incident response decision: pay the ransom or restore from backups even if it means 72 hours of downtime. An organization that never defined its tolerance will make this call under panic rather than policy.

## Key facts
- Risk tolerance is **narrower** than risk appetite — appetite is strategic direction, tolerance is the specific measurable limit
- Organizations typically express tolerance through **key risk indicators (KRIs)** that trigger escalation when thresholds are crossed
- Exceeding risk tolerance does **not** mean the risk is unmanaged — it means the response protocol automatically activates
- **Regulatory environments** (PCI-DSS, HIPAA, SOX) often impose external floors on risk tolerance, overriding internal preference
- Risk tolerance is revisited during **business impact analysis (BIA)** to align with updated recovery time objectives (RTO) and recovery point objectives (RPO)
