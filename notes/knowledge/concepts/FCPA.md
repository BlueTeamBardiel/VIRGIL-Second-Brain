# FCPA

## What it is
Think of it as the "no bribery passport" for American companies doing business abroad — if a U.S. company slips cash to a foreign official to win a contract, the U.S. government can prosecute them even if the bribe happened in another country. The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) is a U.S. federal law enacted in 1977 that prohibits U.S. persons and entities from bribing foreign government officials to obtain or retain business, and mandates accurate books and records to prevent concealment of corrupt payments.

## Why it matters
In cybersecurity contexts, FCPA violations frequently surface during forensic investigations — attackers who compromise financial systems may expose hidden slush fund accounts used to disguise bribes as "consulting fees," triggering both a breach response and a federal corruption investigation simultaneously. Organizations must maintain audit trails and financial controls robust enough to satisfy FCPA's accounting provisions, meaning log integrity and access controls around financial systems are compliance requirements, not just security best practices.

## Key facts
- **Two main provisions**: Anti-bribery (no paying foreign officials) and Accounting (accurate books, internal controls, no off-the-books accounts)
- **Enforced jointly** by the DOJ (criminal) and SEC (civil) — penalties can exceed $1 billion in major cases (e.g., Siemens paid $800M+ in 2008)
- **Third-party liability**: Companies are liable for bribes paid through intermediaries, agents, or subsidiaries — due diligence on vendors is legally required
- **Applies extraterritorially** — a U.S. company's foreign subsidiary can trigger FCPA liability even for acts entirely outside U.S. borders
- **Whistleblower protections** exist under Dodd-Frank for FCPA reporters, creating insider threat and data exfiltration risk scenarios around sensitive financial disclosures
