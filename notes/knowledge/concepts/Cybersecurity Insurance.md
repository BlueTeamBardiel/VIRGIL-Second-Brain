# Cybersecurity insurance

## What it is
Like a seatbelt that doesn't prevent the crash but limits how badly you're broken afterward, cybersecurity insurance is a financial risk-transfer mechanism that reimburses organizations for losses stemming from data breaches, ransomware attacks, business interruption, and regulatory fines. It doesn't replace security controls — it prices and partially absorbs residual risk that controls couldn't eliminate.

## Why it matters
When Colonial Pipeline paid $4.4 million in ransomware to DarkSide in 2021, cybersecurity insurance covered a significant portion of the recovery costs, including forensic investigation and legal fees. Insurers increasingly require proof of MFA, endpoint detection, and incident response plans before issuing policies — meaning insurance now *drives* baseline security hygiene across industries.

## Key facts
- **Four risk treatment options**: Accept, Avoid, Mitigate, **Transfer** — insurance is the primary method of *transferring* risk to a third party (exam-critical distinction)
- Policies typically cover: breach notification costs, legal liability, ransomware payments, business interruption losses, and credit monitoring for affected customers
- Policies typically **exclude**: acts of war (the NotPetya "act of war" exclusion disputes cost Merck ~$1.4B in litigation), insider fraud, and pre-existing vulnerabilities
- Insurers conduct **security posture assessments** before coverage; lack of MFA or unpatched critical systems can result in denial of claims or policy cancellation
- Premiums surged 50–100% after the 2020–2021 ransomware wave, forcing organizations to treat cyber risk quantitatively (see FAIR model)
