# BCP

## What it is
Like a hospital that has generators, backup blood supplies, and pre-assigned emergency roles *before* a disaster strikes, a Business Continuity Plan is an organization's pre-built survival playbook. It is a documented strategy that ensures critical business functions can continue operating during and after a disruptive event, covering people, processes, and technology. BCP is broader than disaster recovery — it addresses the whole organization staying alive, not just restoring IT systems.

## Why it matters
In 2021, the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack forced a shutdown of fuel distribution across the US East Coast. Organizations with mature BCPs had pre-identified alternate suppliers, manual fallback procedures, and crisis communication chains — reducing downtime from weeks to hours. Those without them scrambled reactively, suffering far greater operational and financial losses.

## Key facts
- **BCP ≠ DRP**: Business Continuity Plan covers the entire organization's operational survival; Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP) is a *subset* focused specifically on restoring IT systems and data.
- **BIA drives BCP**: The Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is the foundation — it identifies critical functions, **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective) and **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective) targets.
- **RTO** = maximum tolerable downtime before resuming operations; **RPO** = maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.
- BCP must be **tested regularly** — accepted test types include tabletop exercises, simulations, parallel testing, and full interruption tests.
- **MTTR** (Mean Time to Repair) and **MTBF** (Mean Time Between Failures) are metrics used to refine BCP effectiveness over time.
