# CWE-327

## What it is
Like hiring a locksmith who uses a master key design from 1970 that burglars have long since reverse-engineered, using a broken or risky cryptographic algorithm means relying on protection that attackers already know how to defeat. CWE-327 describes the use of cryptographic algorithms that are known to be weak, deprecated, or fundamentally flawed — such as MD5, SHA-1, DES, or RC4 — where stronger alternatives exist. The vulnerability isn't a bug in implementation; it's a bad choice of weapon before the fight begins.

## Why it matters
In 2017, the WannaCry ransomware campaign exploited systems partly enabled by years of legacy crypto debt — organizations running outdated cipher suites couldn't enforce strong TLS configurations that would have limited lateral movement and C2 communication. Defenders patching CWE-327 in their TLS configurations by disabling RC4 and enforcing TLS 1.2+ directly reduces the attack surface for downgrade and BEAST-style attacks.

## Key facts
- **DES** uses a 56-bit key, brute-forceable in under 24 hours with modern hardware; replaced by AES (128/256-bit)
- **MD5 and SHA-1** are collision-vulnerable — attackers can forge certificates or file hashes; NIST deprecated SHA-1 for digital signatures in 2011
- **RC4** has statistical biases in its keystream that allow plaintext recovery; banned in TLS by RFC 7465 (2015)
- CWE-327 is distinct from CWE-326 (Inadequate Encryption Strength) — 327 is about *which algorithm*, 326 is about *key length*
- NIST's approved algorithm list (FIPS 140-2/140-3) is the authoritative reference for acceptable cryptographic choices in compliance contexts
