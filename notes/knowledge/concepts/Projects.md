# Projects

## What it is
Like a construction blueprint that organizes every trade — plumbing, electrical, framing — into one coordinated plan, a security project is a time-bounded, goal-driven initiative that organizes people, tools, and processes to deliver a specific security outcome. Unlike ongoing operations, projects have defined start/end dates, scope, and success criteria. In security contexts, this includes initiatives like deploying a SIEM, conducting a red team engagement, or implementing zero trust architecture.

## Why it matters
When a healthcare organization suffers a ransomware attack and must deploy endpoint detection and response (EDR) across 5,000 devices, that remediation effort is structured as a project — with a project manager, milestones, risk register, and stakeholder sign-offs. Without proper project discipline, rushed deployments leave misconfigured agents, coverage gaps, and undocumented exceptions that attackers exploit in the next incident.

## Key facts
- Security projects follow structured frameworks such as **PMBOK** or **PRINCE2**, and must align with the organization's risk appetite and business objectives
- A **project charter** formally authorizes the project and identifies the sponsor, scope, and high-level resources — it's the first document created
- **Scope creep** is a major project risk in security: adding unplanned requirements mid-project delays delivery and inflates cost, often leaving critical controls incomplete
- Security projects should include a **lessons learned** phase post-completion, feeding findings back into the risk management process
- Projects differ from **programs** (collections of related projects) and **operations** (ongoing, repeating processes) — Security+ tests this distinction explicitly
