# Log Management

## What it is
Think of log management as a ship's black box recorder — it captures everything that happens across your systems so that when something goes wrong, you can reconstruct exactly what occurred and when. Precisely, log management is the systematic process of collecting, aggregating, storing, normalizing, and disposing of log data generated by operating systems, applications, and network devices. It transforms raw, chaotic event records into organized, queryable data for security analysis and compliance.

## Why it matters
During the 2020 SolarWinds breach, attackers deliberately disabled and tampered with Windows Event Logs on compromised hosts to cover their lateral movement for months. Organizations with centralized, immutable log storage (off the compromised host) were able to detect anomalies that local log inspection would have missed entirely — demonstrating that *where* you store logs is as critical as *whether* you store them.

## Key facts
- **Log rotation and retention**: Most compliance frameworks (PCI DSS, HIPAA) require log retention of **90 days online, 1 year archived** — knowing these numbers matters for exam scenarios.
- **Syslog** (UDP/TCP port 514) is the dominant protocol for forwarding logs from network devices; syslog-ng and rsyslog are common implementations.
- **Log normalization** converts disparate log formats (Windows Event IDs vs. Linux syslog vs. firewall logs) into a common schema — essential before feeding data into a SIEM.
- **Integrity protection**: Logs must be protected from tampering; write-once storage or cryptographic hashing of log files supports non-repudiation in forensic investigations.
- **Windows Security Event ID 4624** (successful logon) and **4625** (failed logon) are critical IDs for detecting brute-force and unauthorized access — these appear frequently on CySA+ exams.
