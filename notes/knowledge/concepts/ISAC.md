# ISAC

## What it is
Think of it as a neighborhood watch program, but for entire industries — where hospitals, banks, or power companies share break-in reports with each other so everyone can lock their doors before the burglar reaches their street. An Information Sharing and Analysis Center (ISAC) is a sector-specific nonprofit organization that facilitates the collection, analysis, and bidirectional sharing of threat intelligence between private organizations and government entities within a critical infrastructure sector.

## Why it matters
In 2021, when the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack hit, the downstream effect on fuel distribution highlighted how a single breach in the energy sector could cascade across the entire economy. The E-ISAC (Electricity ISAC) and downstream partners used shared indicators of compromise (IoCs) to help other energy operators harden their environments against the same DarkSide ransomware TTPs before they could be victimized — a textbook example of collective defense in action.

## Key facts
- ISACs are established under Presidential Decision Directive 63 (PDD-63, 1998) and reinforced by the National Infrastructure Protection Plan (NIPP)
- Each critical infrastructure sector has its own ISAC — FS-ISAC (financial), H-ISAC (healthcare), MS-ISAC (state/local government), E-ISAC (energy), and more
- MS-ISAC is particularly relevant for Security+/CySA+ — it provides free threat intelligence services to state, local, tribal, and territorial (SLTT) governments
- Information shared through ISACs is protected from FOIA requests and antitrust liability under the Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act (CISA) of 2015
- ISACs often use the Traffic Light Protocol (TLP) to classify how widely shared threat data can be redistributed
