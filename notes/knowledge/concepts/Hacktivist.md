# Hacktivist

## What it is
Like a protest march that breaks windows instead of just carrying signs, a hacktivist uses hacking techniques not for financial gain but to broadcast a political or social message. Precisely defined: a hacktivist is a threat actor who conducts cyberattacks — defacements, DDoS, data leaks — motivated by ideological, political, or social causes rather than profit. The word blends "hack" and "activism."

## Why it matters
In 2022, the group Anonymous declared cyberwar on Russia following the invasion of Ukraine, taking down Russian government websites and leaking data from state-owned broadcasters. Defenders must recognize that hacktivist attacks often prioritize *visibility and embarrassment* over stealth, meaning public-facing web infrastructure (websites, social media accounts) becomes the primary attack surface requiring hardened DDoS protection and defacement monitoring.

## Key facts
- **Primary motivation**: Ideological/political — NOT financial gain (distinguishes them from cybercriminals) and NOT espionage (distinguishes them from nation-state actors)
- **Common TTPs**: Website defacement, Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS), doxxing, and unauthorized data dumps/leaks to embarrass targets
- **Sophistication level**: Generally low-to-medium; often rely on commodity tools (LOIC — Low Orbit Ion Cannon) and publicly known exploits rather than zero-days
- **Notable groups**: Anonymous, LulzSec, and GhostSec are classic Security+ examples used in exam scenarios
- **Attribution challenge**: Hacktivist groups are loosely organized and decentralized ("leaderless"), making attribution and legal prosecution extremely difficult
