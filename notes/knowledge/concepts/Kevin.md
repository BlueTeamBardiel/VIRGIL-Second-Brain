# Kevin

## What it is
Like a locksmith's apprentice who learned every trick of the trade by sneaking into places he shouldn't have been, Kevin Mitnick was the most notorious social engineer and hacker of the 20th century. Precisely, he represents the archetype of the human-element attacker — someone who exploited trust, impersonation, and psychological manipulation far more than technical exploits to compromise systems and networks.

## Why it matters
Mitnick's 1994–1995 hacking spree — where he compromised Pacific Bell, Motorola, Nokia, and Sun Microsystems largely through phone-based pretexting — demonstrated that technical controls alone are insufficient. His techniques directly shaped modern security awareness training curricula and the formal recognition of social engineering as a primary attack vector in frameworks like NIST SP 800-50.

## Key facts
- Mitnick was arrested in 1995 after hacker Tsutomu Shimomura traced his IP; he served nearly five years in federal prison, including time in solitary because authorities feared he could "launch nuclear missiles by whistling into a payphone"
- His primary weapons were **pretexting** (impersonating IT staff, executives, or vendors) and **dumpster diving** for credentials and internal documentation
- After release, he became a legitimate penetration tester and authored *The Art of Deception* (2002) — a foundational text for Security+ social engineering concepts
- His attacks demonstrated **privilege escalation through social means**: convincing low-level employees to reveal credentials that unlocked higher-access systems
- Mitnick's case directly influenced the **Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)** enforcement priorities and sentencing guidelines for cyber crimes
