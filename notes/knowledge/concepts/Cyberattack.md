# cyberattack

## What it is
Like a burglar who might pick a lock, smash a window, or tailgate an employee through a badge door — the *method* varies wildly, but the goal is unauthorized access to something valuable. A cyberattack is any deliberate, malicious action targeting the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of an information system, network, or data. Attacks can originate from external threat actors, insiders, or nation-states, and range from automated script-kiddie scans to multi-stage APT campaigns.

## Why it matters
In the 2021 Colonial Pipeline incident, attackers used a single compromised VPN password (no MFA) to deploy ransomware, shutting down fuel delivery to the U.S. East Coast for six days. This illustrates how a technically simple attack vector — credential theft — can cascade into a critical infrastructure crisis, making both technical controls and policy decisions life-or-death matters.

## Key facts
- Cyberattacks are classified by **impact type**: confidentiality (data breach), integrity (data manipulation), or availability (DoS/ransomware) — mapped to the CIA Triad
- The **attack surface** is everything exposed to a potential attacker: open ports, user accounts, APIs, physical access points
- MITRE ATT&CK framework categorizes real-world attack **tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs)** used by threat actors — foundational for CySA+
- Attacks are often described in phases using the **Cyber Kill Chain**: Reconnaissance → Weaponization → Delivery → Exploitation → Installation → C2 → Actions on Objectives
- **Motive** matters for attribution and defense: financial (ransomware gangs), espionage (APTs), hacktivism, or insider grievance each suggest different target profiles and TTPs
