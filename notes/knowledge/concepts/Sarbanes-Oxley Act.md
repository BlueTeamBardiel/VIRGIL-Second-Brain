# Sarbanes-Oxley Act

## What it is
Think of SOX like a mandatory receipts-and-audits rule for a restaurant kitchen — every financial ingredient that goes in or out must be logged, traceable, and verified by someone independent. Formally, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002) is a U.S. federal law requiring publicly traded companies to maintain accurate financial records, implement internal controls, and hold executives personally accountable for the integrity of financial reporting. It was enacted after the Enron and WorldCom accounting scandals destroyed billions in investor value.

## Why it matters
A disgruntled sysadmin at a publicly traded firm deletes financial transaction logs stored on corporate servers — under SOX Section 802, this constitutes destruction of audit records and is a federal crime carrying up to 20 years in prison. Security teams must implement tamper-evident logging, log retention policies (minimum 7 years for financial records), and access controls around financial systems specifically to demonstrate SOX compliance during audits. A failed SOX audit can result in executive criminal liability, not just fines.

## Key facts
- **Section 302**: CEOs and CFOs must personally certify the accuracy of financial reports — creating direct executive accountability for IT controls.
- **Section 404**: Requires annual assessment of internal controls over financial reporting, which directly drives IT security control documentation.
- **Section 802**: Criminalizes alteration, destruction, or falsification of financial records — mandates audit log integrity.
- **Retention requirement**: Financial records and audit logs must be retained for **7 years**.
- SOX compliance is enforced by the **SEC** and audited by independent public accounting firms; non-compliance can trigger securities delisting.
