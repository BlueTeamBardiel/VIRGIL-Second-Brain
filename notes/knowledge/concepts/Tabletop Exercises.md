# Tabletop Exercises

## What it is
Like a military war game played with maps and miniatures instead of live ammunition, a tabletop exercise is a discussion-based simulation where stakeholders talk through their responses to a hypothetical security incident — no systems are touched, no actual recovery actions are taken. It tests whether your incident response plan *works on paper* before you need it to work in a burning building.

## Why it matters
During the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack, many organizations realized their incident response plans had never been verbally walked through with executives, legal teams, and PR simultaneously. A tabletop exercise would have surfaced gaps like "who actually authorizes paying a ransom?" or "does legal need to be on every call?" before a real crisis forced those answers under pressure.

## Key facts
- Tabletop exercises are **discussion-based**, not operational — no live systems, no actual failover or recovery actions are executed (distinguishing them from functional or full-scale drills)
- They involve **cross-functional stakeholders**: IT, legal, HR, executive leadership, and communications — not just the security team
- The goal is to identify **gaps in the IRP (Incident Response Plan)**, communication breakdowns, and unclear decision authority *before* an incident
- They are explicitly listed under **NIST SP 800-84** and referenced in **NIST SP 800-61** as a training and preparedness method
- On Security+/CySA+ exams, tabletop exercises fall under the **"Prepare" phase** of incident response and are categorized as a **plan review technique**, not a test of technical controls
