# Security Policy

## What it is
Think of a security policy like the rulebook posted on the wall of a chemistry lab — it tells everyone what protective gear is required, what chemicals can't be mixed, and what happens if you break the rules, before anyone even picks up a beaker. Formally, a security policy is a documented set of rules, expectations, and procedures that govern how an organization protects its information assets and manages risk. It translates management's security intent into enforceable standards that guide technical controls, user behavior, and incident response.

## Why it matters
In 2021, a Colonial Pipeline employee reused credentials from a legacy VPN account that lacked MFA — a direct policy failure around password management and access control standards. A strong security policy mandating MFA for all remote access would have created the technical requirement that, if enforced, could have prevented the $4.4 million ransomware payout. Policy isn't just paperwork; it's the blueprint that controls either get built from or ignored.

## Key facts
- Security policies operate in a hierarchy: **Policy → Standard → Guideline → Procedure** — policies are mandatory, guidelines are advisory
- A **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)** is one of the most common policy types and is legally significant — users must sign it to establish accountability
- Policies must be **reviewed and updated regularly** (typically annually) to remain effective against evolving threats
- The **NIST SP 800-53** and **ISO/IEC 27001** frameworks provide structured catalogs that organizations use to derive their specific security policies
- Security policies are considered an **administrative (managerial) control**, not a technical or physical one — they govern behavior, not systems directly
