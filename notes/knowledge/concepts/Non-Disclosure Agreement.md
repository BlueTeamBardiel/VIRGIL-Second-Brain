# Non-Disclosure Agreement

## What it is
Like a sealed envelope that both parties sign before one person hands over the secret letter inside, an NDA is a legally binding contract that obligates signatories to keep specified information confidential. It establishes what counts as proprietary, who may access it, for how long, and what legal remedies apply if the secret escapes.

## Why it matters
A penetration testing firm discovers a critical zero-day in a client's ERP system during an engagement — without a signed NDA, nothing legally prevents a rogue consultant from selling that finding to a competitor or threat actor. The NDA creates enforceable teeth around responsible disclosure, ensuring findings stay within agreed boundaries until patched. It is also the primary legal instrument protecting red team methodologies and proprietary tooling shared during scoping calls.

## Key facts
- NDAs are a foundational element of **third-party risk management** — vendors, contractors, and cloud providers should sign one before receiving sensitive data or system access.
- Two types matter in security: **unilateral** (one party discloses, e.g., hiring a pentester) and **mutual** (both parties share secrets, e.g., M&A due diligence).
- Violating an NDA does **not** carry criminal penalties by default — it is a civil contract matter, unlike violations of laws such as CFAA or GDPR.
- Security+ and CySA+ frame NDAs under **personnel security controls** alongside acceptable use policies and background checks.
- NDAs have a defined **duration clause** — most expire after 2–5 years, meaning information can legally be disclosed after expiration unless renewed.
