# Misinformation campaign

## What it is
Like a magician who makes you watch the left hand while the right hand palms the card, a misinformation campaign floods the information environment with false narratives to manipulate belief and behavior. Precisely defined, it is a coordinated effort to deliberately create and spread false or misleading information at scale — often using automated bots, fake accounts, or compromised media — to achieve political, financial, or operational objectives.

## Why it matters
During the 2016 U.S. election cycle, the Internet Research Agency (IRA) operated thousands of fake social media personas to amplify divisive content, reaching an estimated 126 million Americans on Facebook alone. From a defensive standpoint, organizations must treat misinformation as an active threat vector — false breach announcements, for example, can tank stock prices or trigger panic-driven security mistakes like employees clicking phishing links "confirming" the fake incident.

## Key facts
- **DISARM Framework** is the primary structured methodology for analyzing and countering influence operations, analogous to MITRE ATT&CK for traditional cyber threats
- Misinformation campaigns commonly exploit **astroturfing** — fabricating the appearance of grassroots public support using coordinated inauthentic accounts
- **Deepfakes** and synthetic media have become a technical force-multiplier, lowering the cost of producing convincing false audio/video evidence
- Security+ and CySA+ categorize misinformation under **influence campaigns**, which fall within the broader threat intelligence domain alongside hybrid warfare tactics
- Detection relies on **OSINT techniques** — tracking metadata, linguistic patterns, bot-like posting cadence, and coordinated inauthentic behavior (CIB) signals
