# NIST SP 800-34

## What it is
Like a hospital's emergency playbook that tells staff exactly who calls whom, which wing evacuates first, and when to declare a full disaster — NIST SP 800-34 is the federal government's official guide for IT Contingency Planning. It provides a structured methodology for developing plans that restore IT systems, operations, and data after a disruption.

## Why it matters
When a ransomware attack encrypted a hospital network's systems in 2020, organizations without structured contingency plans scrambled for days while others with documented recovery procedures restored operations within hours. SP 800-34 defines the hierarchy of recovery documents — BCP, COOP, DRP, IRP — ensuring teams know exactly which plan activates under which conditions, eliminating costly confusion during a crisis.

## Key facts
- Defines **seven-step contingency planning process**: develop policy → BIA → identify controls → create strategy → develop plan → train/test → maintain plan
- Distinguishes five plan types: **BCP** (business continuity), **DRP** (disaster recovery), **COOP** (continuity of operations), **IRP** (incident response), and **Crisis Communications Plan**
- The **Business Impact Analysis (BIA)** is the cornerstone step — it identifies critical systems and establishes **MTD (Maximum Tolerable Downtime)**, **RTO**, and **RPO** targets
- Applies to federal information systems but serves as the de facto standard for private sector contingency planning
- Plans must be **tested annually** at minimum; testing methods range from tabletop exercises (low cost) to full-interruption tests (highest fidelity)
