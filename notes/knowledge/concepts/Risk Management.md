# risk management

## What it is
Think of it like a ship captain deciding which storms to sail through, which to avoid, which to prepare for, and which to simply accept as part of ocean travel. Risk management is the continuous process of identifying, assessing, prioritizing, and responding to threats to organizational assets — balancing the cost of controls against the potential impact of harm.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax suffered a breach exposing 147 million records because a known Apache Struts vulnerability (CVE-2017-5638) went unpatched for months. A functioning risk management program would have flagged that internet-facing system as high-value, prioritized patching it, and potentially avoided a $700M+ settlement — a textbook case of accepted risk becoming realized loss.

## Key facts
- The four risk responses are **Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, and Accept** (ATMA) — know which applies when (e.g., cyber insurance = transfer)
- **Risk = Likelihood × Impact**; both must be assessed, not just one
- **Residual risk** is what remains after controls are applied; **inherent risk** is the raw risk before any controls
- Risk appetite (how much risk an org willingly takes) differs from risk tolerance (the acceptable variance around that appetite)
- Qualitative risk analysis uses subjective rankings (High/Med/Low); quantitative uses numbers like **ALE = SLE × ARO** (Annual Loss Expectancy = Single Loss Expectancy × Annual Rate of Occurrence)
