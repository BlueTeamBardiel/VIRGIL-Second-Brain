# Association Frame

## What it is
Like a photo album that places a celebrity next to a criminal to make them look guilty by proximity, an association frame is a social engineering technique where an attacker links a trusted entity (a brand, person, or organization) to their malicious request to borrow credibility. Precisely, it is a persuasion strategy in which the attacker crafts messaging that associates their content with something the target already trusts, reducing skepticism and increasing compliance.

## Why it matters
In a Business Email Compromise (BEC) attack, a threat actor sends a phishing email styled with a legitimate bank's logo, color scheme, and signature block, then requests an urgent wire transfer. The victim's brain registers "this looks like Wells Fargo" and the association frame does the cognitive heavy lifting — the fraudulent request inherits the bank's trusted reputation before the content is critically evaluated.

## Key facts
- Association frames are a core mechanism behind **brand impersonation phishing**, which accounts for the majority of credential-harvesting campaigns
- They exploit the psychological principle of **halo effect** — positive traits of a trusted brand transfer to the malicious message
- Attackers use visual cues (logos, fonts), linguistic cues (official tone, legal disclaimers), and sender spoofing simultaneously to reinforce the frame
- Security awareness training specifically targets association frame recognition by teaching users to **verify sender domains independently** rather than rely on visual branding
- Association framing also appears in **vishing** (voice phishing), where callers claim to be from Microsoft or the IRS to establish instant authority before making requests
