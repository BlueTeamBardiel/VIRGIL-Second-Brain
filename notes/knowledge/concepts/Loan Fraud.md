# Loan Fraud

## What it is
Like a con artist renting a tuxedo to walk into a bank pretending to be a wealthy investor, loan fraud occurs when someone uses false or stolen identity information to obtain credit they never intend to repay. Precisely, it is a financial crime where fabricated, stolen, or manipulated credentials — income documents, Social Security numbers, credit histories — are submitted to lenders to illegally secure funds.

## Why it matters
In synthetic identity fraud, a leading loan fraud variant, attackers combine a real SSN (often stolen from a child or deceased person) with fabricated names and addresses to build a "Frankenstein" credit profile over months or years, then execute a "bust-out" — maxing all available credit lines simultaneously before disappearing. The FBI estimates synthetic identity fraud costs U.S. lenders over $6 billion annually, making it the fastest-growing financial crime in the country.

## Key facts
- **Synthetic identity fraud** differs from traditional identity theft: no single real victim notices immediately, making detection far slower
- Loan fraud often exploits **data breaches** — stolen PII from breaches feeds directly into fraudulent loan applications
- **Social engineering** is a common entry point; attackers phish bank employees to bypass verification steps
- Lenders use **anomaly detection and behavioral analytics** (a CySA+ relevant defense) to flag mismatches between application data and historical patterns
- Loan fraud is categorized under **financial crimes** in threat intelligence frameworks, linking it to organized crime and money laundering operations
