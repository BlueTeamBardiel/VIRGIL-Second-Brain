# Breach Notification Laws

## What it is
Like a hospital required to call patients when their test results go missing, breach notification laws mandate that organizations formally alert affected individuals and regulators after a data breach exposes personal information. These are legally binding statutes — not voluntary guidelines — that specify *who* must be notified, *what* information must be shared, and *how quickly* the notification must occur.

## Why it matters
In 2017, Equifax discovered a breach exposing 147 million records but waited 40 days before public disclosure — triggering massive regulatory fines and Congressional hearings. Breach notification laws exist precisely to prevent companies from quietly absorbing breaches; they force accountability by creating legal deadlines and penalties for delay, giving victims time to take protective action like freezing credit.

## Key facts
- **All 50 U.S. states** have their own breach notification laws; there is no single federal law, creating a patchwork compliance challenge.
- **GDPR (EU)** requires notification to supervisory authorities within **72 hours** of discovering a breach — one of the strictest timelines globally.
- **HIPAA Breach Notification Rule** requires covered entities to notify affected individuals within **60 days** of discovery; breaches over 500 records also require media notification.
- Notification typically triggers only when **unencrypted** personal data is exposed — properly encrypted data that is breached may exempt an organization from notification requirements (the "safe harbor" provision).
- Failure to comply can result in fines, civil lawsuits, and regulatory action; GDPR penalties can reach **€20 million or 4% of global annual revenue**, whichever is higher.
