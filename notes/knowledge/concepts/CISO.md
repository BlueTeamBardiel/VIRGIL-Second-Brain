# CISO

## What it is
Like a city's Chief of Police who doesn't chase criminals themselves but sets department policy, manages budgets, and answers to the mayor when crime spikes — the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) is the senior executive responsible for an organization's entire information security strategy, governance, and risk posture. They sit at the intersection of business leadership and technical security, translating threats into board-level language and resources into defensive capability.

## Why it matters
During the 2017 Equifax breach, the CISO bore direct responsibility for explaining to Congress why 147 million Americans' data was exposed — and ultimately resigned. A CISO who fails to enforce patch management policy (the Apache Struts vulnerability was known for months) creates an accountability vacuum where technical warnings never reach the people who control budgets and timelines.

## Key facts
- The CISO reports to either the CEO or CIO; reporting to the CIO creates a potential conflict of interest since IT operations and security priorities can clash
- CISOs are accountable for compliance frameworks such as PCI-DSS, HIPAA, and SOX — regulatory violations can carry personal legal liability
- Core CISO responsibilities include risk management, security policy development, incident response oversight, and security awareness programs
- Distinct from a Security Manager or Director — the CISO has C-suite authority and typically interfaces directly with the Board of Directors
- The role is explicitly referenced in NIST SP 800-37 as a key organizational role in the Risk Management Framework (RMF)
