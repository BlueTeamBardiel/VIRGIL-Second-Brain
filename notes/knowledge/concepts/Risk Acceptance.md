# Risk Acceptance

## What it is
Like a race car driver who knows the helmet won't prevent every injury but decides the race is worth the risk anyway — risk acceptance is a formal decision to acknowledge a threat and take no further mitigation action. It is a deliberate risk response strategy where an organization recognizes a vulnerability or threat, quantifies the potential impact, and consciously chooses to live with it because the cost of mitigation exceeds the potential loss.

## Why it matters
A small e-commerce company discovers its legacy inventory system has a low-severity SQL injection vulnerability in an internal-only tool accessible to three employees. After calculating that patching requires $40,000 in developer time while the asset's total value is $15,000, the CISO formally documents acceptance of this risk rather than remediate. This decision must be documented and signed off by management — undocumented acceptance is just negligence wearing a disguise.

## Key facts
- Risk acceptance is one of four core risk response strategies: **accept, avoid, transfer, mitigate** (also called treat/control)
- Accepted risks must be **formally documented** in a risk register with an authorized signature — verbal acceptance is not sufficient
- **Residual risk** is what remains after controls are applied; accepted risk may be residual or entirely unmitigated risk
- Risk acceptance is appropriate when the **cost of control exceeds the asset value** (ALE < cost of safeguard)
- Organizations set a **risk appetite/risk tolerance threshold** — risks below this threshold may be automatically accepted without individual review
- Acceptance is **not the same as ignoring** risk — it requires active acknowledgment and periodic re-evaluation
