# CFAA

## What it is
Think of the internet as a massive system of locked rooms — the CFAA is the federal law that makes it a crime to pick locks you weren't given a key to. The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (1986, 18 U.S.C. § 1030) is the primary U.S. federal statute criminalizing unauthorized access to computer systems and networks. It covers everything from hacking government databases to exceeding authorized access on systems you legitimately use.

## Why it matters
In the 2016 case *United States v. Nosal*, a former employee used ex-colleagues' credentials to access his old employer's database after being explicitly denied access — the court ruled this violated the CFAA even though the accomplices had legitimate credentials. This case defined a critical boundary for defenders: **authorization matters**, meaning insider threat programs and access revocation procedures are not just policy hygiene but legal enforcement mechanisms. Organizations now tie CFAA compliance directly to access control and account deprovisioning workflows.

## Key facts
- Prohibits **unauthorized access** and **exceeding authorized access** to protected computers (virtually any computer connected to the internet qualifies)
- Violations can result in **felony charges** with penalties up to 10–20 years depending on intent and damage caused
- The law covers **seven specific offenses**, including obtaining national security information, committing fraud, and causing damage to systems
- Civil liability exists alongside criminal — organizations can sue attackers under the CFAA for damages exceeding **$5,000 in a 1-year period**
- Controversial because "exceeding authorized access" is broadly interpreted — violating a website's Terms of Service has been argued (and rejected) as a CFAA violation
