# XII. Reporting and Communication

## What it is
Like a surgeon who must document every incision and brief the recovery team before leaving the OR, a security analyst's job isn't done when the threat is neutralized — it's done when the right people have the right information in the right format. Reporting and Communication is the structured process of conveying security findings, incidents, and risk posture to stakeholders at appropriate levels of technical detail and urgency.

## Why it matters
During the 2020 SolarWinds breach, the gap between technical discovery and executive communication cost organizations critical response time — analysts understood the compromise scope weeks before leadership could act on it. Proper escalation procedures and tiered reporting (technical findings to IR teams, executive summaries to C-suite) directly determine how fast an organization can authorize containment resources and make risk decisions.

## Key facts
- **Audience-appropriate communication** is mandatory: technical reports go to SOC/IR teams with IOCs and TTPs; executive reports focus on business impact, risk exposure, and remediation cost
- **Inhibitors to communication** (Security+ objective) include organizational silos, fear of blame, unclear escalation paths, and legal concerns around disclosure
- **Incident severity classification** drives reporting urgency — P1/Critical incidents require immediate verbal escalation, not just ticket creation
- **Chain of custody documentation** within incident reports preserves legal admissibility of evidence for potential prosecution
- **Metrics reported upward** typically include Mean Time to Detect (MTTD), Mean Time to Respond (MTTR), and number of incidents by severity — these drive security budget decisions
