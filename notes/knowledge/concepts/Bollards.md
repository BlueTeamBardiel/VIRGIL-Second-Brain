# Bollards

## What it is
Like a concrete fist planted in the ground, a bollard is a short, sturdy post designed to stop a vehicle dead in its tracks. Precisely defined, bollards are physical security barriers — typically made of steel, concrete, or reinforced materials — installed to prevent unauthorized vehicle access to a protected area. They represent the most fundamental layer of vehicle-borne threat mitigation in physical security architecture.

## Why it matters
In the 2016 Berlin Christmas market attack, a truck was weaponized to drive into a crowd — exactly the threat profile bollards are designed to defeat. A properly rated crash-rated bollard installed at a building perimeter can stop a 15,000-pound vehicle traveling at 50 mph, preventing it from reaching the building envelope and the people inside. This makes bollards a critical control for high-value targets like government buildings, data centers, and financial institutions.

## Key facts
- Bollards are a **physical deterrent control** and fall under the broader category of Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design (CPTED)
- **K-ratings (now ASTM F2656)** classify crash-rated bollards by vehicle weight and speed — e.g., K4, K8, K12 — with K12 stopping a 15,000 lb vehicle at 50 mph
- They can be **fixed, removable, or retractable** — retractable bollards allow authorized vehicle access while maintaining security posture
- Bollards address the **vehicle ramming (VBIED — Vehicle-Borne Improvised Explosive Device)** threat vector
- On Security+ exams, bollards appear under **physical security controls**, alongside fencing, mantraps, and lighting as preventive perimeter defenses
