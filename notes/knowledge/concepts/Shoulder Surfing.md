# Shoulder Surfing

## What it is
Like a nosy stranger craning their neck to read your newspaper on the subway, shoulder surfing is the act of directly observing someone's screen, keyboard, or documents to steal sensitive information. It is a low-tech, physical social engineering attack requiring no malware or network access — just proximity and line of sight.

## Why it matters
In a 2019 3M Visual Hacking Global Study, visual hacking attempts succeeded in 91% of trials in open office environments, with sensitive data captured in under 15 minutes. An attacker positioned behind a user at a coffee shop can capture VPN credentials, banking PINs, or multi-factor authentication codes simply by watching — bypassing every firewall and endpoint control in the organization.

## Key facts
- Classified as a **physical social engineering** attack vector, not a technical exploit — relevant to Security+ Domain 1 (Threats, Attacks, and Vulnerabilities)
- **Privacy screens (polarized film filters)** are the primary technical countermeasure, reducing viewing angles to roughly ±30 degrees
- High-risk environments include ATMs, airport lounges, open-plan offices, and public transit
- Attackers may use **smartphone cameras or small video recorders** to capture keystrokes at a distance, extending the effective range beyond casual observation
- Defense-in-depth countermeasures include: physical screen positioning, clean-desk policies, masked PIN entry pads, and user awareness training
- Shoulder surfing can defeat **MFA** if the attacker captures a TOTP code within its 30-second validity window
