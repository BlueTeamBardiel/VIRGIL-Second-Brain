# EMI

## What it is
Like a truck rumbling past and causing your radio to crackle with static, EMI (Electromagnetic Interference) is unwanted electromagnetic energy that disrupts the normal operation of electronic devices. Precisely, it is the disturbance generated by an external source that degrades the performance of an electrical circuit, and in security contexts, it can be both an attack vector and a physical protection concern.

## Why it matters
In a real-world attack scenario, adversaries can use targeted EMI to crash or manipulate industrial control systems (ICS) or medical devices without ever touching them — a technique demonstrated against implantable cardiac devices at close range. On the defensive side, facilities handling classified information use EMI shielding (Faraday cages, TEMPEST-compliant construction) to prevent sensitive signals from leaking out and being captured by eavesdroppers.

## Key facts
- **TEMPEST** is the NSA/NATO standard that defines requirements for limiting EMI emissions from equipment processing classified data to prevent signal interception
- Faraday cages block external EMI by surrounding a space with conductive material, creating a shield that absorbs and redirects electromagnetic waves
- EMI is distinct from RFI (Radio Frequency Interference), though RFI is a subset of EMI operating specifically in the radio frequency spectrum
- Shielded twisted pair (STP) cable is used over unshielded twisted pair (UTP) in high-security or EMI-heavy environments to reduce signal corruption and leakage
- Physical security standards for government facilities (e.g., SCIFs) mandate EMI shielding to prevent **van Eck phreaking** — reconstructing display output from emitted EM radiation
