# CWE-532

## What it is
Like a receptionist who shouts credit card numbers into a lobby while "quietly" writing them in the official ledger — log files that capture sensitive information expose secrets to anyone with file access. CWE-532 is the insertion of sensitive information (passwords, tokens, PII, session IDs) into application log files where they persist unprotected and are often over-shared.

## Why it matters
In 2019, Twitter disclosed that plaintext passwords were written to internal logs before the hashing process completed — potentially exposing hundreds of millions of credentials to employees with log access. An attacker who compromises a log aggregation system (like a misconfigured Splunk or ELK stack) can harvest credentials and tokens across an entire enterprise without ever touching the application itself.

## Key facts
- Log files are frequently shipped to SIEM tools, cloud storage, and third-party services — massively expanding the attack surface beyond the original application
- Session tokens in logs are immediately weaponizable: no cracking required, unlike hashed passwords
- Common sources: debug-level logging left enabled in production, full HTTP request/response logging, verbose error messages that echo user input
- OWASP recommends never logging credentials, session tokens, or full card numbers (PAN) — sanitize or mask before writing
- CWE-532 is categorized under the broader CWE-200 (Exposure of Sensitive Information) family and is directly relevant to GDPR/HIPAA violation risk
