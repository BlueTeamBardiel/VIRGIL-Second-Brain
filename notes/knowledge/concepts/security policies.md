# security policies

## What it is
Like a restaurant's health code — not the chef, not the ingredients, but the rulebook that governs how everything must be handled — a security policy is a formal, documented statement that defines an organization's rules, expectations, and responsibilities for protecting information assets. It establishes *what* must be done without necessarily specifying *how*, leaving implementation to standards and procedures.

## Why it matters
In the 2013 Target breach, attackers entered through a third-party HVAC vendor. A properly enforced **third-party access policy** would have mandated strict network segmentation and vendor privilege limitations — the technical controls existed, but the policy governing vendor access was inadequate, creating the gap attackers exploited. Policy failure, not just technical failure, caused 40 million card records to be compromised.

## Key facts
- **Policies are mandatory and authoritative** — they come from senior management (often the CISO or board) and carry organizational authority, distinguishing them from guidelines (which are optional)
- **AUP (Acceptable Use Policy)** defines what employees may and may not do with organizational systems — frequently tested on Security+ as a foundational policy type
- **Policy hierarchy**: Policy → Standard → Procedure → Guideline (most authoritative to least, most abstract to most specific)
- **Due diligence vs. due care**: Having a policy demonstrates due diligence; enforcing and following it demonstrates due care — both are required for legal defensibility
- Policies must be **reviewed regularly** (typically annually) and updated when the threat landscape, regulations (like HIPAA, PCI-DSS), or business operations change
