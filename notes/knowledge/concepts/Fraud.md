# Fraud

## What it is
Like a counterfeiter slipping fake bills into circulation — convincing enough to pass at first glance, but fundamentally misrepresenting reality — fraud is the intentional deception of a person or system to gain an unauthorized benefit. Precisely: fraud is a deliberate act of misrepresentation that causes a victim to act against their own interests, resulting in financial, informational, or material harm.

## Why it matters
In Business Email Compromise (BEC) attacks, attackers impersonate a CEO via spoofed email, instructing the finance department to wire funds to an attacker-controlled account. These schemes cost organizations over $2.9 billion annually according to the FBI IC3, making BEC the highest-loss cybercrime category — and it succeeds through fraud, not malware.

## Key facts
- **Identity fraud** vs. **identity theft**: theft is stealing credentials; fraud is *using* them to deceive — Security+ distinguishes these deliberately
- Wire fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1343) is the primary U.S. federal statute covering cyber-enabled financial deception, carrying up to 20 years imprisonment
- **Friendly fraud** (chargeback fraud) occurs when a legitimate cardholder disputes a valid transaction — a significant e-commerce threat distinct from external attackers
- Multi-factor authentication (MFA) is a primary technical control against account takeover fraud, as it invalidates stolen credentials alone
- Fraud indicators in CySA+ often appear in log analysis as anomalous transaction patterns — e.g., multiple small transactions just below reporting thresholds (structuring)
