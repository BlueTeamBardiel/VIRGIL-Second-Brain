# T1566.002

## What it is
Like a fisherman dangling a lure just below the surface rather than casting it to a specific fish, spearphishing via link delivers a malicious URL instead of an attachment — waiting for the victim to bite. Precisely defined, this is a targeted phishing technique where adversaries send carefully crafted messages containing hyperlinks that direct victims to credential-harvesting pages, drive-by download sites, or adversary-controlled infrastructure. The link does the dirty work; the message just has to be convincing enough to earn one click.

## Why it matters
In the 2020 SolarWinds supply chain attack, threat actors used spearphishing links in targeted emails to compromise initial victims before pivoting to the broader campaign. Defenders counter this by deploying URL rewriting and sandboxed link inspection (e.g., Microsoft Safe Links), which detonate the URL in a controlled environment before the user ever resolves it — buying critical detection time.

## Key facts
- Unlike T1566.001 (malicious attachment), this technique avoids triggering attachment-based AV scanning, making it harder to catch at the email gateway
- Links often use URL shorteners, homoglyph domains (pаypal.com vs paypal.com), or legitimate cloud services (OneDrive, Google Docs) to bypass reputation filters
- Time-of-click protection matters because URLs can be benign at delivery and malicious only after the victim clicks (delayed arming)
- Common payloads behind the link: credential harvesting pages, OAuth consent phishing, and drive-by browser exploits
- Mitigation includes multi-factor authentication (limits credential theft impact), DNS filtering, and user training on hovering to inspect URLs before clicking
