# Foot-in-the-Door Technique

## What it is
Like a door-to-door salesman who first asks you to sign a tiny petition, then returns a week later asking for a large donation — banking on your desire to stay consistent with your past behavior. In social engineering, the foot-in-the-door technique is a psychological manipulation method where an attacker makes a small, low-stakes request first to establish compliance, then escalates to the actual, larger target request. The victim's prior agreement creates psychological commitment that makes refusing the bigger ask feel inconsistent.

## Why it matters
A phishing attacker might first send a benign email asking an employee to confirm their job title for a "company directory update" — a harmless request that gets a reply. Having established rapport and a pattern of response, the attacker follows up days later requesting VPN credentials or access to a shared drive, and the employee complies because they've already mentally categorized this contact as legitimate.

## Key facts
- Exploits the **consistency principle** from Cialdini's Influence framework — humans are wired to behave consistently with prior commitments
- The initial request must be **small enough to be almost universally accepted**; too large and the chain breaks
- Effectiveness increases when the initial request requires **active effort** (signing, replying, clicking) rather than passive agreement
- Common in **pretexting scenarios**: attacker first verifies innocuous info (name, department), then escalates to sensitive data requests
- Defense: Security awareness training should teach employees that **any unsolicited request chain is a red flag**, regardless of how harmless the first step seemed
- Appears on Security+ under **social engineering attack types** alongside pretexting, phishing, and influence campaigns
