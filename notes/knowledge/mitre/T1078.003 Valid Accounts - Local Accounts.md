# T1078.003: Valid Accounts - Local Accounts

Adversaries abuse local account credentials for [[Initial Access]], [[Persistence]], [[Privilege Escalation]], or [[Defense Evasion]]. Local accounts configured for users, remote support, services, or administration can be exploited through credential dumping, password reuse, and lateral movement across networked systems.

## What Is It? (Feynman Version)

Local accounts are like the keys you hand out to people who live in your building. The technique is stealing or guessing those keys when the owner is away and then using them to walk through doors the owner never intended to open.

## Why Does It Exist?

Because most systems still rely on a handful of local accounts for day‑to‑day operations—remote‑support logins, service principals, or dormant admin accounts—so that an attacker who can obtain a single valid credential can bypass network firewalls and run scripts as if they were a legitimate user. Before the technique was named, attackers simply “crack the local password” in the same way a burglar would pick a lock; formalizing it highlights that this is a common, repeatable vector.

## How It Works (Under The Hood)

1. **Discovery** – Enumerate local users with `net user`, `wmic useraccount`, or by reading the SAM database on Windows or `/etc/passwd` on Linux.  
2. **Credential Acquisition** –  
   * Dump passwords from memory (`mimikatz`, `NTDSUtil`) or the SAM file.  
   * Brute‑force or guess passwords for accounts that are known to have weak or reused passwords.  
3. **Pass‑the‑Hash/Pass‑the‑Ticket** – Inject the stolen hash into an SMB session or Kerberos ticket to authenticate to other machines as that user.  
4. **Execution** – Use tools such as `psexec`, `wmic`, or SSH keys to launch commands on remote hosts.  
5. **Persistence & Lateral Movement** – Create a new local account with administrative rights or copy an existing privileged account’s credentials to other machines, then use those accounts to maintain access or move further into the network.

## What Breaks When It Goes Wrong?

The first sign is a spike in failed logins or a sudden increase in successful logins from unusual IPs or time windows. If an attacker gains an admin local account, they can silently install rootkits, exfiltrate data, and delete logs, causing:

* **Operational downtime** – Services can be shut down or misconfigured.  
* **Financial loss** – Remediation costs and regulatory fines.  
* **Reputation damage** – Customers lose trust when their data is compromised.  
* **Legal liability** – Failure to protect user data can violate laws such as GDPR or HIPAA.

## Attack Flow

1. **Initial Access** — Obtain valid local account credentials (guessing, dumping, phishing)  
2. **Privilege Escalation** — Abuse local admin accounts to elevate rights  
3. **Lateral Movement** — Reuse credentials across multiple systems  
4. **Persistence** — Create or abuse dormant accounts for continued access  
5. **Defense Evasion** — Use legitimate accounts to blend with normal activity
