# 2.1 - Threat Actors

Understanding threat actors is foundational to the Security+ exam because you must be able to identify *who* is attacking, *why* they're attacking, and *how capable* they are. The exam tests your ability to distinguish between different actor types (nation-states vs. script kiddies, insiders vs. organized crime) and match them to their characteristic behaviors, motivations, and resource levels. This section comprises ~5-7% of the exam's threat/vulnerability domain and appears frequently in scenario-based questions.

---

## Key Concepts

### Definition & Core Attributes
- **Threat Actor**: Any entity responsible for an event with a negative impact on another entity's security; also called a "malicious actor"
- **Threat Actor Attributes**: Measurable characteristics used to categorize and profile attackers—internal/external status, funding level, sophistication, and motivation

### Internal vs. External Classification
- **External Threat Actors**: Attack from outside the organization (most common)
- **Internal Threat Actors**: Operate within the organization, often with legitimate access; typically more dangerous
- The **Insider Threat** category overlaps this boundary—a person with internal access motivated by revenge, financial gain, or espionage

### Sophistication & Capability Levels
- **Low Sophistication**: Runs pre-made scripts or automated vulnerability scanners without understanding the underlying attack mechanics
- **Medium Sophistication**: Can identify vulnerable systems, understands attack chains, may customize exploits; often has institutional knowledge
- **High Sophistication**: Writes custom malware and attack code, can evade detection systems, understands defensive measures deeply

### Funding & Resources
- **No Funding**: Operates on personal time/resources; severely limited tools
- **Limited Funding**: Grassroots or nonprofit support; may use free tools and techniques
- **Extensive Funding**: Nation-states, major criminal enterprises; can acquire 0-days, custom exploits, and develop proprietary tools

### Motivations
- **Data Exfiltration**: Stealing intellectual property, personal data, financial records
- **Espionage**: Government or corporate intelligence gathering
- **Service Disruption**: [[DoS]], [[DDoS]], website defacing, operational sabotage
- **Blackmail/Extortion**: Ransomware, threats to release data
- **Financial Gain**: Direct theft, cryptocurrency theft, fraud
- **Philosophical/Political**: Ideology-driven attacks; activism
- **Revenge**: Disgruntled employees, jilted partners
- **War/Conflict**: Nation-state attacks on critical infrastructure
- **Disruption/Chaos**: For the sake of disruption; often low sophistication actors

## Threat Actor Types

### 1. Nation-States (Highest Threat Level)
**Profile**: External; massive resources; extremely sophisticated; state-sponsored

- **Characteristics**:
  - Government or national security apparatus backing the attack
  - Typically operate as **[[Advanced Persistent Threat (APT)]]** campaigns
  - Can sustain attacks indefinitely with state resources
  - Have access to classified exploits and military-grade capabilities

- **Motivations**:
  - Data exfiltration (espionage, intellectual property theft)
  - Disruption of critical infrastructure
  - War-related objectives
  - Political/philosophical goals
  
- **Real-World Example**: **Stuxnet** — U.S. and Israeli cyberweapon destroyed ~1,000 Iranian nuclear centrifuges; demonstrated nation-state capability to physically damage infrastructure via cyber attack

- **How They Operate**:
  - Long-term reconnaissance and patience
  - Custom exploits and zero-days
  - Defensive evasion and persistence mechanisms
  - Target critical systems: military, utilities, financial networks, government agencies

### 2. Organized Crime (High Threat Level)
**Profile**: External; professional; highly sophisticated; financially motivated

- **Characteristics**:
  - Criminal enterprises with division of labor
  - Monetized attack infrastructure: hackers, exploit managers, data brokers, customer support
  - Significant capital available for tool development and 0-day acquisition
  - Business-like operational structure

- **Motivations**:
  - **Pure financial gain**: ransomware, credit card theft, identity theft, cryptocurrency theft
  - Blackmail and extortion
  - Data sale (selling dumps on dark web marketplaces)

- **How They Operate**:
  - Operate like legitimate businesses with hierarchical structure
  - Specialize in [[Ransomware]] deployment, [[Phishing]] campaigns, [[Malware]] distribution
  - Maintain customer support channels for victims
  - Use cryptocurrency for anonymity and quick cash conversion
  - Often operate from countries with weak cybercrime enforcement

### 3. Insider Threats (Highest Damage Potential)
**Profile**: Internal; varying resources; medium sophistication; motivated by revenge, financial gain, or espionage

- **Characteristics**:
  - Have legitimate access to systems and networks
  - Possess institutional knowledge of security controls and vulnerabilities
  - Can operate undetected for extended periods
  - Often more damaging than external attacks (shorter dwell time needed)

- **Resource Advantage**:
  - Use the organization's own infrastructure against itself
  - No need to breach perimeter security
  - May have elevated privileges (admin, database access)

- **Motivations**:
  - Revenge (termination, promotion denial, workplace conflict)
  - Financial gain (selling data, side consulting)
  - Espionage (recruited by competitors or nation-states)
  - Sabotage (political/philosophical)

- **Red Flags**:
  - Unusual access patterns (accessing systems outside their role)
  - Downloading large amounts of data
  - Access outside normal business hours
  - System/network administration tool usage

### 4. Hacktivists (Medium Threat Level)
**Profile**: External (usually); variable resources; variable sophistication; politically/philosophically motivated

- **Characteristics**:
  - Motivated by a "cause"—activism with hacking as the weapon
  - Can be remarkably sophisticated despite limited funding
  - Often target organizations they view as unethical or corrupt
  - May start external but infiltrate to become insider threats

- **Attack Methods**:
  - [[DDoS]] attacks for disruption
  - Website defacing and data leaks
  - Public document releases ([[Doxing]])
  - Infrastructure sabotage

- **Funding**:
  - Often grassroots with minimal funding
  - Some hacktivist organizations have fundraising mechanisms
  - Rely heavily on free/open-source tools ([[Kali Linux]], [[Metasploit]])

- **Motivations**:
  - Environmental causes
  - Human rights activism
  - Anti-government or anti-corporation causes
  - Revenge (e.g., Anonymous, WikiLeaks supporters)

### 5. Unskilled Attackers / Script Kiddies (Low Threat Level)
**Profile**: Variable internal/external; no funding; low sophistication; opportunistic

- **Characteristics**:
  - Run pre-made scripts and automated vulnerability scanners without understanding the mechanics
  - No advanced technical knowledge
  - Often motivated by "the hunt" or disruption rather than profit
  - Success rate is high against unpatched/poorly configured systems

- **Attack Methods**:
  - Automated vulnerability scans ([[Nmap]], [[Metasploit]] framework)
  - Running publicly available exploit code
  - [[SQL Injection]], [[XSS]], [[Buffer Overflow]] attacks via scripts
  - Brute force attacks

- **Motivations**:
  - Curiosity and thrill-seeking
  - Disruption and chaos
  - Data exfiltration (for bragging rights, not profit)
  - Philosophical or political reasons (though less coordinated than hacktivists)

- **Why They Succeed**:
  - Many organizations are patched poorly or not at all
  - Default credentials and weak passwords remain common
  - No sophisticated defense is needed to stop them

### 6. Shadow IT (Medium Threat Level - Internal)
**Profile**: Internal; limited resources; medium sophistication; motivated by efficiency/autonomy

- **Characteristics**:
  - Business units or departments building their own IT infrastructure outside IT governance
  - Working around organizational IT policies and controls
  - Often unencumbered by security controls the central IT team implements
  - May be innovative but creates security blindspots

- **Motivation**:
  - Speed to market (IT can't keep up with business needs)
  - Avoiding IT roadblocks and change management processes
  - Perceived inefficiency of central IT
  - Cost (cloud services may be cheaper than internal infrastructure)

- **Security Risk**:
  - Unknown systems not monitored by [[SIEM]] or [[IDS]]/[[IPS]]
  - No patches, vulnerability management, or security baselines
  - Often uses unencrypted cloud storage ([[SaaS]] without security review)
  - Creates compliance violations (HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOX)
  - Difficult to respond to incidents on unknown systems

- **Example**: Finance team building their own data warehouse in AWS without IT/Security approval

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Think of threat actors like different types of burglars:

- **Nation-states** are like professional government intelligence agencies with unlimited budgets. They're patient, sophisticated, and have access to advanced tools. They might spend months observing your house, finding structural weaknesses, and waiting for the perfect moment.

- **Organized crime** are like professional heist crews—they've got a plan, a team, and they're doing it for money. One person scouts, another cracks the safe, a third fences the goods.

- **Insiders** are like a disgruntled housemaid who already has the keys and knows where the valuables are. They're the most dangerous because they don't need to break in.

- **Hacktivists** are like protest groups that might chain themselves to your front door—they want to make a statement and cause disruption.

- **Script kiddies** are like teenagers throwing rocks at windows—they're not sophisticated, but enough undefended windows might break.

- **Shadow IT** is like a tenant secretly running a business in the basement without the landlord knowing—it creates risks the main organization can't manage.

**In technical reality**: Threat actors differ in three key dimensions: **access level** (internal vs. external), **capability** (technical skill and resources), and **motivation** (why they're attacking). The exam tests your ability to match these attributes to attack patterns and recommend appropriate mitigations.

---

## Exam Tips

- **Distinguish motivation from capability**: An attacker can be highly motivated but low-skill (script kiddie), or low-motivation but highly skilled (organized crime doing contract work). The exam often presents scenarios mixing these attributes.

- **Nation-states = APT**: When you see "Advanced Persistent Threat," think nation-states first. APTs are characterized by long dwell times, custom malware, and sophisticated evasion. This is a high-confidence answer choice.

- **Insider threat ≠ just passwords**: The exam tests whether you understand that insider threats are sophisticated, have resource advantages, and are harder to detect than external attacks. They're not just about someone writing passwords on sticky notes (though that's one vector).

- **Watch for "funding" questions**: Low-sophistication attackers typically have no formal funding and use free tools. High-sophistication actors have budget. This is a quick way to eliminate answer choices.

- **Scenario questions will mix attributes**: Example: "A disgruntled database administrator with detailed knowledge of your backup infrastructure deletes critical files and covers their tracks." This is clearly an insider threat (internal, medium sophistication, revenge motivation). Don't confuse it with organized crime or nation-states.

- **Shadow IT as internal threat**: The exam may test whether you recognize that non-malicious internal actors (Shadow IT, rogue departments) create security risks equivalent to actual threat actors because they bypass controls.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing sophistication with funding**: Some hacktivists are highly sophisticated despite minimal funding (they're just motivated). Script kiddies have no funding and low sophistication. Don't assume all well-funded actors are sophisticated (though it's more common), or that low-funded actors are always unsophisticated.

- **Overlooking insider threats as the most damaging**: Candidates often focus on nation-states as the "biggest threat" and underestimate insider threats. The reality: insider threats cause disproportionate damage relative to effort because they already have access. The exam rewards recognizing that insider threats are high-priority.

- **Thinking Shadow IT is malicious**: Shadow IT is a **governance and security risk**, not necessarily a malicious threat. The people building it often have good intentions (faster business delivery). But it creates blind spots in monitoring, patching, and incident response. Don't label it as intentional sabotage; label it as an **uncontrolled risk**.

## Real-World Application

**For Morpheus's COCYTUS homelab**: Understanding threat actors informs your defensive posture. A sophisticated nation-state APT might use zero-days and custom malware—you can't prevent those with signatures alone; you need [[behavioral detection]] and network segmentation ([[VLAN]]s, [[Zero Trust]] architecture). An insider threat in your lab might be a compromised student account with excessive permissions—this requires [[Active Directory]] role-based access controls, privilege account management, and [[Wazuh]] alerting on unusual access patterns. Script kiddies are a constant noise factor—automated [[vulnerability scanning]], [[IDS]]/[[IPS]], and keeping systems patched will stop most of them. Shadow IT in a homelab often means lab members spinning up their own VMs/services outside your monitoring—integrate everything into your [[Wazuh]] [[SIEM]], use [[Tailscale]] for network visibility, and document the infrastructure so you can monitor it.
