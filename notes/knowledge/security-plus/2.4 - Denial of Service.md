# 2.4 - Denial of Service

Denial of Service (DoS) and Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks aim to make services, systems, or networks unavailable to legitimate users by overwhelming resources, exploiting vulnerabilities, or flooding bandwidth. This is a critical exam topic because DoS/DDoS attacks are common in real-world threat landscapes and require understanding of both attack mechanics and mitigation strategies. The Security+ exam tests your ability to distinguish between attack types, recognize reflection/amplification techniques, and understand why these attacks matter beyond just "bringing down a service."

---

## Key Concepts

- **Denial of Service (DoS)** — An attack that forces a service to fail by overloading it, exploiting design flaws, or consuming critical resources; renders a system unavailable to legitimate users.
  
- **Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)** — A coordinated attack using multiple compromised computers (a botnet) to overwhelm a target's bandwidth or computing resources simultaneously.

- **Botnet** — A network of infected computers (zombies or bots) controlled remotely by an attacker; used to launch large-scale DDoS attacks. Example: Zeus botnet infected over 3.6 million PCs at its peak.

- **Asymmetric Threat** — An attack where the attacker has fewer resources than the victim but can still cause significant damage (e.g., one attacker controlling thousands of botnet computers vs. a large organization).

- **DDoS Reflection and Amplification** — A technique that turns a small attack into a large attack by bouncing traffic off legitimate third-party servers, amplifying the payload without the attacker needing massive resources.

- **Protocol Abuse** — Exploiting legitimate network protocols (NTP, DNS, ICMP) that lack authentication or rate-limiting to amplify or reflect attack traffic.

- **NTP Amplification** — Using Network Time Protocol servers to reflect and amplify DDoS traffic; NTP responses can be much larger than requests.

- **DNS Amplification** — Abusing DNS resolvers to reflect and amplify attack traffic against a victim.

- **ICMP Flood** — Sending massive numbers of ICMP Echo Requests (ping) to consume bandwidth and processing resources.

- **SYN Flood** — Overwhelming a service with half-open TCP connections (SYN packets) without completing the three-way handshake, exhausting the server's connection table.

- **Layer 2 Loop (Friendly DoS)** — An unintentional denial of service caused by a switching loop when Spanning Tree Protocol (STP) is misconfigured or absent; broadcast storms can overwhelm a network.

- **Bandwidth DoS** — Consuming all available bandwidth through massive data transfers, rendering legitimate traffic unable to reach its destination.

- **Smokescreen DoS** — Using a DoS attack as a distraction or cover for another exploit (e.g., DoS masking a [[DNS]] spoofing or data exfiltration attack).

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**Simple Analogy:**
Imagine a small restaurant with one cashier. A denial of service attack is like having thousands of people call the restaurant simultaneously asking "Are you open?" without hanging up—the cashier can't take real orders because all the phone lines are jammed. In a DDoS version, an attacker doesn't have to make these calls themselves; they've tricked thousands of other phones into making the calls on their behalf, multiplying the impact. With reflection/amplification, the attacker calls a big company (like a directory service) and asks them to send a phonebook to the restaurant; the restaurant gets flooded with huge unsolicited responses meant for someone else.

**Technical Reality:**
- **DoS**: A single attacker floods a target with requests, malformed packets, or consumes resources (CPU, memory, bandwidth) faster than the service can handle them. The service fails because it's designed to handle normal traffic, not deliberate overload.

- **DDoS**: The attacker remotely commands a botnet of thousands of compromised hosts to simultaneously attack a single target. Since traffic originates from many sources, traditional blocking becomes difficult. This is an **asymmetric threat**—one attacker controlling massive distributed resources.

- **Reflection/Amplification**: The attacker sends small spoofed requests to a legitimate public service (like an NTP server or DNS resolver) that generates much larger responses. These responses are directed at the victim, so the attacker amplifies their attack without needing a massive botnet. The third-party service becomes an unwitting accomplice.

## Exam Tips

- **Know the difference between DoS and DDoS**: DoS = single source, DDoS = multiple sources (botnet). Exam often tests whether you can identify which attack type is occurring based on traffic patterns.

- **Reflection and Amplification are separate concepts but often paired**: Reflection means traffic bounces off a third party; amplification means responses are larger than requests. Both can occur together (DNS/NTP amplification attacks use reflection). Don't confuse them with direct DDoS floods.

- **Protocol abuse is the key to reflection attacks**: The exam expects you to know that NTP, DNS, and ICMP are commonly abused because they lack authentication and rate-limiting. This is why ISPs should block packets from unauthorized sources.

- **"Friendly DoS" is a real exam topic**: Unintentional DoS (misconfigured STP causing Layer 2 loops, accidental bandwidth overload) tests your understanding that not all DoS is malicious. A Layer 2 loop creates broadcast storms that can take down an entire network segment.

- **Mitigation strategies appear alongside attack types**: Expect questions linking DoS attacks to defenses like [[rate limiting]], [[firewall]] rules, [[IPS]] systems, redundancy, [[DDoS mitigation services]], and ingress filtering (blocking spoofed source IPs).

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing reflection with amplification**: Candidates sometimes think reflection IS amplification. Remember: reflection = bouncing traffic off a third party; amplification = making responses larger than requests. Both happen in NTP/DNS attacks, but they're distinct concepts.

- **Assuming all DoS is malicious**: The exam tests whether you understand "friendly DoS" scenarios (Layer 2 loops, misconfigured networks, accidental bandwidth hogging). A DSL line downloading huge Linux ISOs or a network switch without STP can cause unintentional DoS.

- **Underestimating the asymmetry**: Candidates sometimes think a DDoS attacker needs resources equal to or greater than the victim. This is false—a single attacker with a 50,000-node botnet can bring down a major corporation. The asymmetric nature is the whole point; the exam tests whether you grasp why botnets are so dangerous.

## Real-World Application

In your homelab (YOUR-LAB fleet), a [[Wazuh]] SIEM would detect unusual traffic spikes or repeated connection attempts that might indicate a DoS attack. A [[Pi-hole]] DNS sinkhole could be misconfigured to accidentally create a layer of amplification if DNS queries aren't rate-limited. Understanding reflection/amplification is critical for ISP-level defenders and cloud security teams; in a homelab context, you should know how to configure [[firewall]] rules to rate-limit ICMP and DNS responses, use [[Tailscale]] to segment traffic away from public DoS risk, and monitor [[Active Directory]] authentication attempts (a SYN flood can mask credential attacks during the chaos).

## Study Notes

**Why this matters for the exam**: Denial of Service is 22% of Domain 2, and DoS/DDoS questions appear in multiple question formats—scenario-based, concept-matching, and mitigation-focused. The exam loves testing whether you can:
1. Identify a DoS vs. DDoS from a description.
2. Recognize reflection/amplification techniques and which protocols are abused.
3. Distinguish intentional attacks from unintentional "friendly DoS."
4. Recommend appropriate defenses (rate limiting, firewall rules, DDoS mitigation services).

**Key takeaway**: DoS is about **availability**. It's one of the three pillars of the [[CIA Triad]]. An attacker doesn't need to steal data or modify it; they just make the service disappear. This is often the easiest attack for a threat actor to execute at scale (via botnets) and hardest for defenders to stop completely without planning and redundancy.
