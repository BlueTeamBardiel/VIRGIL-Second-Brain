# 1.2 - The CIA Triad

The [[CIA Triad]] represents the three foundational pillars of information security: **Confidentiality**, **Integrity**, and **Availability**. These principles form the bedrock of every security policy, control, and design decision in modern IT environments, and understanding them is absolutely critical for the Security+ exam. The [[CIA Triad]] (sometimes called the **AIC Triad** when reordered) provides a consistent framework for evaluating security controls, identifying vulnerabilities, and building comprehensive defense strategies.

---

## Key Concepts

### **Confidentiality**
- **Definition**: Ensures that sensitive information is accessible only to authorized individuals or systems; prevents unauthorized disclosure.
- **Core Principle**: "Need-to-know" — only those with legitimate business reason can access data.
- **Primary Mechanisms**:
  - [[Encryption]] — Converts plaintext data into ciphertext that only authorized parties with the correct key can decrypt.
  - [[Access Controls]] — Technical and administrative controls that restrict who can view or interact with resources (e.g., [[RBAC]], [[ACL]]).
  - [[Multi-Factor Authentication]] ([[MFA]]) — Requires multiple forms of verification (something you know, have, are) before granting access.
  - [[Least Privilege]] — Users and processes operate with minimum necessary permissions.
- **Real-World Examples**: Patient medical records protected by [[HIPAA]], financial data behind authentication, classified government documents.

### **Integrity**
- **Definition**: Guarantees that data has not been accidentally or maliciously modified during storage or transmission; ensures messages remain authentic and unaltered.
- **Core Principle**: Detection of unauthorized or accidental changes; "data is as intended."
- **Primary Mechanisms**:
  - [[Hashing]] — Cryptographic function that maps arbitrary-length data to fixed-length hash value; any change to data produces different hash (e.g., [[MD5]], [[SHA-256]]).
  - [[Digital Signatures]] — Mathematical scheme combining [[Hashing]] and [[Asymmetric Encryption]] to prove authenticity and integrity; sender signs with private key, receiver verifies with public key.
  - [[Certificates]] (X.509) — Digital documents binding a public key to an identity; signed by a trusted [[Certificate Authority]] ([[CA]]); used alongside digital signatures.
  - [[Non-Repudiation]] — Prevents sender from denying they created or sent a message; provides proof of authenticity and integrity (often tied to digital signatures and [[PKI]]).
  - [[HMAC]] (Hash-based Message Authentication Code) — Combines hashing with a secret key for message integrity verification.
  - [[Checksums]] — Simpler integrity check (less robust than hashing).
- **Real-World Examples**: Ensuring a software download matches the publisher's original file, verifying email hasn't been tampered with, confirming database records are authentic.

### **Availability**
- **Definition**: Ensures systems, networks, and data are accessible to authorized users whenever needed; services must be up and running when required.
- **Core Principle**: "Systems must be accessible" — no point in securing data if users can't access it legitimately.
- **Primary Mechanisms**:
  - [[Redundancy]] — Duplicate systems, data, or components so that if one fails, another takes over (e.g., redundant servers, [[RAID]] arrays, backup systems).
  - [[Fault Tolerance]] — Systems designed to continue operation even when hardware or software failures occur; maintains service continuity.
  - [[Load Balancing]] — Distributes traffic across multiple servers to prevent any single point of failure and optimize performance.
  - [[Patching]] — Regular security and stability updates close vulnerabilities and fix bugs that could cause downtime.
  - [[Disaster Recovery Planning]] ([[DRP]]) — Documented procedures to restore systems after major incidents.
  - [[Business Continuity Planning]] ([[BCP]]) — Ensures business operations continue during disruptions.
  - [[Backup and Recovery]] — Regular backups enable restoration of data/systems after loss or corruption.
- **Real-World Examples**: Bank websites must be online 24/7, e-commerce platforms need high uptime, hospital systems require continuous availability.

### **Trade-Offs Between the Three Pillars**
- **Confidentiality vs. Availability**: Stricter access controls increase confidentiality but may slow system access or create bottlenecks.
- **Integrity vs. Availability**: Extensive integrity checking (hashing, signing) adds computational overhead and latency.
- **Security vs. Usability**: Maximum security often reduces user convenience; organizations must balance protection with practical accessibility.

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**Imagine a bank vault:**

- **Confidentiality** = Only authorized people (customers, managers) have keys and can open the vault. The vault door is locked. A burglar can't just walk in and steal money — they don't have access.
  
- **Integrity** = You store your documents in the vault, and they're in a specific condition (pages in order, ink unblemished). When you retrieve them, you can verify they're exactly as you left them — no pages missing, no alterations. A tamper seal on the vault proves no one accessed it.

- **Availability** = The vault is open during business hours when you need to access your money. If the vault breaks, the bank has a backup vault. If the main branch is destroyed, there's another branch with your backup records. You can always get your money when you legitimately need it.

**In Technical Reality:**

- A [[TLS]] connection [[Encryption|encrypts]] data in transit (confidentiality), includes [[Digital Signatures]] so you know the server is authentic and data wasn't altered (integrity), and is hosted on redundant servers across multiple data centers (availability).
- A [[Wazuh]] [[SIEM]] agent monitors for unauthorized file modifications ([[Integrity]]), controls who can access logs ([[Confidentiality]]), and is deployed in high-availability clusters ([[Availability]]).
- Your [[Tailscale]] network provides [[VPN]] [[Encryption]] for confidentiality, uses digital certificates for integrity verification, and maintains multiple relay servers for uptime.

## Exam Tips

- **The [[CIA Triad]] appears in almost every domain** — You'll see it referenced in access control questions, incident response scenarios, and policy design. Know this cold.

- **Distinguish between similar mechanisms**:
  - [[Hashing]] (integrity) vs. [[Encryption]] (confidentiality) — Hashing is one-way (can't decrypt), encryption is two-way (can decrypt with key).
  - [[Digital Signatures]] (integrity + non-repudiation) vs. [[Encryption]] (confidentiality) — Digital signatures prove origin and integrity; encryption hides content.
  - [[Authentication]] vs. [[Authorization]] — Authentication proves *who* you are (confidentiality control); authorization confirms *what* you can access (access control).

- **Scenario questions test prioritization** — A question might ask: "A hospital must prioritize X over Y because..." Watch for questions where a security measure impacts availability (e.g., "MFA slows user login — is this acceptable?"). The answer is context-dependent; understand trade-offs.

- **Non-repudiation is integrity's partner** — Non-repudiation (proving the sender can't deny sending something) is often mentioned alongside integrity and digital signatures. Remember: digital signatures provide both integrity AND non-repudiation.

- **Availability is often forgotten** — Many candidates focus only on confidentiality and integrity, but availability is equally important. A DDoS attack threatens availability. Ransomware threatens both integrity and availability. Know examples of each.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing hashing with encryption**: Candidates often think hashing is like encryption. Remember: [[Hashing]] is one-directional (cannot be reversed); [[Encryption]] is two-directional (can be decrypted with the key). Hashing provides integrity; encryption provides confidentiality.

- **Underestimating availability**: Test-takers sometimes treat availability as an afterthought, but it's equally important as the other two pillars. A system that's perfectly secure but offline 50% of the time is useless. Know examples of [[Redundancy]], [[Fault Tolerance]], and disaster recovery.

- **Mixing up non-repudiation**: Candidates conflate non-repudiation with other concepts. Non-repudiation = proof that a specific person sent/signed something; they cannot deny it later. It requires [[Digital Signatures]] and [[PKI]], not just encryption or hashing alone.

## Real-World Application

In your **COCYTUS homelab**, the [[CIA Triad]] guides every decision:
- **Confidentiality**: Your [[Active Directory]] and [[Tailscale]] enforce encryption and access controls so lab VMs and secrets stay private.
- **Integrity**: [[Wazuh]] monitors file integrity with [[OSSEC]] modules, alerting if critical configs are modified.
- **Availability**: Your redundant storage, automated backups, and load-balanced services ensure labs stay online for your team's testing and security research.

A sysadmin managing production infrastructure must balance these three constantly: encrypt data (confidentiality), verify no tampering occurred (integrity), and ensure the database server is always reachable (availability).
