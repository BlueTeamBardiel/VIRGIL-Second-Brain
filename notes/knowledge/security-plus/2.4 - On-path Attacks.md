# 2.4 - On-path Attacks

On-path attacks (formerly called man-in-the-middle or MITM attacks) are a critical threat vector where an attacker intercepts and potentially modifies network traffic between a victim and their intended destination without the victim's knowledge. This topic covers both network-level and browser-level variants and is essential for Security+ because understanding these attacks underpins knowledge of encryption, [[authentication]], network segmentation, and defensive technologies. The exam expects you to distinguish between on-path network attacks and on-path browser attacks, recognize attack mechanisms like [[ARP]] poisoning, and understand why encryption and [[MFA]] are crucial mitigations.

---

## Key Concepts

- **On-path Network Attack (formerly MITM)**
  - Attacker positions themselves between victim and destination on the network
  - Intercepts traffic, inspects it, and forwards it to the real destination
  - Victim is unaware traffic was redirected or observed
  - Operates at the network/routing layer

- **On-path Browser Attack (formerly Man-in-the-Browser)**
  - Malware or [[Trojan]] running on the victim's local machine
  - Acts as a proxy within the browser or browser environment
  - Attacker operates from within the victim's own computer
  - Can intercept or modify data before it leaves the machine

- **[[ARP]] Poisoning**
  - Network-layer attack that exploits the Address Resolution Protocol
  - ARP has no built-in security or authentication mechanism
  - Attacker sends spoofed [[ARP]] replies to redirect traffic
  - Typically operates on the local subnet/LAN only
  - Directs victim traffic through attacker's machine

- **Traffic Redirection**
  - Core mechanism of both attack types
  - Victim's packets flow through attacker's system before reaching destination
  - Attacker can inspect, modify, or block traffic
  - Often transparent to the victim (no error messages)

- **Encrypted Traffic Proxying**
  - On-path browser attacks can relatively easily intercept encrypted traffic
  - Malware on the victim's machine can access plaintext before encryption
  - Appears normal to the victim since encryption/decryption happens locally
  - [[TLS]]/[[HTTPS]] provides little protection against on-path browser attacks

- **Banking/Credential Harvesting**
  - Exam often references scenario: malware waits for victim to log into bank
  - Once credentials are entered, malware captures them or performs fraudulent transactions
  - The malware sees plaintext credentials and session data

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**The Postal Service Interception:**

Imagine you're sending a letter to your bank through the mail system. Normally, the postal worker picks it up from your mailbox, delivers it to the bank, and the bank sends a response back to you. An **on-path network attack** is like a dishonest postal worker who intercepts your letter, reads it, makes a photocopy, seals it back up, and delivers the original to the bank. You never know your letter was read. Meanwhile, an **on-path browser attack** is like a criminal who breaks into your house, sits at your desk, and watches over your shoulder as you write and send letters. They're literally in your own home, so they see everything before it ever leaves.

**The ARP Poisoning specificity:** ARP is like the system that lets devices on your local network say "Hey, if you want to reach 192.168.1.5, send it to my MAC address." There's no ID check—anyone can claim to be anyone else. A hacker floods the network saying "All traffic for the router should come to me instead," and now they're the middleman.

**Why it matters technically:** In both cases, the attacker gains visibility and control. With encryption like [[TLS]], they might not read the content, but on-path browser attacks bypass this entirely because the malware sits *before* encryption happens on the victim's machine.

## Exam Tips

- **Distinguish MITM from MitB:** The exam will test whether you know the difference. Network attacks redirect at the router/ARP layer; browser attacks are malware on the victim's machine. Don't confuse them.

- **ARP Poisoning is LOCAL SUBNET ONLY:** On-path network attacks via [[ARP]] cannot cross subnets or the internet—they only work on local networks. If a question mentions attacking across the internet, it's not ARP poisoning.

- **Encryption Does NOT Protect Against On-path Browser Attacks:** A common trap: "If traffic is encrypted with [[TLS]], how can an on-path browser attack read it?" Answer: The malware is ON the victim's machine, so it sees plaintext before encryption. [[HTTPS]] doesn't help.

- **Recognize the Banking Scenario:** Expect an exam question like "Malware on a user's computer waits for them to log into their bank account, then steals credentials. What type of attack is this?" Answer: On-path browser attack (or Man-in-the-Browser).

- **Mitigations Ladder:** Know that [[ARP]] spoofing is mitigated by [[ARP]] inspection, [[VLAN]] segmentation, [[encryption]], and [[MFA]]. On-path browser attacks are mitigated by endpoint detection, [[EDR]], strong authentication, and malware prevention—not network-layer controls alone.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing the Layer:** Candidates sometimes think on-path network attacks require malware. They don't—[[ARP]] poisoning and routing attacks are network-level. Conversely, on-path browser attacks ARE always malware-based. The exam tests this distinction heavily.

- **Assuming Encryption Solves On-path Browser Attacks:** Many students believe [[HTTPS]]/[[TLS]] protects against all MITM variants. True for network-level attacks, false for browser-level attacks where malware is already inside the victim's system watching keystrokes and form submissions.

- **Forgetting ARP is Subnet-Only:** A question might ask "How would an attacker perform an on-path attack across the internet on a victim in another country?" and the answer is "Not with [[ARP]] poisoning"—that's local-only. On-path attacks at larger scales use routing attacks, BGP hijacking, or DNS spoofing, which are beyond 2.4 scope but worth knowing exist.

## Real-World Application

In your homelab, an on-path network attack could occur if an attacker on the same subnet performs [[ARP]] poisoning to intercept traffic between a workstation and the [[Active Directory]] domain controller, potentially capturing [[LDAP]] credentials or [[NTLM]] hashes. Deploying [[Wazuh]] with network monitoring enabled and implementing [[VLAN]] segmentation (isolating critical systems) reduces this risk. On-path browser attacks are more insidious: if a developer's workstation is compromised with a [[Trojan]], it could intercept credentials or [[API]] tokens even if they're transmitted over [[TLS]]/[[HTTPS]]. This is why endpoint detection (via [[Wazuh]] agent or [[EDR]]), [[MFA]], and regular malware scanning are non-negotiable in a production homelab.

## Mitigation Strategies (Expanded)

### Network-Level Defenses (Against On-path Network Attacks)

- **[[ARP]] Inspection / [[ARP]] Spoofing Prevention**
  - Switch-level feature that validates [[ARP]] replies
  - Drops suspicious [[ARP]] packets before they poison the cache
  - Requires switch support; not all consumer-grade equipment has it

- **[[VLAN]] Segmentation**
  - Separates subnets so [[ARP]] poisoning is contained
  - Attacker on VLAN-A cannot easily poison VLAN-B traffic
  - Limits the blast radius of a single compromised device

- **[[VPN]] / [[TLS]] Encryption**
  - Encrypts traffic end-to-end between client and server
  - Prevents inspection even if attacker redirects traffic
  - Doesn't protect against on-path browser attacks

- **[[DNS]] Security ([[DNSSEC]])**
  - Prevents [[DNS]] spoofing attacks that redirect users to malicious sites
  - Works alongside on-path network attacks (attacker might hijack [[DNS]] to redirect to phishing site)

### Endpoint Defenses (Against On-path Browser Attacks)

- **Malware Detection & [[EDR]]**
  - Real-time monitoring of process behavior
  - Detects suspicious proxy or credential-stealing behavior
  - In YOUR-LAB: [[Wazuh]] endpoint agent with behavior analysis

- **[[MFA]] (Multi-Factor Authentication)**
  - Even if credentials are stolen via on-path browser attack, second factor prevents login
  - Banking systems now mandate [[MFA]] for this reason
  - Defeats the "wait for login and steal credentials" attack

- **Certificate Pinning**
  - Application-level defense where apps verify [[TLS]] certificate specifics
  - Prevents malware from using its own [[TLS]] certificates to intercept traffic
  - More common in mobile apps; desktop browsers don't support it well

- **Host-Based Firewall & Process Isolation**
  - Blocks unexpected outbound connections from suspicious processes
  - Restricts malware's ability to exfiltrate data or maintain command & control
