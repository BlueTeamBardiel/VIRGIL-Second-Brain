# 1.2 - Physical Security

Physical security is the foundation of any comprehensive security program and represents the first line of defense against unauthorized access, theft, and sabotage. The Security+ exam tests your understanding of physical barriers, access controls, monitoring systems, and personnel-based security measures that protect facilities and assets. This section covers approximately 12% of Domain 1.0 and emphasizes how to layer multiple physical controls to create defense-in-depth protection.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Barricades & Bollards**: Physical barriers designed to prevent vehicular access while channeling pedestrian traffic through controlled points; can be concrete, metal, or even moats for extreme security needs.

- **Access Control Vestibules (Mantrap)**: A room or passage with two controlled doors where only one can be unlocked at a time, creating a "human airlock" to prevent piggybacking and unauthorized entry.

- **Fencing**: Perimeter barriers that define property boundaries; rated by transparency (see-through vs. opaque), robustness (resistance to cutting), and climbing difficulty (razor wire, height).

- **Security Guards**: Personnel providing active monitoring and validation of employee identification at entry points; critical for controlling physical access and responding to incidents in real-time.

- **Two-Person Integrity/Control**: A principle requiring two authorized individuals to work together to access sensitive physical assets, preventing single points of failure and insider threats.

- **Access Badges/Cards**: Electronically tracked identification worn by authorized personnel; includes photo, name, and other identifying details; logged and auditable.

- **Video Surveillance (CCTV)**: Closed-circuit television systems that monitor facilities 24/7; can include motion detection, object recognition, facial recognition, and license plate identification.

- **Lighting**: Strategic illumination that deters attackers (who avoid lit areas), enables better camera visibility, supports facial recognition, and reduces shadows and glare.

- **Infrared (IR) Sensors**: Detect infrared radiation emitted by warm objects; function in darkness; commonly used in motion detectors and perimeter monitoring.

- **Pressure Sensors**: Detect changes in force distribution; used on floors (to detect footsteps in restricted areas) and windows (to detect forced entry).

- **Microwave Sensors**: Detect movement across large, open areas by transmitting and receiving microwave signals; useful for warehouse or outdoor perimeter monitoring.

- **Ultrasonic Sensors**: Emit ultrasonic sound waves and detect reflections to sense motion, collisions, or proximity; effective for indoor spaces.

---

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Think of physical security like layers of a medieval castle. The outermost layer is the moat and high walls (fencing, barricades) that deter attackers from even attempting entry. The drawbridge and gatehouse (access control vestibules, guards) ensure that only authorized visitors can pass through. Inside, torches (lighting) illuminate the hallways so guards can spot trouble, and sentries (cameras, sensors) monitor every movement. The castle isn't relying on just one defense—if someone climbs the wall, the lighting exposes them; if they slip past the gate, a guard challenges them; if they hide in a corner, motion sensors trigger an alarm.

**In technical reality**: Physical security creates a **defense-in-depth strategy** where multiple overlapping controls make it exponentially harder for an attacker to compromise a facility. A single breach of one control (e.g., bypassing a badge reader) doesn't grant access because a second control (e.g., a guard, lighting, or motion sensor) independently validates the threat. Modern facilities layer these controls with [[SIEM]] systems and [[Incident Response]] protocols to correlate physical events with [[Malware]] detection and [[Network Security]] alerts.

## Exam Tips

- **Understand Control Layers**: The exam tests whether you grasp that physical security isn't one solution—it's many controls working together. A question might ask which combination of controls best protects a data center; the answer will involve guards + fencing + lighting + sensors + CCTV.

- **Distinguish Sensor Types by Use Case**: Don't confuse the sensors. **Infrared** works in darkness (thermal), **pressure** detects force on specific surfaces, **microwave** covers large open areas, and **ultrasonic** uses sound reflection. A question asking "which sensor detects a person walking across a warehouse floor in daylight?" is **microwave**, not infrared.

- **Access Control Vestibule Configurations**: The exam loves the "one door open / other locked" scenario. Remember: the vestibule prevents tailgating and piggybacking by ensuring only one door unlocks at a time. If both doors were open simultaneously, the vestibule fails.

- **Two-Person Integrity vs. Two-Factor Authentication**: Don't conflate these. Two-person integrity is **physical access control** (two people required to open a safe or server room). [[MFA]] is **logical access control** (two factors for login). The exam tests this distinction.

- **Video Surveillance Features Matter**: Motion recognition and object detection (license plate, facial recognition) are exam favorites. A camera that simply records video is less valuable than one that can alert on detected intrusions. Know that lighting directly impacts facial recognition accuracy.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming Physical Security is Optional**: Many candidates focus heavily on [[Encryption]], [[Firewall|Firewalls]], and [[MFA]], then skip physical security questions. The exam weight (12% of Domain 1.0) shows it's critical. A facility with perfect cybersecurity but unlocked server rooms is still compromised.

- **Confusing "Prevent Access" with "Prevent All Access"**: Bollards and barricades have limits—they slow attackers but don't make a facility impenetrable. The exam asks what they *can* do (channel people, prevent vehicles) not what they *guarantee*. Extreme measures like moats are mentioned to show the spectrum of options.

- **Misinterpreting Sensor Capabilities in Darkness**: Infrared sensors work in darkness, but **non-IR cameras cannot see in darkness as well**. The PDF states "Non IR cameras can see better" when it's lit—this means IR cameras lose their advantage in daylight but win in darkness. A question pairing a night-vision camera with infrared sensor is asking about a dual-capability setup.

## Real-World Application

In your homelab, physical security translates to protecting the server rack from unauthorized access: barricades prevent untrained users from damaging equipment, access badges control who enters the lab, motion sensors integrated with [[Wazuh]] alert on after-hours intrusions, and CCTV logs all activity. When combined with [[Active Directory]] access controls and [[Tailscale]] VPN policies, a breach requires crossing *multiple layers*—an attacker can't just plug into an ethernet port if the room is locked, monitored, and access is logged both physically and digitally. In a corporate SOC environment, this means the [[SIEM]] correlates camera motion events with failed login attempts to detect insider threats.

---

## Deeper Dive: Why Physical Security Matters for [[Zero Trust]]

[[Zero Trust]] architecture assumes breach and verifies every request. Physical security supports this:
- **Guards validate identity** before granting building access (first verification point)
- **Two-person integrity** ensures no single compromised employee can access critical assets
- **Sensors detect anomalies** (someone in the data center at 3 AM) that trigger investigation
- **CCTV provides audit trails** linking physical presence to logical actions (who was in the room when data was exfiltrated?)
