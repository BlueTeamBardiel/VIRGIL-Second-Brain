# 3.4 - Power Resiliency

Power resiliency is the ability of an IT infrastructure to maintain operations and data availability during power disruptions. This topic covers the mechanisms and technologies used to mitigate short-term blackouts, brownouts, and surges, as well as long-term power failures through [[UPS]] systems and generators. Understanding power resiliency is critical for Security+ because it directly supports the [[CIA Triad]]'s *availability* pillar—a system that loses power cannot maintain confidentiality, integrity, or availability, making power resilience a foundational security control.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Power Resiliency**: The engineering and planning necessary to ensure IT systems remain operational during power disruptions; a critical component of [[Business Continuity]] and [[Disaster Recovery]]
  
- **[[UPS]] (Uninterruptible Power Supply)**: A device providing short-term backup power during outages
  - Bridges the gap between power loss and either generator startup or graceful shutdown
  - Protects against blackouts (complete power loss), brownouts (reduced voltage), and surges (voltage spikes)

- **UPS Types** (by switching mechanism):
  - **Offline/Standby UPS**: Battery only powers devices when main power fails; slowest switching (5–10 ms); cheapest option; suitable for non-critical systems
  - **Line-interactive UPS**: Monitors and regulates incoming voltage; faster switching than offline; handles brownouts without battery drain; mid-range cost
  - **On-line/Double-conversion UPS**: Continuously converts AC to DC and back; zero switching time (instantaneous); most expensive; best for mission-critical systems; trades efficiency for reliability

- **UPS Features**:
  - Auto shutdown: Gracefully powers down systems when battery is depleted
  - Battery capacity: Measured in VA (volt-amperes) or watts; determines run time
  - Outlets: May be battery-backed or surge-protected only
  - Phone line suppression: Protects against power surges traveling through telephone/data lines

- **Generators**: Long-term backup power source for entire buildings or facilities
  - Fuel storage required (diesel, natural gas, or propane); must be regularly tested and maintained
  - Takes 5–30 seconds to reach full capacity after startup
  - Typically powers designated outlets marked as "generator-powered"
  - Should be paired with [[UPS]] to handle the generator startup delay

- **Power Outage Types**:
  - **Blackout**: Complete loss of electrical power
  - **Brownout**: Reduced voltage supply (insufficient power delivery)
  - **Surge/Spike**: Sudden temporary increase in voltage (can damage equipment)

- **Third-party dependency**: Most organizations cannot generate their own power; power availability is controlled by utility providers and subject to external factors

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Think of your home's water supply during a city outage: normally, water flows from the municipal system. If the main line breaks, you don't have water—unless you have a small water tank (like a [[UPS]]) that keeps your house running for a few hours. For longer outages, you'd need a backup well (like a generator) that can pump water indefinitely, but it takes time to start up. That's why you use both: the tank keeps things going while the well gets running.

**Technical reality**: A [[UPS]] contains rechargeable batteries that instantly switch to power the system when mains power drops below a threshold. This "switching time" is measured in milliseconds. For longer outages, a generator engine (usually diesel) starts automatically but requires 5–30 seconds to stabilize. The [[UPS]] keeps the infrastructure alive during this startup window. Once the generator reaches stable output, it can power the entire facility for as long as fuel is available. [[UPS]] systems are also used to handle brownouts (low voltage) by actively regulating the power supply before it reaches sensitive equipment.

## Exam Tips

- **Distinguish UPS types by switching time and use case**: 
  - Offline = cheapest, slowest (5–10 ms) = home/small business use
  - Line-interactive = mid-tier, faster, handles brownouts = small data centers
  - On-line = zero switching, most expensive = critical mission systems (servers, [[Wazuh]], [[SIEM]])
  - Expect a scenario question: "A hospital needs zero power interruption to life-support systems. Which UPS type?" → **On-line**

- **UPS ≠ Generator**: Exam often mixes these. Remember: UPS is short-term (minutes to hours), Generator is long-term (hours to days/indefinitely). A complete answer to "how do you handle a 12-hour power outage?" requires **both**.

- **Generator startup delay is critical**: Questions may ask why a [[UPS]] is still needed if you have a generator. Answer: "Because the generator takes 5–30 seconds to reach full capacity, the [[UPS]] bridges that gap to prevent system shutdown."

- **Power surges and brownouts are different problems**: 
  - Surge = overvoltage spike → needs suppression/protection
  - Brownout = under-voltage, sustained low power → UPS/line-interactive regulators help
  - Offline [[UPS]] doesn't help with brownouts (doesn't activate until complete loss)

- **Battery capacity is measured in VA or watts**: A 1500 VA [[UPS]] won't keep a 2000W server running for long. Look for power consumption of all devices to determine [[UPS]] sizing.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming [[UPS]] solves everything**: Candidates forget that [[UPS]] only buys time (typically 5–30 minutes). A generator is required for extended outages. An exam question on "maintaining 8-hour uptime" requires both technologies.

- **Confusing "double-conversion" with "double the power"**: On-line UPS continuously converts AC→DC→AC; it's not twice as powerful, it's continuously powering the load from battery (even when mains are present). This is why it generates more heat but provides zero switching delay.

- **Forgetting about fuel logistics**: Generators need fuel. A candidate might know the technical specs but miss that a generator sitting without maintenance for a year won't start during an emergency. The exam may test knowledge that generators require periodic testing, fuel rotation, and load-testing.

---

## Real-World Application

In your homelab, a [[UPS]] protecting the core switch and [[Active Directory]] domain controller ensures that network authentication and VLAN segregation remain available during power events—critical for [[Zero Trust]] and [[Wazuh]] monitoring continuity. A larger setup might use a generator to sustain the entire lab during extended outages, allowing for continued [[Incident Response]] training without data loss on the DFIR appliances. Without power resiliency, a simple brownout could trigger false alerts in [[Wazuh]], corrupt logs, or cause desynchronization in [[Tailscale]] peer connections.

## Study Notes

**For the exam, remember the three-layer power strategy:**
1. **Immediate protection** (milliseconds): [[UPS]] surge suppression / voltage regulation
2. **Short-term backup** (minutes to hours): [[UPS]] battery power
3. **Long-term backup** (indefinite): Generator with fuel supply

**Quick Reference Table:**

| Feature | Offline UPS | Line-Interactive | On-line UPS |
|---------|-------------|------------------|-------------|
| **Switching Time** | 5–10 ms | 2–5 ms | ~0 ms (instantaneous) |
| **Handles Brownouts** | No | Yes | Yes |
| **Cost** | Low | Medium | High |
| **Best For** | Home/small office | Small business | Data centers, hospitals |
| **Power Efficiency** | High | Medium | Lower (continuous conversion) |

**Exam Scenario Patterns:**
- "System needs zero downtime" → On-line [[UPS]] + Generator
- "Extended outage expected" → Generator + [[UPS]] (for startup delay)
- "Budget-conscious small office" → Offline [[UPS]] + Surge protectors
- "Voltage stability is the main concern" → Line-interactive [[UPS]]
