# 1.1 - Security Controls

Security controls are the foundational mechanisms organizations use to manage risk, protect assets, and respond to security threats. This section covers the categorization of controls by implementation type (technical, managerial, operational, physical) and by function (preventive, detective, corrective, compensating, deterrent, directive). Understanding these distinctions is critical for the Security+ exam, as questions frequently test your ability to identify the appropriate control type for a given scenario and to understand when and how each control applies.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Security Control**: Any mechanism, process, or policy designed to reduce risk, prevent security events, minimize impact, or limit damage to assets (data, physical property, computer systems)

- **Technical Controls**: System-based controls implemented through technology
  - Examples: [[Firewall|Firewalls]], [[Anti-virus|anti-virus software]], [[Encryption]], operating system access controls
  - Automated enforcement with minimal human intervention

- **Managerial Controls**: Administrative and policy-based controls associated with security design and implementation
  - Examples: Security policies, standard operating procedures (SOPs), security frameworks
  - Focus on governance, planning, and organizational structure

- **Operational Controls**: People-based controls implemented through human actions and processes
  - Examples: [[Security]] awareness training, security guards, background checks, incident response procedures
  - Depend on human behavior and execution

- **Physical Controls**: Mechanisms that limit or restrict physical access to assets and facilities
  - Examples: Guard shacks, badge readers, fences, locks, video surveillance, bollards
  - Protect tangible infrastructure and resources

---

### Control Function Types (The Six Categories)

- **Preventive Controls**: Block access or prevent an event from occurring
  - Action: "You shall not pass" — deny before it happens
  - Examples: Firewall rules, door locks, security policies, badge checks at entry
  - Most desirable control type (proactive)

- **Detective Controls**: Identify and log intrusion attempts or breaches after they occur
  - Action: Find the problem after the fact
  - Examples: System logs, login reports, motion detectors, [[SIEM]] systems, security audits, regular patrols
  - Does NOT prevent access but documents what happened

- **Corrective Controls**: Applied after detection to reverse or mitigate the impact of an event
  - Action: Fix the problem and restore normal operations
  - Examples: [[Restore|Restoring from backups]] (ransomware mitigation), policies for reporting, law enforcement contact, incident remediation procedures
  - Minimizes downtime and damage

- **Compensating Controls**: Substitute or supplement existing controls when primary controls are insufficient
  - Action: Use alternative means to prevent exploitation of a weakness
  - May be temporary until permanent fix is implemented
  - Examples: Firewall blocking an unpatched application, separation of duties, redundant systems, backup generators
  - Often used during transition periods or when budget constraints exist

- **Deterrent Controls**: Discourage an intrusion attempt without directly preventing access
  - Action: Make an attacker think twice — psychological barrier
  - Relatively weak on their own; often combined with other controls
  - Examples: Warning signs ("Authorized Personnel Only"), threat of consequences, reception desk, splash screens with legal warnings
  - Effective against casual or opportunistic threats

- **Directive Controls**: Direct subjects toward security compliance through guidance and training
  - Action: Tell people what to do
  - Relatively weak; relies on voluntary compliance
  - Examples: Security awareness training, compliance policies and procedures, storage guidelines for sensitive files, user education
  - Foundational but not sufficient alone

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**Think of security controls as layers in a bank's security system:**

Imagine a bank wants to protect its vault. A **preventive control** is like the heavy vault door itself—it stops a thief from entering in the first place. A **detective control** is the security camera and alarm system—if someone bypasses the door, the camera catches them and the alarm alerts guards. A **deterrent control** is the guard standing in the lobby or the sign saying "This area is monitored"—it discourages casual theft attempts. A **corrective control** is the backup safe or emergency procedures that minimize losses if a theft does occur. A **compensating control** is the extra guard hired when the vault door is being serviced. And **directive controls** are the training employees receive on not sharing door codes.

**Technical connection**: In a digital environment, a [[Firewall|firewall]] (technical, preventive) blocks malicious traffic before it reaches your systems. Audit logs and [[IDS|intrusion detection systems]] (technical, detective) capture evidence of attacks. Backup and restore procedures (technical, corrective) recover from ransomware. Security policies (managerial, directive) guide employee behavior. This layered approach ensures that if one control fails, others still provide protection.

## Exam Tips

- **Distinguish preventive vs. detective**: Preventive stops the event; detective finds it after it happens. Test questions often ask which control "prevents" vs. which "identifies." Read carefully.

- **Recognize compensating controls in scenarios**: When a question describes a temporary or alternative solution while a primary control is being fixed, think "compensating." Example: "We're blocking port 22 at the firewall until we can patch the SSH vulnerability" = compensating control.

- **Managerial controls set the tone**: If a question asks about "governance," "policy," "planning," or "frameworks," you're likely dealing with a managerial control. These are administrative, not technical.

- **Operational controls involve people**: Look for keywords like "training," "awareness," "security guards," "background checks," or "incident response teams." These are people-dependent and require human execution.

- **Common trap—confusing corrective with preventive**: A corrective control does NOT prevent the event; it minimizes impact *after* the event. Example: Backup procedures are corrective (they restore after loss), NOT preventive (they don't stop the loss from happening).

- **Physical controls protect infrastructure**: If the scenario involves physical access (building, server room, network closet), you're in the physical controls domain. Think badges, locks, video, guards.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming detective controls prevent attacks**: Candidates often confuse detection with prevention. A log or alarm system does NOT stop an attacker—it only reveals they were there. This is a critical distinction on the exam.

- **Misidentifying directive controls as preventive**: A policy or sign that says "You must do X" is directive (guidance), not preventive (blocking). Directives rely on compliance; prevention is automatic. Candidates often mistake training or awareness programs as prevention when they're actually directive.

- **Treating compensating controls as permanent solutions**: Compensating controls are temporary workarounds or alternatives. If a question frames a control as "until we can implement X" or "because our primary control isn't sufficient," it's compensating. Don't assume it's the final solution.

## Real-World Application

In your homelab, security controls manifest across multiple layers: [[Tailscale]] acts as a preventive control (blocking unauthorized VPN access), [[Wazuh]] functions as a detective control (identifying intrusion attempts via log analysis), and regular backups serve as corrective controls (enabling recovery from ransomware). [[Active Directory]] enforces both technical (password policies) and managerial controls (group policy objects). Guard procedures around physical access to servers represent operational controls. This defense-in-depth strategy mirrors enterprise SOC practices and ensures that no single control failure compromises the entire lab.
