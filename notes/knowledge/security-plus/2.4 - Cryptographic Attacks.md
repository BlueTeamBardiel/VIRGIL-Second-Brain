# 2.4 - Cryptographic Attacks

Cryptographic attacks target the implementation and weaknesses of encryption systems rather than brute-forcing keys directly. This section covers how attackers exploit algorithmic flaws, downgrade vulnerabilities, and protocol weaknesses to compromise data confidentiality and integrity. Understanding these attacks is critical for the Security+ exam because they test your ability to recognize real-world cryptographic failures and recommend appropriate mitigations.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Cryptographic Attack**: Any method used to break, undermine, or bypass encryption systems—typically by exploiting implementation weaknesses rather than attacking the key directly
- **Birthday Attack**: A probabilistic attack exploiting the mathematical principle that hash collisions become likely much faster than brute force would suggest (similar to the "birthday paradox" where 23 people have a ~50% chance of sharing a birthday)
- **Hash Collision**: When two different plaintexts produce the same hash digest value, compromising the uniqueness property of [[Hashing]] functions
- **MD5 (Message Digest Algorithm 5)**: A cryptographic hash function published in 1992; collisions were identified as early as 1996, making it cryptographically broken and unsuitable for further use
- **Downgrade Attack**: Forces a system to abandon strong encryption and fall back to weaker, legacy protocols or algorithms
- **POODLE (Padding Oracle On Downgraded Legacy Encryption)**: A 2014 [[TLS]] vulnerability that exploits downgrade mechanisms to force clients to use [[SSL]] 3.0, which contains significant cryptographic weaknesses
- **SSL Stripping**: An on-path attack that intercepts HTTPS traffic and rewrites it to HTTP, allowing an attacker to capture unencrypted credentials and data
- **On-Path Attack** (formerly Man-in-the-Middle): An attack where the adversary positions themselves between two communicating parties to intercept, read, or modify traffic
- **Protocol Downgrade**: Forcing the use of older, less secure versions of encryption protocols (e.g., SSL 3.0 instead of [[TLS]] 1.2+)
- **Brute Force (in context of hash attacks)**: An attacker generates multiple versions of plaintext and compares their hashes to a target, hoping to find a collision

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**The Safe Analogy:**
Imagine a safe with a combination lock. You've encrypted your data and locked it inside—but the attacker doesn't try to guess the combination. Instead, they look for structural flaws in the safe itself. Maybe the metal is weak, or there's a design flaw they can exploit. Similarly, cryptographic attacks don't brute-force the key; they exploit weaknesses in *how the encryption was built or implemented*.

**The Birthday Party Analogy:**
At a birthday party with 23 people, there's a ~50% chance two share a birthday. You'd think you'd need 183 people for that probability (365 days ÷ 2), but the math works differently. Similarly, in hash collisions, you can find matching digests far faster than the theoretical security strength suggests—not because the algorithm is weak, but because probability works in the attacker's favor when you have enough attempts.

**SSL Stripping in Plain Terms:**
You ask a taxi for a secure ride (HTTPS). An attacker intercepts your request and tells you there's no secure option available (forces downgrade to HTTP), then drives you while recording everything you say. You think you're secure, but you're not.

## Exam Tips

- **Birthday Attack vs. Brute Force**: The exam will test whether you understand that birthday attacks find collisions *exponentially faster* than brute force. With a 128-bit hash, you need only ~2^64 attempts (not 2^128) to have a 50% collision chance.
  
- **POODLE and Downgrade Attacks are Related**: Modern exam questions often ask why POODLE works—the answer is always "because systems were allowed to downgrade to SSL 3.0." Know that modern browsers/TLS implementations now refuse to downgrade, which mitigates POODLE.

- **SSL Stripping vs. Other On-Path Attacks**: Distinguish between SSL stripping (HTTP/HTTPS rewriting) and other [[Man-in-the-Middle]] attacks. SSL stripping specifically downgrades the *protocol*, not the cipher strength.

- **MD5 Is Deprecated**: The exam expects you to know that MD5 collisions were demonstrated in 1996 and 2008 (the RapidSSL certificate attack). Never recommend MD5 for security purposes in real scenarios.

- **Mitigation Focus**: The exam tests mitigation as much as attack mechanics. Know that you mitigate birthday attacks with larger hash outputs, and downgrade attacks by disabling legacy protocol versions.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing "breaking the key" with "breaking the algorithm"**: Candidates often assume cryptographic attacks mean guessing the key. They don't—they exploit implementation flaws, protocol weaknesses, or mathematical properties of the algorithm itself.

- **Underestimating hash collision probability**: Many students think MD5's 128-bit output means 2^128 attempts are needed for a collision. The birthday paradox means collisions occur around 2^64 attempts—still a massive number, but *much* faster than brute force. This is why even "good" hash functions with reasonable output sizes can be vulnerable.

- **Not recognizing SSL/TLS version in downgrade attacks**: On the exam, when you see "forced to use an older protocol," immediately think "downgrade attack." POODLE is the canonical example, but the principle applies to any scenario where legacy protocol versions remain enabled.

---

## Real-World Application

In a homelab like **COCYTUS**, you'd encounter these attacks if you misconfigured [[TLS]] certificates or left legacy [[SSL]] versions enabled on web services. For instance, if a self-hosted service supports both TLS 1.3 and SSL 3.0, an on-path attacker (perhaps via ARP spoofing on your home network) could strip HTTPS down to HTTP or force the downgrade to SSL 3.0. Similarly, using weak hash functions like MD5 for file integrity verification (e.g., verifying downloaded ISOs) leaves you vulnerable to collision attacks. In a properly hardened homelab with [[Tailscale]] for remote access, you'd enforce minimum [[TLS]] versions (1.2+) and disable legacy protocols entirely to prevent downgrade attacks.
