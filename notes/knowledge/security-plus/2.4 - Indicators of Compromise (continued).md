# 2.4 - Indicators of Compromise (continued)

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs) are forensic artifacts and behavioral patterns that reveal unauthorized access, data exfiltration, or active exploitation within a network environment. This section focuses on identifying advanced IoCs through authentication anomalies, resource consumption patterns, log analysis gaps, and post-breach intelligence. Mastering these indicators is critical for Security+ candidates because they form the foundation of threat detection, incident response, and SIEM-based security operations.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Impossible Travel**: A user authenticated in two geographically distant locations within a timeframe that makes physical travel impossible (e.g., login in Nebraska followed by Australia 3 minutes later). This IoC is easily automated in [[SIEM]] platforms and indicates compromised credentials or account takeover.

- **Authentication Logs**: The primary source for detecting impossible travel and anomalous access patterns; includes logon/logoff events, timestamps, source IP addresses, and failure attempts. These logs are foundational to [[Incident Response]] workflows.

- **Out-of-Cycle Logging**: Unexpected events occurring outside normal operational windows—such as [[Operating System]] patches deployed outside the standard patch day or administrative access at 3 AM when no maintenance is scheduled. These anomalies warrant immediate investigation.

- **Resource Consumption Anomalies**: Unusual spikes in bandwidth, CPU, memory, or disk I/O that deviate from baseline behavior. File transfers at odd hours (e.g., 3 AM bulk data exfiltration) are a classic indicator of [[Data Exfiltration]] or [[Ransomware]] preparation.

- **Firewall Log Analysis**: Detailed traffic flow records showing source/destination IPs, ports, protocols, and applications used. Outbound transfers to unknown destinations are critical IoCs indicating [[Command and Control (C2)]] communication or data theft.

- **Network Disruption**: Unexpected outages, resource unavailability, or "server is down" states that may mask the actual exploit or be a byproduct of attacker activity (e.g., denial of service, misconfigured ransomware, brute force lockouts).

- **Encrypted Data at Rest**: Sudden appearance of encrypted files on systems not normally encrypted can indicate [[Ransomware]] attack initiation or data exfiltration preparation.

- **Brute Force Attack Indicators**: Repeated failed authentication attempts, account lockouts, and credential stuffing patterns visible in authentication logs and [[IDS]]/[[IPS]] alerts.

- **Missing Logs**: Suspicious absence of expected log entries—attackers deliberately delete or manipulate logs to cover their tracks. Missing logs are themselves a strong indicator of compromise.

- **Log Retention and Security**: Logs must be centralized, encrypted, and monitored in real time to prevent tampering. [[Wazuh]], [[Splunk]], and similar [[SIEM]] solutions provide immutable log storage and alerting.

- **Published/Documented Breaches**: Post-breach intelligence showing company data published on dark web forums, paste sites, or attacker-controlled infrastructure—often in conjunction with [[Ransomware]] extortion threats.

- **Partial vs. Full Data Release**: Attackers may leak a sample of data to prove exfiltration, demand ransom, or publish entire datasets. Context matters—raw data dumps can be analyzed by researchers to identify the breach source.

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Imagine your home security system tracks all entry/exit logs, plus energy usage (power, water), and valuables inventory. If someone logged in at the front door in California, then magically at the back door in Tokyo three minutes later, alarms would sound—that's **impossible travel**. If your power bill suddenly spikes at 3 AM (when no one's home), or you find your front door lock scratched and your jewelry boxes opened, those are **resource anomalies** and **physical evidence of compromise**.

Now imagine a burglar tries to cover their tracks by erasing the security footage—the *absence* of that footage is itself evidence they were there. If weeks later you hear news that your valuables are being sold at a pawn shop under your name, that's **post-breach intelligence**.

**Technical reality**: In cybersecurity, logs are your security footage. [[Authentication Logs]] record "who logged in where and when." [[Firewall Logs]] show "what data left the house and where it went." Missing logs and unusual resource consumption (bandwidth spikes, CPU usage, encrypted files) are red flags. [[SIEM]] platforms like [[Wazuh]] automate this detective work by correlating logs across systems and alerting on anomalies in real time.

## Exam Tips

- **Impossible Travel is heavily tested**: Expect scenario-based questions asking you to identify impossible travel from a log dump. Look for two authentications from distant locations separated by an impossibly short time window. The correct answer will involve [[SIEM]] alerting or [[Incident Response]] escalation.

- **Don't confuse "out-of-cycle" with "scheduled maintenance"**: Out-of-cycle means *unexpected*. A patch deployed on Thursday when patch day is Tuesday, or a database backup at 2 AM when backups normally run at 6 PM—these are IoCs. Scheduled maintenance is expected and logged; anomalies are not.

- **Resource consumption is often the first real notification**: Questions may emphasize that months of undetected breach activity only become visible when an attacker's actions cause a tangible impact (bandwidth spike, server outage, encrypted files). This tests your understanding that *prevention* is easier than *detection*.

- **Missing logs = suspicious**: If you're asked "what's the most suspicious log entry?" and one option is "no log entry at all," that's likely correct. Attackers use log wiping tools ([[Living off the Land]] techniques) to eliminate evidence. Centralized, immutable logging (covered in [[SIEM]] and [[Incident Response]] sections) mitigates this.

- **Published data is post-breach intelligence**: Questions may ask what IoC indicates data has already been exfiltrated. The answer is: when company data appears on dark web forums, paste sites, or public disclosures. This is confirmation of breach, not detection.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing "anomaly" with "malicious activity"**: A resource consumption spike at 3 AM *could* be legitimate (batch jobs, scheduled backups, time zone differences in global teams). The exam tests whether you recognize it as *suspicious* and worthy of investigation, not automatically malicious. Always recommend log correlation and context before declaring a breach.

- **Assuming all missing logs are deleted by attackers**: Sometimes logs aren't collected due to misconfiguration, storage limits, or timestamp synchronization issues across systems. However, on the Security+ exam, missing logs in a breach scenario almost always point to attacker action. Context matters—read the scenario carefully.

- **Ignoring the "published data" phase**: Many candidates focus only on detection and miss that post-breach threat intelligence (data dumps, dark web leaks) is a critical IoC. Questions may ask "what should the CISO do after learning company data is on a dark web forum?" Answer: [[Incident Response]] escalation, legal/law enforcement notification, customer notification, and threat hunting for the attack vector.

## Real-World Application

In your homelab, [[Wazuh]] continuously monitors [[Active Directory]] authentication logs for impossible travel patterns (e.g., a user logging in from the office VPN, then from a remote IP in a different country within 60 seconds). Firewall logs from the edge ([[Tailscale]] exit nodes) are parsed for unusual outbound bandwidth spikes indicating potential data exfiltration. Missing logs or gaps in log timestamps trigger alerts, ensuring that any attacker attempting to cover their tracks is immediately flagged. Post-breach, threat intelligence feeds and dark web monitoring provide external confirmation of data compromise.

## Extended Study Notes

### Why IoCs Matter in Practice

Indicators of Compromise form the backbone of modern threat detection and response. Unlike preventive controls ([[Firewall]], [[IPS]], [[MFA]]), IoCs are *reactive*—they confirm that an attack has already occurred. However, they are invaluable because:

1. **They reveal the attack timeline**: Logs show when compromise began, how long the attacker persisted, and what they accessed.
2. **They enable forensics**: [[DFIR]] teams use IoCs to reconstruct attack chains and identify affected assets.
3. **They inform threat intelligence**: Published data or C2 infrastructure details help identify the attacker group and their TTP (tactics, techniques, procedures) per [[MITRE ATT&CK]].

### The Attacker's Perspective: Covering Tracks

Every IoC discussed in this section represents a trace left behind by an attacker. Sophisticated attackers will:

- Use [[Living off the Land]] techniques (native OS tools) to avoid leaving tool signatures.
- Delete or manipulate logs (application logs, Windows Event Viewer, syslog).
- Attempt to blend malicious activity with legitimate traffic (e.g., exfiltration disguised as a routine backup).
- Time attacks during high-traffic periods to avoid resource consumption anomalies.

This is why **centralized, immutable log storage** (a core function of [[SIEM]] and [[Wazuh]]) is critical. If logs are stored locally on a compromised system, attackers can delete them. If logs are sent to a remote collector in real time with no local copy, attackers cannot erase the evidence.

### Automation and Alert Fatigue

Modern [[SIEM]] platforms automatically correlate logs to detect impossible travel and other IoCs. However, the Security+ exam and real-world SOCs both struggle with **alert fatigue**—too many alerts, many false positives. Candidates must understand:

- **Baselines and anomalies**: What's "normal" for your environment? A 2 AM file transfer might be suspicious at a 9-5 office, but routine in a 24/7 NOC.
- **Tuning and correlation**: Use multiple IoCs together (impossible travel + resource spike + missing logs) to increase confidence in alerts.
- **Escalation procedures**: Not every alert triggers incident response; some are investigated and dismissed. [[Incident Response]] procedures should define when to escalate.

### Log Types and What They Reveal

| Log Type | IoCs Detected | Example |
|----------|---------------|---------|
| Authentication | Impossible travel, brute force, unusual logon times | Failed logons, logon from new IP |
| Firewall | Outbound C2, data exfiltration, policy violations | Traffic to known C2 IP, unusual egress port |
| Operating System | Out-of-cycle activity, resource consumption, file changes | Unexpected patch, CPU spike, encrypted files |
| Application | Data access, malicious queries, abuse of privileges | SQL injection attempts, unauthorized API calls |
| Proxy | Web-based exfiltration, malware C2 over HTTP/HTTPS | Large downloads to external cloud storage |

## Practice Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Impossible Travel**
A user's account shows a successful login from the New York office at 2:00 PM EST, followed by a successful login from a Tokyo IP at 2:15 PM EST. You have a 15-minute window. Should you immediately disable the account?

*Answer*: Yes. Impossible travel is a high-confidence IoC. The attacker either compromised credentials or breached a session token. Immediate action (disable account, force password reset, review recent actions) is warranted. This would also trigger investigation of how credentials were compromised (phishing, malware, credential stuffing).

**Scenario 2: Out-of-Cycle Logging**
Your patch management system shows Windows Server 2022 received a critical security patch on a Wednesday, but your organization's patch day is the second Tuesday of each month. What should you do?

*Answer*: Investigate immediately. This is an out-of-cycle patch—either:
1. An authorized emergency patch (documented and approved by change control).
2. An attacker patched a system to prevent other attackers from exploiting the same vulnerability (malware author "tidying up").
3. A system compromise or unauthorized access.

Check the patch source (WSUS, Windows Update, unauthorized tool) and audit administrative access logs.

**Scenario 3: Missing Logs**
You're investigating a breach and notice that firewall logs from 11 PM to 2 AM (the likely time of data exfiltration, based on resource consumption spikes) are absent from your [[SIEM]]. What does this indicate?

*Answer*: The absence of logs is itself evidence of compromise. The attacker likely:
1. Accessed the firewall and disabled logging or log forwarding.
2. Modified the firewall configuration to prevent log collection.
3. Deleted logs from the SIEM (if they compromised the SIEM).

This elevates the severity—the attacker had firewall/SIEM access, meaning network infrastructure was compromised, not just an endpoint.
