# 4.6 - Identity and Access Management

Identity and Access Management (IAM) is a comprehensive framework for controlling who has access to what resources, when, and under what circumstances. In modern distributed environments where applications and data span desktops, browsers, mobile devices, cloud storage, and private data centers, IAM ensures that the right people get the right permissions at the right time while preventing unauthorized access. This topic is critical for Security+ because it forms the foundation of enterprise security posture and appears frequently in scenario-based exam questions about user provisioning, authentication methods, and access control implementation.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Identity and Access Management (IAM)**: A holistic security discipline managing user/entity lifecycles, authentication, authorization, and resource access across distributed environments
  
- **The IAM Core Principle**: "Give the right permissions to the right people at the right time" — the foundational maxim of access control

- **Digital Identity**: Every entity (human and non-human) receives a unique digital representation in the system; this identity persists across the entity's lifecycle

- **Identity Lifecycle Management**: The complete process of creating, maintaining, modifying, and removing digital identities as employees are hired, transferred, promoted, or separated

- **Provisioning**: The account creation process that occurs during hiring, transfers, or role changes; includes establishing account details (name, attributes, group permissions)

- **De-provisioning**: The account removal and access revocation process that occurs during job separation or role changes; critical for security to prevent orphaned accounts

- **Permission Assignments (Least Privilege)**: Each entity receives only the minimum permissions required to perform their job function
  - **Group Assignments**: Users are added to security groups that collectively hold permissions, rather than assigning individual permissions
  - **Private Storage**: Users can have private file systems even on shared computers, isolating their data from other users on the same machine
  - **No Privileged OS Access**: User accounts should never have administrator or root-level operating system access

- **Access Control**: The enforcement mechanism ensuring entities only access resources they are authorized to use; tied directly to identity and authentication validation

- **Authentication vs. Authorization** (critical distinction):
  - **Authentication**: Proving you are who you claim to be (identity verification)
  - **Authorization**: Determining what authenticated entities are allowed to do (permission enforcement)

- **Identity Proofing**: The verification process confirming an entity's claimed identity before granting system access
  - **Resolution**: Determining who the system thinks you are based on provided information
  - **Validation**: Gathering information from the user (passwords, security questions, biometrics) to establish identity
  - **Verification/Attestation**: Confirming identity through external means (passport, in-person meeting, or automated verification systems)

- **Single Sign-On (SSO)**: An authentication mechanism allowing users to provide credentials once and gain access to all authorized resources without re-authenticating
  - **Time-Limited Sessions**: SSO tokens typically expire after a set duration (e.g., 24 hours), requiring re-authentication
  - **Infrastructure Requirement**: Underlying systems must support SSO protocols; not all legacy applications support this

- **Identity Governance**: Continuous monitoring and documentation of which resources each entity can access; often driven by regulatory requirements (compliance auditing)

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Imagine a large corporate office building with multiple departments (HR, Finance, Engineering, Marketing). Each employee receives an access card (digital identity) when hired. The card contains their name, department, and clearance level. 

- When they arrive at the building, they scan the card at the main entrance (authentication — proving they belong)
- The system checks what doors they're authorized to open based on their department and role (authorization)
- If they're promoted to a manager, their card is updated with new clearance levels (provisioning/re-provisioning)
- When they leave the company, their card is deactivated and no longer opens any doors (de-provisioning)
- If the office had a "guest pass" system (SSO), a visitor could check in once at the front desk and access all their assigned meeting rooms without showing credentials again until the pass expires

The technical reality mirrors this: [[Active Directory]], [[LDAP]], or cloud IAM systems maintain digital identities with attributes and group memberships. [[Authentication]] methods (passwords, [[MFA]], biometrics) verify identity at login. [[Authorization]] systems ([[RBAC]], [[ABAC]]) enforce what each identity can access. Lifecycle events (hiring, role changes, termination) trigger provisioning automation to keep permissions current and prevent access drift.

## Exam Tips

- **Distinguish Authentication from Authorization**: The exam loves scenario questions where you must identify whether a problem is an authentication issue (proving identity) or authorization issue (granting permissions). A user who can't log in = authentication failure. A user who logs in but can't access a file = authorization failure.

- **SSO Limitations**: Remember that SSO is a convenience feature, not a security feature. The exam may ask what happens when SSO infrastructure fails or when legacy applications don't support SSO protocols. The answer is usually "users must authenticate to each application individually" or "SSO is not an option."

- **De-provisioning as a Critical Security Control**: The exam frequently tests whether you understand that removing access when someone leaves is as important as granting access when they're hired. Watch for scenarios about former employees retaining access or contractors' accounts not being removed after engagement ends — these are de-provisioning failures.

- **Least Privilege & Group Assignments**: Expect questions about whether a user should have individual permissions or be added to a group. The best practice is almost always group-based assignment because it scales better and reduces administrative overhead. Individual permissions are harder to audit and manage.

- **Identity Proofing Levels**: The exam may present scenarios requiring different verification rigor. Remote contractors might use automated verification (security questions, email confirmation), while high-security roles might require in-person proofing or third-party identity validation.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing Provisioning with De-provisioning**: Candidates often think "provisioning" means removing accounts. Remember: **provisioning = creating/enabling**, **de-provisioning = removing/disabling**. The exam uses these terms precisely, and mixing them up will cost points on scenario questions.

- **Assuming SSO Solves All Authentication Problems**: Students sometimes believe SSO is more secure or eliminates the need for individual application authentication. SSO is convenient but introduces new attack surfaces (SSO token theft) and doesn't work if the underlying infrastructure doesn't support it. It's a user experience improvement, not a security upgrade.

- **Ignoring Identity Governance and Compliance**: Candidates underestimate how often regulatory requirements drive IAM decisions (PCI-DSS, HIPAA, SOX, GDPR). The exam tests whether you understand that IAM isn't just technical — it's also a compliance and audit function. Not documenting who accessed what resources can be a regulatory violation.

## Real-World Application

In Morpheus's COCYTUS homelab, IAM principles apply across multiple layers: [[Active Directory]] serves as the identity provider for Windows systems and can integrate with Linux systems via [[LDAP]]. [[Tailscale]] acts as an access control layer, restricting which remote systems can connect to the homelab infrastructure. The [[Wazuh]] SIEM monitors authentication events and tracks access to critical systems, providing identity governance and audit trails. When adding a new lab user or removing access after an experiment, proper provisioning/de-provisioning prevents orphaned accounts that could be exploited. Similarly, in production environments, integrating [[SAML]] or [[OAuth]] with centralized IAM platforms ensures contractors and third parties receive appropriate just-in-time access without permanent credentials.
