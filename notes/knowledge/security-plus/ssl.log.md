# ssl.log

## What it is
Like a customs manifest for every encrypted shipment crossing a border, ssl.log records the handshake metadata for every TLS/SSL session — without decrypting the cargo itself. In Zeek (formerly Bro), ssl.log captures negotiated cipher suites, certificate details, server names, and protocol versions for each encrypted connection observed on the wire.

## Why it matters
During the 2014 POODLE attack, threat hunters could identify vulnerable clients by scanning ssl.log for connections still negotiating SSLv3 — the exact protocol version being exploited — without needing to decrypt a single byte of traffic. Similarly, defenders use ssl.log to detect self-signed certificates, expired certs, or anomalous SNI values that indicate C2 communication hiding inside HTTPS.

## Key facts
- ssl.log is generated by **Zeek** and captures fields including `version`, `cipher`, `subject`, `issuer`, `validation_status`, and `server_name` (SNI)
- The `server_name` field from SNI (Server Name Indication) reveals the intended destination even in encrypted traffic, making it critical for threat hunting
- A `validation_status` of `self signed certificate` is a high-fidelity indicator of malicious or misconfigured infrastructure
- ssl.log does **not** contain payload content — it captures only handshake metadata, making it privacy-preserving but still analytically rich
- Correlating ssl.log with `uid` fields ties the TLS session to corresponding `conn.log` entries, enabling full session reconstruction
