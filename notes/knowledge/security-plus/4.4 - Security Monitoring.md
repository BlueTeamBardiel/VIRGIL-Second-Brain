# 4.4 - Security Monitoring

Security monitoring is the continuous, 24/7/365 observation and analysis of systems, networks, and applications to detect and respond to security events in real-time. This section covers the core pillars of a modern security operations capability: monitoring entry points and computing resources, log aggregation through [[SIEM]] tools, vulnerability scanning, reporting mechanisms, and long-term data archiving. Understanding these components is critical for the Security+ exam because monitoring forms the foundation of any [[Incident Response]] capability—you cannot respond to threats you don't detect.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Security Monitoring**: The continuous, real-time observation of systems, networks, applications, and user activity to identify security events, anomalies, and potential breaches. Operates on the principle that attackers are always active (24/7/365).

- **Entry Points**: All potential pathways for attack or unauthorized access, including logins, publicly available services, data storage locations, and remote access systems ([[VPN]], [[Tailscale]], bastion hosts, etc.).

- **Computing Resource Monitoring**: Oversight of three critical layers:
  - **Systems**: [[Authentication]] patterns (logins from unusual locations), service activity, backup status, software versions
  - **Applications**: Uptime/availability metrics, response times, data transfer rate anomalies, security notifications from developers
  - **Infrastructure**: Remote access systems (employee/vendor/guest), [[Firewall]] rule changes, [[IPS]] alerts and attack patterns

- **[[SIEM]]** (Security Information and Event Manager) / **SEM**: A centralized platform that aggregates logs from diverse sources (servers, [[Firewall]]s, [[VPN]] concentrators, SANs, cloud services) into a single database, enabling correlation and centralized reporting.

- **Log Aggregation**: The process of collecting and consolidating logs from multiple systems and security devices into a centralized repository for analysis, correlation, and long-term retention.

- **Scanning**: Active, proactive assessment of systems and devices to identify:
  - [[Operating System]] types, versions, and patch levels
  - Device driver versions and outdated software
  - Installed applications and their configurations
  - Configuration anomalies and deviations from baselines
  - Newly discovered vulnerabilities in real-time

- **Vulnerability Scanning**: Systematic identification of security weaknesses across systems; complements passive monitoring with active checks.

- **Reporting**: Analysis and synthesis of collected monitoring and scanning data into actionable intelligence:
  - **Status Reports**: Compliance metrics, device update status, systems running unsupported or outdated software
  - **Analytical Reports**: Response to newly discovered vulnerabilities (blast radius assessment)
  - **Ad Hoc Reports**: Prepared on-demand to support incident investigations or proactive security decisions

- **Archiving**: Long-term retention of security logs and event data to meet regulatory/legal requirements and enable historical forensic analysis. Industry data (IBM, 2022) shows companies take ~9 months on average to identify and contain a breach—archive longevity is critical to forensic success.

- **Correlation**: The ability to connect events across disparate systems (e.g., linking a failed login attempt on a firewall with an authentication event in [[Active Directory]] and an application access log).

- **Anomaly Detection**: Identifying deviations from baseline behavior in systems, networks, or user activity; a key function of modern monitoring solutions.

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Imagine you own a large apartment building with hundreds of units. Security monitoring is like having security guards stationed at every entrance (front door, fire escape, loading dock) AND having security cameras throughout common areas that feed to a central control room. The guards watch in real-time and react immediately to suspicious activity. The control room staff reviews all camera footage, correlates incidents ("Was that the same person?"), and keeps a filing cabinet of tape archives for months in case they need to review what happened during a burglary three months ago.

Now apply this to IT: Your systems are the building, logins and network traffic are the people and vehicles entering/exiting, and your [[SIEM]] is the control room receiving feeds from all your security devices ([[Firewall]]s, [[IDS]]/[[IPS]], servers, [[VPN]] gateways). Scanning is the periodic security audit where guards walk every hallway and check door locks. Reporting is the summary you present to building management: "97% of tenants have working locks, but Unit 302's lock is broken—fix it now." Archiving is the tape library you keep for 7 years to prove you followed the lease terms and security regulations.

In the technical reality, [[SIEM]] tools like [[Wazuh]] (used in Morpheus's COCYTUS fleet) ingest gigabytes of log data per day, normalize it into a searchable format, apply correlation rules to detect multi-step attacks, and generate dashboards and alerts so your [[SOC]] team knows immediately if something is wrong.

## Exam Tips

- **Distinguish [[SIEM]]/SEM from other tools**: The exam expects you to know that [[SIEM]] is specifically for *log aggregation and correlation*, not vulnerability scanning ([[Nmap]], [[Nessus]]) or packet capture ([[Wireshark]]). SIEM is the central nervous system; scanning tools are diagnostic equipment.

- **Know the "24/7/365" principle**: Exam questions often test whether you understand that security monitoring is *continuous*, not scheduled. A system you only scan once a month is vulnerable for 29 days.

- **Recognize the three layers of computing resource monitoring**: Systems (authentication/servers), Applications (availability/data transfers), Infrastructure (remote access/firewall reports). Questions may ask you to categorize where a specific metric belongs.

- **Understand log aggregation's core value**: The exam emphasizes that centralized logging enables *correlation*—the ability to see related events across systems. This is why a [[SIEM]] is better than reviewing individual server logs.

- **Remember the 9-month baseline**: The IBM 2022 statistic (9 months average to identify and contain a breach) is cited in the material and may appear on exam questions about why archiving and long-term monitoring matter. Questions may ask "Why should a company archive logs for 1+ years?" Answer: forensic investigations often start months after a breach occurs.

- **Scanning is *active* verification**: Don't confuse passive monitoring (log review) with active scanning. Scanning proactively checks system state; monitoring observes behavior. The exam may ask which is more useful for finding an unpatched system (scanning) vs. detecting a compromise in progress (monitoring).

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Conflating vulnerability scanning with monitoring**: Candidates often treat these as the same thing. Scanning is a snapshot in time; monitoring is continuous. On exam questions, if the scenario asks "How do you detect a breach in progress?", the answer is monitoring/[[SIEM]], not scanning.

- **Underestimating the importance of archiving**: Some candidates dismiss archiving as a "nice-to-have" compliance requirement, but the exam (and real-world incident response) emphasize that you cannot investigate a breach that happened 6 months ago without archived logs. Questions may ask why archiving is critical—the answer ties to forensic investigation timelines and regulatory mandates.

- **Treating [[SIEM]] as a scanning tool**: [[SIEM]] aggregates and correlates *existing* logs; it does not generate vulnerability reports. If a question asks "What tool identifies unpatched systems?", the answer is a vulnerability scanner ([[Nessus]], [[Nmap]] with NSE scripts, Qualys, etc.), not [[SIEM]]. [[SIEM]] can *report on* vulnerability scanner results if they're ingested as logs, but it doesn't perform the scan itself.

## Real-World Application

In Morpheus's COCYTUS homelab, [[Wazuh]] functions as the [[SIEM]] backbone, aggregating logs from [[Tailscale]] VPN gateways, [[Active Directory]] domain controllers, firewall systems, and all internal systems. When a suspicious login occurs at 2 AM from an unusual IP, [[Wazuh]] correlates the [[Tailscale]] VPN connection log with the [[Active Directory]] authentication log, triggers an alert, and stores the event in a searchable archive. Regular vulnerability scans (using tools like [[Nessus]] or [[OpenVAS]]) provide the baseline data; monitoring via [[Wazuh]] provides the real-time detection layer. This dual approach—active scanning plus passive continuous monitoring—is the security operations gold standard that the exam emphasizes.
