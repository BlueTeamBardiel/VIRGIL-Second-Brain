# Encryption Standards

## What it is
Think of encryption standards like standardized lock-and-key specifications published by a government agency — any manufacturer can build a compatible lock, but only the right key opens it. Encryption standards are formally defined, publicly vetted algorithms and protocols (like AES or RSA) that specify exactly how plaintext is transformed into ciphertext and back, ensuring interoperability and peer-reviewed security across systems.

## Why it matters
In 2017, researchers discovered that millions of RSA keys generated by certain smartcard chips were vulnerable due to a flawed implementation of RSA key generation — not a flaw in the RSA standard itself, but in a vendor's deviation from it. This "ROCA" vulnerability demonstrated that following the standard precisely matters as much as choosing the right standard, and that weak or nonstandard implementations can silently undermine otherwise sound cryptographic designs.

## Key facts
- **AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)** uses 128-bit blocks with 128, 192, or 256-bit key sizes; it replaced DES after DES's 56-bit key became brute-forceable in under 24 hours
- **DES and 3DES** are considered legacy/deprecated — NIST officially deprecated 3DES in 2023
- **RSA** is an asymmetric standard relying on integer factorization hardness; key sizes below 2048 bits are considered insufficient for modern use
- **Symmetric encryption** (AES) is faster and used for bulk data; **asymmetric encryption** (RSA, ECC) is used for key exchange and digital signatures
- **NIST's Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) project** finalized its first quantum-resistant standards in 2024 (e.g., CRYSTALS-Kyber, CRYSTALS-Dilithium), replacing RSA/ECC against future quantum threats
