# 5.5 - Audits and Assessments

Audits and assessments are systematic evaluations of an organization's security posture, IT infrastructure, and compliance with regulatory requirements. This topic covers both **internal audits** (self-directed evaluations) and **third-party audits** (external expert reviews), as well as the concept of **attestation**—where auditors provide formal opinions on the accuracy of security claims. For the Security+ exam, understanding the distinction between audit types, the role of compliance verification, and who oversees the audit process is critical for demonstrating competency in security program management.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Cybersecurity Audit**
  - A formal examination of IT infrastructure, software, devices, and systems
  - Evaluates the **effectiveness of security policies and procedures**
  - Identifies vulnerabilities **before attackers exploit them** (proactive threat discovery)
  - Can be conducted **internally** (in-house team) or by a **third-party** (external firm)

- **Attestation**
  - A formal statement or declaration by an auditor
  - Provides an **opinion of truth or accuracy** regarding a company's security posture
  - Auditors attest to (formally certify) the validity of security claims
  - Often tied to regulatory or industry standards compliance

- **Internal Audits**
  - Audits conducted **by the organization itself** (not outsourced)
  - Demonstrates organizational commitment to continuous improvement
  - Can be more frequent and proactive than external audits
  - Findings are reviewed by internal **audit committees**

- **Compliance Assessment**
  - Determines whether the organization meets **regulatory or industry requirements**
  - Examples: [[HIPAA]], [[PCI-DSS]], [[GDPR]], [[SOC 2]], [[ISO 27001]]
  - Verifies adherence to legal mandates and contractual obligations
  - Often a driver for both internal and external audits

- **Audit Committee**
  - Governance body responsible for **overseeing risk management activities**
  - **All audits start and stop with the committee** (they commission and approve audits)
  - Reviews audit findings and recommendations
  - Ensures management implements remediation

- **Self-Assessments**
  - The organization performs its own security checks and evaluations
  - Employees or internal teams conduct questionnaires, reviews, and testing
  - Results are **consolidated into ongoing reports** for tracking progress
  - Lower cost than external audits but may lack objectivity

- **Third-Party Audits**
  - External firms (auditors, consultants) conduct independent evaluations
  - Provides unbiased assessment and credibility to audit results
  - Often required for regulatory compliance or certification
  - More expensive but higher external credibility

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**Think of an audit like a home inspection before selling a house.** A home inspector walks through, checks the roof, plumbing, electrical, and structural integrity, then writes a report: "This house is sound, but the roof needs work in 2 years." The inspector attests to the home's condition. Now imagine the homeowner also does their own checklist monthly (self-assessment)—checking for leaks, testing outlets. They keep notes (ongoing reports). Some owners hire an inspector annually (internal audit), and others only hire one when selling (third-party audit for compliance).

**In security:** Your organization is the homeowner. Your IT infrastructure is the house. An audit is the inspection. Attestation is the inspector's formal declaration. An audit committee is the board that decides when inspections happen. Self-assessments are your own checklists. Compliance audits verify the house meets building codes ([[NIST]], [[PCI-DSS]], etc.). The goal? Find problems before they collapse your security posture—before attackers do.

## Exam Tips

- **Distinguish audit types by **source**:** Internal = in-house, Third-party = external firm. The exam tests which type is appropriate for a given scenario.

- **Attestation ≠ Audit:** Attestation is the **auditor's formal opinion/declaration** of security truth. An audit is the **process** that leads to attestation. Don't confuse the two.

- **Audit Committee is the **gatekeeper**:** Remember: "All audits start and stop with the committee." Exam questions often test whether you know the committee **approves** audits, not the other way around.

- **Self-assessments are **continuous and ongoing**:** The exam emphasizes that self-assessments feed into **ongoing reports**, not one-time checks. This supports **continuous improvement** in your security program.

- **Compliance is a **primary driver**:** Questions about "why audit?" often expect answers tied to regulatory requirements ([[HIPAA]], [[PCI-DSS]], [[SOC 2]]) or industry standards. Know why compliance audits matter.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating audits as optional.** Candidates mistakenly think audits are "nice-to-have." Audits—especially compliance audits—are **mandatory** in regulated industries. The exam expects you to recognize audits as a core security program control.

- **Confusing attestation with audit.** Some test-takers think "attestation" is another word for "audit." It's not. **Attestation is the auditor's formal opinion/declaration** that results **from** an audit. Know the difference.

- **Misunderstanding the audit committee's role.** Candidates sometimes think the audit committee *conducts* audits. Wrong. The committee **commissions, oversees, and approves** audits. It's governance, not execution.

## Real-World Application

In Morpheus's [[COCYTUS]] homelab, periodic audits of [[Active Directory]] users, [[Wazuh]] agent configurations, and [[Tailscale]] network access controls ensure compliance with the security baseline. Self-assessments—testing [[MFA]] enforcement, reviewing firewall rules, checking [[Wazuh]] alert thresholds—happen quarterly. A third-party security firm might conduct an annual attestation audit to verify that the homelab's security posture matches documented policies, especially if the lab simulates enterprise compliance requirements (e.g., [[SOC 2]]). This mirrors real-world sysadmin responsibilities: audits aren't just bureaucracy; they're continuous visibility into whether your infrastructure is actually secure.
