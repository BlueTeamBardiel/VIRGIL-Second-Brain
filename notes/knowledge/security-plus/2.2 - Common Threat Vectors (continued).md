# 2.2 - Common Threat Vectors (continued)

This section explores the primary attack pathways that threat actors exploit to compromise systems and networks. Understanding these vectors is critical for the Security+ exam because they form the foundation of threat modeling, risk assessment, and mitigation strategy—roughly 22% of the exam focuses on threats, vulnerabilities, and mitigations. You'll encounter scenario-based questions asking you to identify which vector applies to a given situation and what controls would best address each threat.

---

## Key Concepts

### Vulnerable Software Vectors
- **Client-based vulnerabilities**: Infected executables or known/unknown vulnerabilities in client applications requiring constant updates and patches
- **Agentless vulnerabilities**: Compromises on the server side that affect all users without requiring a deployed client executable; users run fresh instances each time
- **Attack surface expansion**: Every piece of software increases the number of potential entry points for attackers

### Unsupported Systems Vectors
- **Patching limitations**: Unsupported systems no longer receive security patches—a critical prevention tool becomes unavailable
- **End-of-life (EOL) systems**: Manufacturers eventually cease support, leaving known vulnerabilities permanently unfixed
- **Inventory tracking**: A single unsecured system can become a network entry point; accurate asset management is essential
- **Legacy OS risks**: Outdated operating systems remain vulnerable to years of publicly disclosed exploits

### Unsecure Network Vectors
- **Wireless protocols**: Outdated standards ([[WEP]], [[WPA]], [[WPA2]]) are cryptographically broken; open/rogue networks provide no encryption
- **Wired vulnerabilities**: Lack of [[802.1X]] port-based authentication allows unauthorized physical access to network infrastructure
- **Bluetooth risks**: Reconnaissance attacks and implementation vulnerabilities allow nearby attackers to discover and exploit Bluetooth devices
- **Unencrypted data**: Non-encrypted network traffic can be passively intercepted and read by any network observer
- **Centralized connectivity**: Networks connect everything—compromising one point may grant access to all connected systems

### Open Service Ports
- **Port as attack surface**: Every open port is a direct invitation to attackers; each represents a potential [[vulnerability]] or misconfiguration
- **Application-specific ports**: Each application binds to its own port; more services = larger attack surface
- **Firewall rules trade-off**: Rules must explicitly allow traffic to open ports, creating a balance between functionality and security
- **Service enumeration**: Attackers use port scanning ([[Nmap]]) to identify running services and target known vulnerabilities

### Default Credentials
- **Ubiquitous weak defaults**: Most network devices (routers, access points, printers, switches) ship with documented default usernames and passwords
- **Administrative access**: Default credentials often provide full system control, including ability to modify security settings
- **Trivial lookup**: Resources like https://www.routerpasswords.com make finding defaults effortless for attackers
- **Critical remediation**: Changing default credentials is one of the easiest and most important hardening steps

### Supply Chain Vectors
- **Manufacturing compromise**: Attackers can tamper with hardware or software during the manufacturing/distribution process, inserting backdoors or flaws
- **MSP leverage**: Managed Service Providers access multiple customer networks from centralized locations; compromising an MSP provides access to all clients
- **Vendor trust abuse**: Legitimate vendor relationships can be weaponized to gain network access (e.g., 2013 Target breach via HVAC contractor)
- **Counterfeit equipment**: Fake networking equipment may include backdoors, substandard performance, or intentional vulnerabilities (e.g., 2020 fake Cisco Catalyst switches)
- **Third-party risk**: Suppliers, vendors, and partners represent extended attack surface and organizational risk beyond direct control

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Imagine your organization is a castle. **Threat vectors** are all the different ways an invader could breach it:

- **Vulnerable software** = a knight with a known weakness in his armor that an attacker can exploit
- **Unsupported systems** = a section of the castle wall that the guards stopped maintaining and patrolling
- **Unsecure networks** = all the gates, windows, and secret tunnels connecting the castle—if they're poorly guarded or unencrypted, anyone in the surrounding area can walk through or eavesdrop
- **Open ports** = literally open doors in your fortress walls; each one is an entry point an attacker can attempt
- **Default credentials** = locks on the main gates that use the manufacturer's "universal key" (which everyone knows about)
- **Supply chain** = trusting a blacksmith to forge your swords, only to discover he secretly installed tracking devices in each one

In reality, every organization has **multiple simultaneous threat vectors**. Your job as a security professional is to identify which ones matter most (risk assessment), prioritize remediation, and implement controls that either eliminate the vector entirely or reduce the likelihood of successful exploitation.

## Exam Tips

- **Scenario matching**: You'll see questions like *"A user downloads malware from a phishing email. Which threat vector does this represent?"* → This is a **client-based vulnerable software vector**. Know how to classify threats quickly.

- **Vector vs. Control distinction**: The exam tests whether you can identify both the vector AND the appropriate control. For example:
  - Vector: **open service ports** → Control: [[firewall]] rules restricting unnecessary ports
  - Vector: **default credentials** → Control: mandatory credential change during setup
  - Vector: **unsupported systems** → Control: EOL policy + migration planning

- **Supply chain depth**: Memorize the three components: **MSPs** (access from one location to many customers), **vendors/suppliers** (legitimate trust relationships), and **manufacturing** (hardware-level tampering). The Target and Cisco examples are likely exam favorites.

- **Wireless > Wired fallacy**: Candidates often think wireless is the primary network threat. The exam tests that **both wired and wireless** are equally risky without proper security (802.1X, WPA3, encryption). An unsecured wired port may be less visible but just as exploitable.

- **Default credentials are "free access"**: On the exam, when a question mentions default credentials, the answer almost always involves "change them immediately" or recognizing them as a critical control gap. Don't overthink it.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing "unsupported" with "unpatched"**: Unsupported systems *can't* be patched by the manufacturer—this is worse than outdated systems that *can* receive patches. Candidates often treat these identically.

- **Underestimating supply chain risk**: Many candidates skip supply chain vectors as "less technical" than malware or exploits. The exam weights this heavily because it's a modern, realistic threat (Target, SolarWinds, 3CX). Know the three sub-vectors: MSPs, vendors, and manufacturing.

- **Thinking "open ports" are only a firewall problem**: Candidates see "open port" and immediately jump to "firewall rules." While that's one control, the underlying issue is unnecessary services running. The best control is to disable the service entirely or restrict it to specific [[VLANs]]/IP ranges. Firewall rules are a secondary control.

---

## Real-World Application

In your homelab, this section directly applies to hardening the fleet: audit all running services on each node and close unnecessary ports (reducing attack surface), enforce [[WPA3]] or better on wireless networks, document all credentials in a secure vault rather than using defaults, and regularly patch all systems via automation. For a small MSP or homelab operating Wazuh, applying supply chain risk awareness means vetting all third-party monitoring integrations and being cautious about which cloud services have access to your infrastructure telemetry—a compromised log aggregation service could expose all security data.
