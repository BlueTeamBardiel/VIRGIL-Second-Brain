# 2.4 - Indicators of Compromise

Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) are observable artifacts and events within a system or network that signify a successful intrusion or active threat with high confidence. Understanding IOCs is critical for Security+ candidates because they form the foundation of threat detection, [[SIEM]] operations, and [[Incident Response]] workflows. The exam expects you to recognize common IOCs, distinguish them from false positives, and understand how organizations use them to detect and investigate breaches in real time.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Indicator of Compromise (IOC)**: An event or artifact that confirms or strongly suggests that a system or network has been breached. High confidence = high priority response.
  
- **Unusual Network Activity**: Unexpected spikes in bandwidth consumption, data transfers to unknown IPs, or protocol anomalies that deviate from baseline behavior.

- **File Hash Changes**: Modifications to file hashes (MD5, SHA-1, SHA-256) indicate files have been altered, potentially by malware or unauthorized access. Critical for detecting [[Malware]] modifications.

- **Irregular International Traffic**: Network flows originating from or destined to unexpected geographic locations, often a sign of data exfiltration, [[C2 (Command and Control)]] communication, or compromised accounts.

- **DNS Data Changes**: Unauthorized modifications to [[DNS]] records, DNS cache poisoning, or queries to malicious domains; indicates potential [[DNS]] hijacking or [[Malware]] beaconing.

- **Uncommon Login Patterns**: Authentication events that deviate from user baselines—unusual times, atypical devices, rapid-fire failed attempts, or privilege escalation anomalies.

- **Spikes in Read Requests**: Abnormal increases in file access requests to specific resources; suggests data reconnaissance, lateral movement, or preparation for exfiltration.

- **Concurrent Session Usage**: A user logged in from multiple geographic locations or devices simultaneously in a way that violates physical possibility or organizational policy.
  - Example: User authenticated from New York at 2 PM and Tokyo at 2:15 PM—impossible without cloning.
  - Indicates credential compromise, [[MFA]] bypass, or [[Session Hijacking]].

- **Account Lockout**: Credentials failing repeatedly, triggering automatic account disabling; may indicate brute-force attacks, password spray, or (conversely) legitimate user lockout.
  - **Critical distinction**: Is this accidental or malicious? Attackers sometimes lock accounts as a distraction.

- **Blocked Content**: Security tools (firewall, proxy, [[IDS]]/[[IPS]]) blocking outbound connections to known malicious sites, patch repositories, or C2 infrastructure.
  - Attacker strategy: Block auto-updates and patch downloads to prevent remediation.
  - Evidence: Blocked connections to Windows Update, third-party anti-malware update servers, or security patch sites.

---

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**Imagine a bank vault:** Security guards monitor the vault door for signs of tampering—scratches on the lock, unusual foot traffic patterns at odd hours, repeated failed key attempts, or someone's ID badge being used in two cities at once. None of these individually proves a robbery, but *together* they form a pattern that says, "Something's wrong here."

In cybersecurity, IOCs are the "scratches on the lock"—observable evidence that an attacker has touched your system. A single IOC might be a false alarm, but clusters of IOCs (unusual login + file hash change + blocked update connection) form a high-confidence signal that you've been breached.

**The technical reality:** Your [[SIEM]] (like [[Wazuh]]) collects logs from every device on your network—firewalls, servers, domain controllers, endpoints. It compares current events against baselines and known-bad indicators (threat intelligence feeds from [[MITRE ATT&CK]], vendor feeds, etc.). When it sees a match—a hash known to be malicious, a domain known to host [[C2]], a login pattern that breaks the user's baseline—it raises an alert. Your [[SOC]] team then investigates.

---

## Exam Tips

- **IOC ≠ Proof of Breach**: An IOC is a *strong indicator*, not definitive proof. The exam may ask you to distinguish between confirmed compromises and suspicious activity requiring investigation. Stay precise with language.

- **Baseline is Everything**: You can't detect "uncommon login patterns" without knowing what "common" looks like for that user. The exam tests your understanding that detection requires baseline knowledge. New employees or systems without baselines = harder to detect anomalies.

- **Concurrent Sessions are a Red Flag**: If an exam question mentions a user logged in from two different countries within minutes, that's almost always an IOC. Recognize this instantly.

- **Account Lockout: Attacker Tactic or Accident?**: The exam may present account lockouts as either a sign of brute-force attack *or* as cover for social engineering (attacker locks account, calls help desk to reset password). Read carefully to determine context.

- **Blocked Content = Persistence Risk**: When a system tries to download patches and is blocked (by attacker-modified firewall rules or proxy settings), it's an IOC *and* a sign the attacker is trying to prevent remediation. The exam tests whether you understand this two-layer implication.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing IOCs with Vulnerabilities**: An IOC is evidence of *exploitation*. A vulnerability is a weakness. The exam tests whether you know IOCs come *after* a breach begins, while vulnerabilities exist before. Don't mix them.

- **Assuming All Concurrent Sessions Are Malicious**: Some organizations legitimately use VPNs, [[Tailscale]], or distributed teams. The exam may include a "trick" scenario where concurrent sessions are normal. Context matters—look for *policy violations*, not just geographic impossibility alone.

- **Missing the Account Lockout Social Engineering Angle**: Many candidates see "account lockout" and think "brute-force attack." But the exam often tests whether you recognize that attackers use lockouts as a *pretext* to contact the help desk and reset the password through social engineering. Don't assume the obvious cause.

---

## Real-World Application

In your homelab, [[Wazuh]] continuously monitors [[Active Directory]] logon events, file integrity (via [[FIM]]), and network traffic captured by [[IDS]] rules. When a suspicious pattern emerges—a user from the lab accessing resources at 3 AM, or a sudden spike in DNS queries to external domains—Wazuh raises an alert. you use these IOCs to trigger [[Incident Response]] workflows: isolate the host via [[Tailscale]] network controls, collect forensics, and determine scope. In production, this same process scales: a SOC team uses IOCs to triage thousands of daily events and focus resources on high-confidence breaches.

## Study Questions (Self-Test)

1. A user's account is locked after 5 failed login attempts. Is this necessarily an IOC, or is it a security control? What context would make it an IOC?

2. You observe a user logged in from New York at 10:00 AM and from Singapore at 10:05 AM. Is this definitely a compromised account? What other explanations might exist?

3. Your firewall blocks an outbound connection from a server to `windowsupdate.microsoft.com`. The attacker modified the firewall rules to block this. Is the blocked connection itself the IOC, or is it something else?

4. What is the difference between a *file hash change* IOC and a *hash-based malware signature* used in threat intelligence?

5. How would you distinguish between an account lockout caused by brute-force attack vs. one caused by a user forgetting their password?
