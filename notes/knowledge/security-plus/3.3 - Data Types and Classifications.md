# 3.3 - Data Types and Classifications

Understanding data types and classifications is fundamental to designing secure systems and implementing appropriate access controls. The Security+ exam tests your ability to recognize different categories of sensitive data, distinguish between [[PII]] and [[PHI]], and apply the correct security controls based on classification levels. This section bridges the gap between identifying what data exists in an organization and determining how to protect it—a critical skill for security architects and system administrators.

## Key Concepts

### Data Types (By Nature & Format)

- **Regulated Data**: Information managed by third-party oversight or governed by government laws and statutes (e.g., HIPAA, GDPR, PCI-DSS). Non-compliance carries legal penalties.
- **Trade Secrets**: Proprietary formulas, processes, or methods unique to an organization. Loss compromises competitive advantage.
- **Intellectual Property (IP)**: Organization-owned creations that may be publicly visible but are protected by copyright, trademark, or patent law.
- **Legal Information**: Court records, attorney communications, judge documents, and associated [[PII]] typically stored across multiple systems.
- **Financial Information**: Internal company financials, customer payment records, credit card data, bank records, and transaction histories.
- **Human-Readable Data**: Plaintext information easily understood by humans without decoding (e.g., documents, spreadsheets, emails).
- **Non-Human-Readable Data**: Encoded, encrypted, or binary formats not immediately interpretable by humans (barcodes, images, encrypted files, encoded data streams).
- **Hybrid Formats**: Structured data formats containing both human and machine-readable elements (CSV, XML, JSON, YAML).

### Data Classifications (By Sensitivity Level)

- **Proprietary**: Data owned by the organization; may include trade secrets and unique organizational information. Loss impacts competitive standing.
- **PII (Personally Identifiable Information)**: Any data that can identify an individual—name, date of birth, mother's maiden name, biometric data, Social Security number, driver's license number. [[PII]] requires [[NIST]] and regulatory compliance measures.
- **PHI (Protected Health Information)**: Health-related data associated with individuals—health status, medical records, healthcare payments, diagnoses. Governed by [[HIPAA]] in the United States.
- **Sensitive**: Classification for intellectual property, [[PII]], and [[PHI]]. Requires mandatory access controls and audit logging.
- **Confidential**: Highly sensitive data requiring explicit approval to access. May require additional permissions, restricted network access, and formal audit trails.
- **Public / Unclassified**: Data with no access restrictions. Examples include marketing materials, public whitepapers, published documentation.
- **Private / Classified / Restricted**: Data with restricted access requiring NDAs, clearances, or role-based authorization. Often compartmentalized.
- **Critical**: Data essential for business continuity; availability is paramount. Loss of access creates operational impact. Often overlaps with other classifications.

### Key Distinctions

| Aspect | Regulated | Trade Secret | Intellectual Property |
|--------|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Managed By** | Third-party/Government | Organization internally | Copyright/Trademark law |
| **Visibility** | Often confidential | Confidential | May be public |
| **Loss Impact** | Legal/Financial penalties | Competitive disadvantage | IP theft, legal action |

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Imagine a hospital as an organization. Different information requires different levels of protection:

- **Highly Sensitive (Confidential/Critical)**: Patient medical records are like vault contents—only authorized doctors and nurses can access them. If they're breached, federal law (HIPAA) requires notification and carries massive fines.
- **Sensitive (Proprietary)**: The hospital's proprietary surgical technique is a trade secret. It's not public, but staff who work with it know it. Leaking it costs the hospital competitive advantage.
- **Public**: The hospital's visiting hours and location are posted on the website—anyone can see them, and there's no security concern.
- **Format Matters**: A patient name on a spreadsheet (human-readable) is much easier to misuse than encrypted medical data (non-human-readable). A CSV file with both patient names and diagnoses is a hybrid format—partly readable.

**Technical Reality**: Organizations must map data types (what it is) to classifications (how sensitive), then apply matching security controls: [[encryption]] for confidential data, [[MFA]] for access, audit logging, and [[DLP|Data Loss Prevention]] rules to prevent exfiltration.

## Exam Tips

- **Know the definitions cold**: The exam frequently presents scenarios like "A company stores customer credit card numbers in plaintext. What classification should this data have?" Answer: **Confidential or Restricted** (at minimum). Credit card data is regulated (PCI-DSS) and financially sensitive.

- **PII vs. PHI distinction**: PII is *any* personal identifier (name, SSN, DOB). PHI is *health-specific* personal information. Expect scenario questions: "A health insurance company has employee Social Security numbers → PII. Employee health claims → PHI." Both require protection, but PHI has stricter HIPAA rules.

- **Classification ≠ Encryption**: A common trap—students confuse data classification with the security controls applied to it. Classification is the *label*; [[encryption]], [[access control]], and auditing are the *controls*. The exam tests whether you apply the right control to the right classification.

- **Hybrid formats and human-readability**: CSV, JSON, and XML files are human-readable in structure but may contain sensitive data. If a JSON API response contains [[PII]] or [[PHI]], it's still sensitive data even though the format itself is "readable." Don't assume format = classification.

- **Regulatory overlap**: Expect questions testing your knowledge of data types that fall under *multiple* regulations (e.g., customer health records = both [[PII]] and [[PHI]], requiring GDPR *and* HIPAA compliance). Answer: "Apply the stricter standard."

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing "Proprietary" with "Trade Secret"**: Proprietary data is property of the organization; trade secrets are formulas/processes with competitive value. Not all proprietary data is a trade secret, and not all trade secrets are proprietary (e.g., you could license a trade secret). On the exam, if asked "What classification protects unique formulas?" → Trade Secret.

- **Assuming all regulated data requires encryption**: Regulated data (like data under GDPR) requires protection, but regulation doesn't *mandate* encryption specifically—it mandates "appropriate technical and organizational measures." The exam may test whether you select encryption as *one option* among many controls, not the *only* option.

- **Missing that data classification is organization-dependent**: The exam material states "not all data has the same level of categorization." A license tag number might be Public in one organization, but Restricted in a law enforcement agency tracking suspects. Don't assume absolute classification levels; context matters. Answer by reading the scenario carefully.

## Real-World Application

In **your YOUR-LAB fleet**, a mix of systems (Active Directory, Wazuh SIEM, Tailscale VPN, Pi-hole DNS) handles data with different classifications:

- **Confidential/Restricted**: Active Directory stores employee credentials and group memberships—classified as Confidential, requiring [[MFA]], encryption in transit (LDAPS), and audit logging via [[Wazuh]].
- **Sensitive**: Wazuh log data may contain [[PII]] (usernames, IP addresses) and security events—classified as Sensitive, requiring restricted [[VPN]] access via [[Tailscale]] and retention policies.
- **Public**: Pi-hole blocklist statistics and general network health dashboards → Public, no special access controls needed.

A sysadmin must tag data at ingestion, apply [[DLP]] rules, and ensure [[encryption]] and access controls match the classification. This directly prevents scenarios where sensitive logs are accidentally exposed or unencrypted credentials leak.
