# 5.2 - Business Impact Analysis

Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is the process of determining the potential effects of disruptions to critical business functions and services. This section focuses on the recovery-focused metrics that organizations use to plan and measure resilience: **Recovery Time Objective (RTO)**, **Recovery Point Objective (RPO)**, **Mean Time to Repair (MTTR)**, and **Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF)**. Understanding these metrics is essential for the Security+ exam because they form the foundation of [[Incident Response]] planning, [[Disaster Recovery]] strategies, and business continuity policy decisions.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Recovery Time Objective (RTO)**
  - The maximum acceptable downtime for a critical system or service
  - Measured from the moment a failure/incident occurs to the moment full service is restored
  - Example: "Our email system must be back online within 4 hours"
  - RTO is a *business decision*, not a technical one—it reflects acceptable business loss
  - Shorter RTO = higher recovery costs (redundancy, faster failover systems)

- **Recovery Point Objective (RPO)**
  - The maximum acceptable amount of data loss during a failure
  - Measured in time: "How far back does my data go after recovery?"
  - Example: "Our database can lose up to 1 hour of transactions"
  - RPO determines backup frequency: if RPO = 1 hour, you must back up every hour
  - Shorter RPO = more frequent backups = higher storage/bandwidth costs

- **Mean Time to Repair (MTTR)**
  - The average time required to diagnose and fix an issue after it's detected
  - Includes: detection time + diagnosis time + repair time + testing time
  - A *measured metric* based on historical incident data, not a target
  - Lower MTTR indicates better support processes and faster response capabilities
  - Used to calculate actual recovery cost after an incident occurs

- **Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF)**
  - The average time elapsed between unplanned outages on a system
  - Calculated: Total uptime / Number of failures during a period
  - Example: "Our server has MTBF of 720 hours = one failure every 30 days"
  - Used for *predictive planning*: "Based on historical data, expect ~12 outages per year"
  - Higher MTBF = more reliable infrastructure

- **Distinction: RTO vs. RPO vs. MTTR**
  - **RTO** = target recovery time (what we *want* to achieve)
  - **MTTR** = actual average repair time (what we *historically* achieve)
  - **RPO** = maximum acceptable data loss (how current does data need to be?)
  - These three work together: minimize MTTR to meet RTO; increase backup frequency to meet RPO

- **Distinction: MTBF vs. MTTR**
  - **MTBF** = time *between* failures (reliability metric)
  - **MTTR** = time to *repair* after failure (recoverability metric)
  - Combined: Higher MTBF + Lower MTTR = more resilient system

- **Cost-Benefit Trade-offs**
  - Shorter RTO requires more investment: redundant systems, failover infrastructure, remote sites
  - More frequent backups (shorter RPO) require more storage, bandwidth, and management overhead
  - Organizations balance cost against acceptable business impact

---

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**Imagine a restaurant's ice cream machine breaks down:**

- **MTBF** = "On average, the machine breaks once every 6 months" (reliability)
- **MTTR** = "It takes the repair technician 4 hours to fix it when it breaks" (repair speed)
- **RTO** = "We've decided we can't go without ice cream for more than 2 hours; after that, we lose too many customers" (our business limit)
- **RPO** = "We need to know customer orders from the last 30 minutes; losing more than that is unacceptable" (data we can afford to lose)

If MTTR (4 hours) exceeds RTO (2 hours), **we have a problem**: the machine can't be fixed in time before business impact becomes unacceptable. The solution might be to buy a backup machine (reduce MTTR), or buy a faster repair contract (reduce MTTR), or accept longer closures (increase RTO).

**Technical reality:** In a [[Data Center]] or [[Cloud]] environment, these metrics translate to:
- MTBF = how often a server/database/application fails (uptime percentage)
- MTTR = how fast [[Incident Response]] and recovery procedures execute
- RTO = backup/disaster recovery system activation time
- RPO = backup frequency (hourly, daily, continuous replication)

---

## Exam Tips

- **RTO vs. RPO confusion is the #1 trap**: RTO is about *time to recover* (when do we get running again?), RPO is about *data currency* (how recent is our data?). Study examples of each separately.

- **MTTR is measured, not targeted**: The exam often asks "what is MTTR?" The answer is calculated from historical data, not a goal we set. RTO is the goal; MTTR is the reality. If MTTR > RTO, we have a problem.

- **Shorter RTO/RPO = higher cost**: A common exam question frames this as a policy/budget trade-off. "To meet a 1-hour RTO, we need X investment." Know that aggressive targets require redundancy, replication, and frequent backups—all expensive.

- **MTBF predicts future failures**: Recognize that MTBF is used for *statistical planning*, not reactive response. "Based on MTBF, we expect 3 failures next quarter; plan maintenance windows accordingly."

- **Watch for scenarios linking metrics to architecture**: If an exam question says "RPO is 15 minutes, RTO is 1 hour," you should think: "We need hourly backups *minimum*, and a failover system that can spin up in under 1 hour." Connect metrics to actual recovery infrastructure.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing MTTR with RTO**: Candidates think "MTTR is our target recovery time" when it's actually the *historical average* of how long repairs took. RTO is the target we set; MTTR is what we historically achieve. If MTTR > RTO, the infrastructure is inadequate.

- **Forgetting that RPO drives backup frequency**: Students miss that "RPO of 4 hours" directly translates to "backup every 4 hours." If you want RPO of 1 hour, you need hourly backups—which has storage and bandwidth costs. The exam tests whether you understand this dependency.

- **Treating all metrics as independent**: These four metrics are *interconnected* in planning. You can't just pick arbitrary numbers—shorter RTO requires faster MTTR (through redundancy/failover), which costs money. Shorter RPO requires more frequent backups. The exam rewards recognizing these trade-offs.

## Real-World Application

In Morpheus's [[COCYTUS]] homelab, these metrics would apply to [[Active Directory]] replication targets: "If the primary AD server fails, RTO = 15 minutes (time to promote a replica DC), RPO = 5 minutes (replication frequency), MTTR = 10 minutes (how long it actually takes to fix). MTBF is measured by tracking how often the primary fails (goal: once per year)." For [[Wazuh]] or [[Splunk]] log aggregation, an organization might set RTO = 1 hour (logs must be queryable within 1 hour of failure) and RPO = 15 minutes (acceptable data loss before last backup). A sysadmin must size backup storage and failover capacity to meet these targets without bankrupting the company.
