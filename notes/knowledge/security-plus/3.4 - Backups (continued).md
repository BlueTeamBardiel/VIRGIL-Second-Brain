# 3.4 - Backups (continued)

Backups are a critical component of [[Security Architecture]] and business continuity planning. This section covers advanced backup strategies including [[Snapshots]], [[Replication]], and [[Journaling]], as well as the equally important practice of recovery testing and validation. For the Security+ exam, understanding not just *how* to back up data, but *how to verify those backups work* is essential—a backup that cannot be restored is worthless.

---

## Key Concepts

- **[[Snapshots]]**: Point-in-time copies of an entire system state, popular in virtualized and cloud environments. Incremental snapshots after the first capture only changes since the last snapshot, enabling fast recovery and efficient storage.

- **Incremental vs. Full Snapshots**: A full snapshot captures the entire system; incremental snapshots after the first contain only deltas (changes), reducing storage overhead and backup time.

- **Recovery Testing (Disaster Recovery Testing)**: The practice of simulating a disaster scenario, restoring data from backup, and confirming the restoration is successful. This is *not optional*—it is mandatory for compliance and operational readiness.

- **Restoration Confirmation**: After a recovery, you must test the restored application and data to ensure they function correctly. A backup that restores corrupted or non-functional data is a failed backup.

- **Periodic Audits**: Regular, scheduled reviews of backup integrity (weekly, monthly, quarterly). These audits verify that backups exist, are complete, and are recoverable.

- **[[Replication]]**: An ongoing, near-real-time synchronization of data across multiple physical or geographical locations. Data is always available because multiple copies exist simultaneously.

- **Data Synchronization**: Replication keeps data consistent across all replicated locations, ensuring no single point of failure for critical data.

- **[[Journaling]]**: A data integrity mechanism that logs intended write operations *before* committing them to storage. If a failure occurs during a write (e.g., power loss), the journal allows the system to recover or roll back without corruption.

- **Journal Entry Sequence**: Write intent to journal → Write data to storage → Update/clear journal entry. This three-step process ensures atomicity.

- **Corruption Prevention**: Journaling prevents data corruption when unexpected failures (power outages, crashes) occur mid-write operation.

---

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**Snapshots** are like taking a photograph of your entire house at a specific moment. If you take a photo today and another tomorrow, the second photo only needs to capture what *changed* since yesterday—you don't re-photograph the walls. If disaster strikes, you can "rewind" to any photo and restore your house to that exact state. This is fast because you're only storing differences, not redundant information.

**Replication** is like having two identical twin houses in different cities that stay perfectly synchronized. Whatever happens in House A (new furniture, paint, repairs) instantly happens in House B. If House A burns down, House B is ready to use immediately—zero downtime.

**Journaling** is like keeping a checklist before you move heavy furniture. Before moving the couch, you write "moving couch from living room to bedroom." You move it. Then you check off the list. If the power goes out mid-move and you black out, you can look at the checklist and know exactly where you left off, preventing a corrupted state.

**Recovery Testing** is the practice that separates sysadmins who sleep well at night from those who don't: actually *using* your snapshots or replicas to restore a system and confirming it works. Backups are useless if you've never proven they restore correctly.

## Exam Tips

- **Know the Three Backup Strategies**: Snapshots (point-in-time, efficient), Replication (real-time, multiple locations), and Journaling (data integrity during writes). The exam may ask which is appropriate for a given scenario.

- **Recovery Testing is Mandatory**: The exam will test your understanding that *performing* backups is only half the battle. You must regularly test restoration. Look for answer choices that emphasize "testing," "simulating," or "validating" recovery—these are correct.

- **Incremental vs. Full**: Snapshots can be incremental (after the first full snapshot). Understand that the first snapshot is full; subsequent ones are deltas. This is more efficient but requires the full snapshot to restore.

- **Journaling ≠ Backup**: Journaling is a *data integrity* mechanism, not a backup strategy. It prevents corruption during writes. Don't confuse it with snapshots or replication. A question might ask "what prevents corruption when power fails mid-write?"—the answer is journaling, not snapshots.

- **Audit Frequency (Weekly/Monthly/Quarterly)**: The exam may present a scenario asking about compliance or best practices. Periodic audits (mentioned as weekly, monthly, quarterly) are best practice. More frequent = better, but at minimum, regular testing is required.

- **Replication ≠ Backup**: Replication keeps data synchronized in real-time but does *not* protect against logical corruption, ransomware, or accidental deletion (if the deletion replicates instantly). Combined with backups, replication is powerful; alone, it is not sufficient.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming a Backup Exists = Disaster Ready**: Candidates often miss that the exam distinguishes between "performing backups" and "validating backups work." A backup that has never been tested is a liability, not an asset. The exam will reward answers that emphasize recovery testing and validation.

- **Confusing Journaling with Backup**: Journaling is a write-ahead logging mechanism for data integrity. It is *not* a backup strategy. Some candidates select "journaling" when the question asks "what backup method ensures fast recovery from snapshots?" The answer is incremental snapshots, not journaling.

- **Forgetting Replication's Limitations**: Replication is fantastic for availability and speed but does not protect against logical corruption, ransomware, or intentional data deletion if those issues replicate to all copies. The exam may test whether you understand that replication complements but does not replace backups.

---

## Real-World Application

In your homelab, snapshots are critical for rapid VM recovery—if a security tool or service breaks, reverting a snapshot takes seconds. [[Replication]] across multiple nodes ensures critical services (like [[Wazuh]], [[Active Directory]], or [[Pi-hole]]) stay available even if one host fails. [[Journaling]] at the filesystem level (ext4, XFS, BTRFS) protects against data corruption during unexpected power events in the lab. Most importantly, you must periodically run disaster recovery drills—actually breaking a critical service and restoring from snapshot/backup—to confirm the strategy works before a real emergency.
