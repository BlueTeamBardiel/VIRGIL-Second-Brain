# 4.6 - Identity and Access Management (continued)

Identity and Access Management (IAM) encompasses the protocols, standards, and frameworks that organizations use to authenticate users and authorize their access to resources across networks and applications. Section 4.6 focuses on the specific protocols and technologies—particularly [[LDAP]], [[SAML]], and [[OAuth]]—that enable identity verification and access control in complex, multi-organizational environments. Understanding these distinctions is critical for the Security+ exam, as questions frequently test candidates' ability to differentiate between authentication and authorization, and to recognize which protocol solves which problem in real-world scenarios.

---

## Key Concepts

- **[[LDAP]] (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol)**: A protocol for querying and updating directory information over IP networks; based on the X.500 specification and used in systems like [[Active Directory]], Apple OpenDirectory, and Novell eDirectory. The "lightweight" aspect refers to its use of the TCP/IP stack rather than the full OSI protocol stack (which the heavier DAP protocol required).

- **X.500 Directory Information Tree**: A hierarchical structure organizing directory data with container objects (country, organization, organizational units) at higher levels and leaf objects (users, computers, printers, files) at the endpoints.

- **[[SAML]] (Security Assertion Markup Language)**: An open XML-based standard for authentication and authorization that allows users to authenticate through a trusted third-party to gain access to resources. Often called a "one standard does it all" solution, though it was not originally designed for mobile applications.

- **[[OAuth]]**: An authorization framework (not an authentication protocol) that determines what resources a user can access; widely supported by industry leaders (Twitter, Google, Facebook, LinkedIn). Works in conjunction with [[OpenID Connect]] for single sign-on authentication.

- **[[Federation]]**: The ability to provide network access to third parties (partners, suppliers, customers) through establishing trust relationships between organizations, enabling SSO and cross-organizational authentication and authorization.

- **Interoperability**: The ability of different authentication systems to communicate and work together; depends heavily on the specific environment and the protocols deployed (e.g., VPN concentrator to LDAP, or mobile app to OAuth).

- **Authentication vs. Authorization**: Authentication verifies *who* you are; authorization determines *what* you can access. [[OAuth]] handles authorization; [[OpenID Connect]] handles authentication.

- **Trust Relationship**: A formal connection between two organizations in a federated network that establishes the degree of trust and the rules for cross-organizational authentication.

---

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

Imagine a large apartment complex (your organization) with a front desk (directory server) that maintains a master list of all residents and their permissions—who has access to the gym, pool, parking garage, etc. **[[LDAP]]** is like the phone system residents use to call the front desk and ask "What's my access level?" The desk answers with the information stored in its organized database.

Now imagine that the apartment complex wants to allow guests from partner hotels to use certain amenities. The two buildings establish a formal agreement (a **trust relationship**) and create a system where guests can use their hotel key cards ([[Federation]]). When a partner hotel guest arrives, the apartment complex's front desk checks the agreement and grants appropriate access.

**[[SAML]]** works like a letter of introduction: you show up with a signed document from a trusted third party proving your identity, and the front desk accepts it as proof without needing to re-verify you. **[[OAuth]]**, by contrast, is more like a valet ticket: it doesn't prove *who* you are, but rather *what services* you're allowed to use (valet parking, but not the safe deposit box).

**Technical reality**: [[LDAP]] queries hierarchical directory trees to pull user attributes and group memberships. [[SAML]] uses XML assertions signed by an identity provider to communicate identity across organizational boundaries. [[OAuth]] uses tokens and scopes to delegate resource access without sharing passwords. [[Federation]] ties these together by establishing trust relationships and standards between organizations, enabling seamless access without re-authentication.

## Exam Tips

- **Distinguish [[OAuth]] from [[OpenID Connect]]**: The exam frequently tests whether you know that [[OAuth]] is *authorization only* (what you can access) and [[OpenID Connect]] is the authentication layer (who you are). A question asking "which framework handles single sign-on?" is testing whether you know it's the combination, not [[OAuth]] alone.

- **Know [[LDAP]]'s role in Active Directory**: The exam assumes you understand that [[LDAP]] is the protocol used by Windows [[Active Directory]] to query and update user/computer information. If a question mentions "directory queries" or "organizational unit lookups," think [[LDAP]].

- **[[SAML]] is enterprise/web-focused; [[OAuth]] is application/mobile-focused**: Watch for scenario questions that hint at mobile apps (red flag for [[OAuth]]) vs. enterprise federation (red flag for [[SAML]]). The notes explicitly state [[SAML]] "has been [[SAML]]'s largest roadblock."

- **Federation requires a trust relationship**: Any scenario involving third parties (partners, suppliers, customers) accessing resources across organizational boundaries requires establishing and defining the degree of trust. This is a key differentiator from internal [[LDAP]]-based authentication.

- **Interoperability is environment-dependent**: The exam may ask what determines how systems communicate—the answer is that it depends on what's already deployed. There's no one-size-fits-all answer; it's about the specific IAM strategy in place.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing [[OAuth]] with authentication**: Candidates often think [[OAuth]] proves identity. It doesn't—it only delegates authorization. Many answers will tempt you with "[[OAuth]] verifies who the user is," which is incorrect. [[OpenID Connect]] adds the authentication layer on top.

- **Forgetting [[LDAP]] is a *protocol*, not a directory**: Some candidates treat [[LDAP]] as if it's a specific product (like [[Active Directory]]). [[LDAP]] is the communication protocol; [[Active Directory]] is a directory service that *uses* [[LDAP]]. Other services (eDirectory, OpenDirectory) also use [[LDAP]].

- **Underestimating the importance of trust relationships in federation**: Candidates sometimes think federation is just "letting external users log in." The exam emphasizes that trust relationships must be *established* and the *degree* of trust must be *defined*. This is not a technical configuration detail—it's a policy and governance concern.

## Real-World Application

In Morpheus's COCYTUS homelab environment, [[LDAP]] is critical for managing [[Active Directory]] across the fleet—when a new VM or container needs user authentication, it queries the directory tree to pull group memberships and permissions. Implementing [[Federation]] would allow Morpheus to grant access to partner organizations (e.g., contractors running security tests) without creating separate accounts; establishing trust relationships and using [[SAML]] or [[OAuth]] would enable those third parties to authenticate with their own credentials. [[Wazuh]] security monitoring benefits from understanding these IAM flows—detecting anomalous authentication patterns, unauthorized federation access, or token abuse requires knowledge of how these protocols operate under normal conditions.
