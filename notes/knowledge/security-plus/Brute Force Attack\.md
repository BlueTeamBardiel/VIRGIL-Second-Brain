# Brute Force Attack

## What it is
Like a locksmith trying every key on a massive ring until one turns the tumbler, a brute force attack systematically tries every possible combination of characters until the correct password or key is found. It requires no cleverness — only computation and time.

## Why it matters
In the 2012 LinkedIn breach, attackers dumped 6.5 million SHA-1 hashed passwords; because SHA-1 is fast to compute, cracking tools like Hashcat ran brute force and dictionary attacks that cracked the majority of hashes within days. This breach accelerated the industry shift toward slow, salted hashing algorithms like bcrypt and Argon2, which are specifically designed to make brute force computationally expensive.

## Key facts
- **Online vs. offline attacks**: Online brute force hits a live service (detectable, rate-limited); offline attacks run against a stolen hash file at full GPU speed — far faster and harder to stop.
- **Account lockout** after 3–5 failed attempts is the primary defensive control against online brute force; it is explicitly tested on Security+.
- **NIST SP 800-63B** recommends checking new passwords against lists of known-breached passwords rather than enforcing complexity rules alone.
- **GPU acceleration** allows modern rigs to test billions of MD5 hashes per second, making short passwords under ~10 characters practically indefensible without proper hashing.
- A **reverse brute force** (password spraying) flips the attack — one common password (e.g., `Winter2024!`) is tried against many usernames, evading per-account lockout policies.
