# 1.2 - Non-repudiation

Non-repudiation is a cornerstone security principle that ensures a party cannot deny having performed an action—particularly sending a message or signing a document. On the Security+ exam, non-repudiation is tested as a key pillar alongside [[CIA Triad|integrity and authenticity]], and it specifically relies on [[Digital Signatures|digital signature]] technology using [[PKI|public key infrastructure]]. Understanding how non-repudiation works through hashing and encryption is essential for passing the SY0-701 exam and for implementing secure logging, audit trails, and compliance frameworks in production environments.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Non-repudiation Definition**: The inability to deny or disavow an action; proof that a specific party created, sent, or signed a message.
  
- **Three Pillars of Non-repudiation**:
  - **Proof of Integrity**: Verify the message has not been altered (using [[Hashing|hashes]]).
  - **Proof of Origin**: Confirm the source of the message and that it came from the claimed sender.
  - **Authenticity Assurance**: Ensure the signature is genuine and cannot be forged.

- **[[Digital Signatures]]**: The cryptographic mechanism that provides non-repudiation.
  - Created by encrypting a [[Hashing|hash]] of the plaintext with the sender's **private key**.
  - Verified by decrypting with the sender's **public key** (proving they own the private key).
  - The signature is sent alongside the plaintext (plaintext is **not** encrypted by default).

- **[[Hashing]] vs. [[Encryption]]**:
  - **Hashing**: One-way function that produces a fixed-length fingerprint (message digest); deterministic (same input = same output).
  - **Encryption**: Two-way process that can be decrypted; used for confidentiality.
  - Non-repudiation uses **both**: hash for integrity, encryption for signature creation.

- **Hash Properties**:
  - Avalanche effect: Changing even one character in a file completely changes the hash.
  - Acts as a fingerprint—unique to the exact content at that moment.
  - Does **not** inherently tie data to an individual (only proves data integrity).

- **[[PKI]] Role**: 
  - Public key infrastructure provides the key pair management needed for non-repudiation.
  - Sender uses private key (secret, only they possess); receiver uses sender's public key (available to all).
  - The fact that only the private key holder could have created the signature is what prevents repudiation.

- **Digital Signature Process**:
  1. Sender hashes the plaintext using a [[Hashing|hashing algorithm]] (e.g., [[SHA-256]]).
  2. Sender encrypts the hash with their **private key** → creates the digital signature.
  3. Sender transmits plaintext + digital signature to receiver.
  4. Receiver decrypts the signature using sender's **public key** → obtains the original hash.
  5. Receiver hashes the received plaintext independently.
  6. Receiver compares the two hashes; if they match, signature is valid and message is authentic.

- **Why the Plaintext Isn't Encrypted**:
  - Non-repudiation does **not** require confidentiality.
  - The plaintext travels in the clear; only the hash is encrypted.
  - This proves authenticity and integrity but not privacy.

- **Common [[Hashing|Hashing]] Algorithms Used**:
  - [[SHA-256]], [[SHA-384]], [[SHA-512]] (SHA-2 family, current standard).
  - [[MD5]] (deprecated, vulnerable to collisions).
  - Algorithm choice affects hash length and collision resistance.

## How It Works (Feynman Analogy)

**Imagine you're hiring Bob, and you need to send him a job offer letter.**

In the physical world, you sign the contract with a pen. Your signature is unique—only you can reproduce it reliably, and anyone can visually verify it's your signature. If you later claim "I didn't sign that," the evidence of your signature proves otherwise. That's **non-repudiation**.

In the digital world, you can't use a pen. Instead:
1. You create a unique "fingerprint" (hash) of the document using a special algorithm—like running the entire job offer through a blender and getting a single number. If even one word changes, the fingerprint changes completely.
2. You then "sign" that fingerprint by encrypting it with your **private key**—a secret code only you have. This encrypted fingerprint is your **digital signature**.
3. You send Bob both the original job offer (not encrypted, he needs to read it) and your digital signature.
4. Bob receives both. He then uses your **public key** (which everyone knows) to decrypt your signature and recover the fingerprint you created.
5. Bob independently creates a fingerprint of the job offer he received.
6. If Bob's fingerprint matches the one you encrypted, he knows: (a) the message is exactly as you wrote it (integrity), (b) it came from you and nobody else (authenticity and origin), and (c) you cannot claim you didn't send it (non-repudiation).

**Technical reality**: The private key is mathematically linked to the public key in such a way that only someone with the private key could have created that signature. This creates mathematical proof that you performed the action.

## Exam Tips

- **Non-repudiation ≠ Confidentiality**: A common trap. Non-repudiation uses [[Digital Signatures]] which prove who sent the message and that it wasn't altered—but the plaintext is **not encrypted**. Don't confuse it with [[Encryption|encryption]] for privacy.

- **Distinguish from [[Authentication|Authentication]]**: 
  - [[Authentication|Authentication]] = "Are you who you claim to be?" (identity verification at login).
  - **Non-repudiation** = "You cannot deny you sent this message" (proof of action/accountability after the fact).
  - The exam may ask which one proves identity vs. which one prevents denial of an action. Non-repudiation is the answer for preventing denial.

- **Private Key = Signature Creation; Public Key = Signature Verification**:
  - If a question asks "What proves that only Alice could have signed this?" → Answer: **She encrypted it with her private key, which only she possesses.**
  - If a question asks "What key does Bob use to verify Alice's signature?" → Answer: **Alice's public key.**

- **Hash Integrity Check**:
  - On the exam, if you see "someone changes one bit of the message, what happens?" → **The hash changes, signature verification fails, non-repudiation is broken.**
  - This tests understanding of [[Hashing|hash]] avalanche effect and its role in integrity.

- **Signature Placement & Transmission**:
  - Exam may show a diagram asking where the signature goes. Answer: **It travels with the plaintext, not inside it.**
  - The signature is a separate artifact sent alongside the message.

- **When Non-repudiation Applies**:
  - Email signatures, contract signing, financial transactions, audit logs, and code signing.
  - Exam questions about "proof of who sent a message" often point to non-repudiation.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing Hashing with Encryption**: Candidates often think a [[Hashing|hash]] is encrypted data or reversible. Reality: hashing is one-way; you cannot reverse a hash to recover the plaintext. Encryption is two-way.

- **Thinking Non-repudiation Requires Message Encryption**: Many students assume the entire message must be encrypted for non-repudiation. Wrong. Only the hash is encrypted (via the private key). The plaintext can be in the clear. This is a frequent exam trick.

- **Misattributing Key Roles**: Confusing which key encrypts vs. decrypts the signature.
  - **Private key encrypts** (only the owner has it) → creates the signature.
  - **Public key decrypts** (everyone has it) → verifies the signature.
  - If you reverse these in an answer, you've lost non-repudiation.

## Real-World Application

In Morpheus's [[Active Directory]]-based homelab and [[COCYTUS]] fleet, non-repudiation is critical for audit trails and compliance. For example, when using [[Wazuh]] as a [[SIEM]], digitally signed logs (via [[PKI|certificate-based]] authentication) ensure that log entries cannot be repudiated by a user or system—proving definitively who triggered an alert or accessed a resource. Similarly, in a production infrastructure, digitally signed configurations or deployment packages prevent operators from later denying they deployed a particular change, which is essential for [[Incident Response|incident response]] and [[Forensics|forensic]] investigations.
