# SHA-384

## What it is
Like taking SHA-512 and deliberately throwing away a third of its output — SHA-384 is SHA-512 run with different initialization values, then truncated to 384 bits. It belongs to the SHA-2 family of cryptographic hash functions, producing a fixed 384-bit (48-byte) digest from any input of arbitrary length.

## Why it matters
TLS 1.2 and 1.3 cipher suites frequently specify SHA-384 in their HMAC or PRF constructions — for example, `TLS_ECDHE_RSA_WITH_AES_256_GCM_SHA384`. When a server is misconfigured to accept SHA-1 in certificate signatures, attackers can exploit collision weaknesses (as demonstrated by the SHAttered attack in 2017); SHA-384 closes that door by providing far greater collision resistance, making it the preferred hash in high-security government and financial environments requiring FIPS 140-2 compliance.

## Key facts
- **Output size:** 384 bits (48 bytes), offering 192 bits of collision resistance under birthday attack math
- **Parent algorithm:** Derived from SHA-512 — uses the same 64-bit word operations and 80 rounds, just with different IV constants and truncated output
- **NIST approved:** Listed in FIPS PUB 180-4; acceptable for use in U.S. federal systems and satisfies Suite B cryptography requirements
- **Performance quirk:** On 64-bit processors, SHA-384 is often *faster* than SHA-256 because SHA-256 uses 32-bit operations — counterintuitive but exam-relevant
- **No known practical attacks:** Pre-image, second pre-image, and collision attacks remain computationally infeasible as of 2024; length-extension attacks that affect SHA-256 also affect SHA-384, mitigated by using HMAC
