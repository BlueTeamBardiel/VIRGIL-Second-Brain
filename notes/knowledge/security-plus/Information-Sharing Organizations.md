# Information-Sharing Organizations

## What it is
Think of these like neighborhood watch programs scaled to entire industries — members share threat sightings so everyone on the block benefits from what one person spotted. Formally, Information-Sharing Organizations are structured groups that collect, analyze, and distribute threat intelligence among members who share common sector interests or missions. They transform isolated incident data into collective defense.

## Why it matters
During the 2020 SolarWinds supply chain attack, organizations subscribed to the Cyber Threat Alliance and FS-ISAC received early indicators of compromise — malicious domains and hashes — allowing members to block attacker infrastructure before conducting their own forensic investigations. Without these sharing channels, each victim would have discovered the breach independently, giving attackers far more dwell time.

## Key facts
- **ISACs (Information Sharing and Analysis Centers)** are sector-specific bodies (e.g., FS-ISAC for finance, H-ISAC for healthcare) established after Presidential Decision Directive 63 (1998) to protect critical infrastructure.
- **ISAOs (Information Sharing and Analysis Organizations)** are more flexible, non-sector-bound groups created under Executive Order 13691 (2015) that anyone can form around any shared interest.
- **STIX/TAXII** are the standard language (STIX) and transport protocol (TAXII) used to automate machine-readable threat intelligence sharing between these organizations.
- The **Cyber Threat Alliance (CTA)** and **TLP (Traffic Light Protocol)** govern how sensitive shared data is classified and re-distributed (TLP:RED = no further sharing; TLP:GREEN = community-wide).
- Membership often requires reciprocity — you must contribute intelligence, not just consume it, ensuring the ecosystem stays healthy.
